## Overview

This skill is relevant for a founder choosing a cofounder, making one of their first ten
hires, deciding on equity or vesting, or facing a hire that isn't working out. Altman is
speaking specifically about the earliest stage of a company — before you have a hiring
process, before you have an HR function, when a single bad hire can kill the company.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I don't have a cofounder yet — is it okay to just pick someone who also wants to start a company?"
- "How much equity should I give my first employee?"
- "My cofounder wants to work remotely — does that matter?"
- "I've got two candidates for the same role — one has more experience, one seems sharper. Who do I pick?"
- "An early hire isn't working out. How long do I wait before firing them?"
- "How do I even find good people to hire when I have no network?"
- "My cofounder and I haven't talked about the equity split yet — when should we?"

This is not the right skill for: hiring and culture at a company past its first ~10-20
employees (that's a different set of problems — closer to Lecture 10/11 territory, Chesky/
Lin and Collison/Silbermann, not yet built in this repo), management technique once you have
a team (Horowitz's "How to Manage," not yet built), or the execution/focus side of running a
company day to day — see `operations/execution-altman.md`, built from the second half of
this same lecture.

## Assumptions

1. **The company is very early** — pre-cofounder, or in the first ~10 hires. Altman's advice
   (fire fast, 25% of your time on hiring, work on a trial project before hiring) is
   calibrated for a stage where one bad decision can end the company. It gets less literally
   applicable as the company scales — Altman says this himself ("later you need to learn how
   to hire fast to scale up the company," which is a different skill than what's covered here).
2. **The person is deciding about a specific cofounder or hire**, not asking about hiring
   process in the abstract. If they haven't described a specific person or situation, ask.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Altman states blunt, specific
   rules ("don't do remote cofounders," "fire fast," "give 10% to your first ten employees")
   backed by YC data. Reflect that register — ask when you need more context, state a view
   plainly once you have it.

## Insights

> "You would never hire someone like this, and yet people are willing to choose their
> business partners this way. It's really, really bad."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: On "cofounder dating" — picking a cofounder the way strangers pair up because they
both want to start something, without a shared history. Altman's point: founders apply far
less scrutiny to the highest-stakes hire (a cofounder) than to a junior employee.

> "We had one YC batch where nine of about 75 companies added on a random cofounder between
> when we interviewed the companies and when they started, and all nine of those teams fell
> apart in the next year."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: The concrete data point behind the warning — a 100% failure rate for last-minute,
low-history cofounder additions in that batch.

> "For the top 20 most valuable YC companies, all of them have at least two founders... we
> probably fund at a rate of something like one out of ten solo teams."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: Solo founding is possible but structurally disadvantaged in Altman's data — not a
moral judgment, a pattern-matching observation about what YC has actually funded and seen
succeed.

> "There's a sign in the conference room he uses for interviews... it says that mediocre
> engineers do not build great companies... every person in a startup sets the tone."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014, relaying a friend's practice

Context: Why a mediocre hire is catastrophic at 5-10 people but survivable at a big company —
there's no scale to absorb it; every person's output is a visible fraction of the whole.

> "Airbnb spent five months interviewing their first employee... before they hired a single
> person they wrote down a list of the cultural values that they wanted any Airbnb employee
> to have. One of those was that you had to bleed Airbnb."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: Illustrating "hire slowly, with an extremely high bar" — including Brian Chesky's
famously intense screening question about whether a candidate would take the job even with a
terminal diagnosis (later relaxed from one year to ten, but Altman says he still asks it).

> "There are three things that I look for when I hire people: are they smart, do they get
> things done, and do I want to spend a lot of time around them? If I get a yes for all
> three of these, I almost never regretted a hire."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: Altman's own three-question filter, offered as something you can assess in an
interview but assess far better by actually working with someone first.

> "It's way better to work with someone on a quick project for a day or two before hiring
> them... most first-time founders are very bad interviewers, but very good at evaluating
> someone after they've worked together."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: Direct alternative to interview-based hiring — a trial project surfaces the smart/
gets-things-done/enjoyable-to-work-with answers far more reliably than a conversation does.

> "For whatever reason, founders are usually very stingy with equity to employees and very
> generous with equity to investors. I think this is totally backwards... I think as a rough
> guideline you should aim to give 10% of the company to the first ten employees."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: Altman's reasoning: employees add value over years; investors write a check once and
often contribute less than promised afterward. The YC companies that were most generous with
early equity were, in his portfolio, also the most successful.

> "Every first-time founder waits too long [to fire]. The right answer is to fire fast when
> it's not working out. It's better for the company. It's also better for the employee."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: Paired with a clarifying answer from the Q&A — this isn't about firing someone over
one or two mistakes. It's for the case where someone is consistently getting decisions wrong
over weeks, which by that point is "painfully obvious," not a judgment call requiring debate.

## Principles

1. **A cofounder gets less scrutiny than a junior hire, and that's backwards.** Apply at
   least as much rigor to choosing a cofounder as you would to any hire — shared history
   (ideally years) beats a fast match with a stranger.
2. **Solo founding is possible but structurally disadvantaged.** Two or three cofounders is
   the pattern behind most successful YC companies; more than three adds coordination cost
   without clear benefit.
3. **The cost of a bad early hire is nonlinear.** A mediocre hire in a big company causes
   friction. A mediocre hire in the first five can kill a startup outright — there's no scale
   to absorb it.
4. **Trial work beats interviewing.** A day or two of real collaboration reveals more than an
   interview, and most first-time founders are bad interviewers anyway.
5. **Reference checks need to be adversarial, not polite.** "How was so-and-so" gets nothing.
   "Is this person in the top 5% of people you've worked with, and why aren't you trying to
   hire them back" gets something.
6. **Experience matters for scaling a large team; aptitude matters more for the earliest
   hires.** Altman says most of his best-ever hires had never done that specific job before.
7. **Employee equity is where founders are backwards.** Employees compound value over years;
   investors write a check once. Stinginess with early-employee equity is a common, correctable
   mistake — 10% to the first ten hires is Altman's rough guideline.
8. **Cofounder vesting isn't optional.** A near-even split with no vesting means a founder who
   leaves after two months can walk away with a permanent, unearned chunk of the cap table —
   which makes the company nearly unfundable afterward.
9. **Fire fast, not fast-and-loose.** The signal to fire isn't one or two mistakes — it's a
   sustained pattern over weeks where you'd have made the opposite call every time. Once that
   pattern is real, waiting only makes it worse for both sides.
10. **Remote cofounders are, in Altman's own data from this era, close to a non-starter.**
    Communication and speed are what an early startup runs on, and video calls don't replace
    being in the same room. (See "Where This Can Mislead You" — this is time-bound advice
    from 2014, not a law.)

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Fire fast" can be misapplied as "fire on your first bad feeling."** Altman's own Q&A
  clarification matters here: the signal isn't one or two mistakes anyone would make. It's a
  sustained pattern where someone is consistently getting the call wrong over weeks — by which
  point, in his words, it's "painfully obvious," not ambiguous. If it still feels ambiguous,
  it's probably not yet the "fire fast" situation.
- **The remote-cofounder rule is 2014-specific advice, not a timeless law.** This lecture
  predates the shift to distributed teams becoming normal and well-tooled across the industry.
  Treat "don't do remote cofounders" as a real, data-backed warning about communication
  overhead — not as proof that a specific remote cofounder relationship can't work with
  enough deliberate structure.
- **The equity numbers (10% to the first ten hires, standard 4-year/1-year-cliff vesting) are
  real YC norms, not universal constants.** They're a reasonable default, not a rule that
  overrides the specifics of your cap table, your investors' terms, or your geography.
- **The James Bond / "relentlessly resourceful" framing is memorable, not diagnostic.** It's
  a vibe check, not a substitute for the harder, more specific three-question filter (smart,
  gets things done, enjoyable to work with) or a real trial project. Don't let a founder
  stop at "they seem like a James Bond type" as the actual evaluation.
- **The burnout answer Altman gives in the Q&A is worth carrying alongside the intensity
  material in this lecture:** "founder depression is this serious thing, and you need to have
  a support network." The advice to work at an all-consuming intensity is not license to
  ignore that founders burn out in ways that are genuinely dangerous, not just uncomfortable.

## Thinking Framework

**Choosing a cofounder**
Ask: "How long have you actually known this person, and in what context — did you work
together, or are you deciding to start a company together based on a conversation?"

If the history is short or purely social: say so plainly. "That's close to the 'cofounder
dating' pattern Altman found had a 100% failure rate in one YC batch. What would it take to
work on something small together for a few weeks before committing?"

If the history is real (worked together, ideally years): move to the equity/vesting
conversation — that's the next place founders under-prepare.

---

**Evaluating an early hire (or a candidate you're deciding between)**
Ask about all three of Altman's filters directly, one at a time: "Are they smart? Do they get
things done? Do you actually want to spend a lot of time around them?" If any answer is
lukewarm, say so — this isn't a 2-of-3 framework, and Altman is explicit that all three
answering yes is what predicts a hire he never regretted.

Then ask: "Have you worked with them on anything real yet, even for a day?" If not, that's
the concrete next step to recommend before a hiring decision — not another interview round.

---

**Equity and vesting**
If a founder asks how much equity to give an early hire: state Altman's rough guideline
directly (roughly 10% split across the first ten hires, vesting over four years with a
one-year cliff) and ask what's making them hesitate to be that generous — usually it's a
scarcity mindset about the cap table that Altman argues is backwards.

If two cofounders haven't set their split: don't let this sit. "This doesn't get easier with
time — what's stopping you from having this conversation this week?"

---

**A hire that isn't working out**
Ask: "Is this a pattern over weeks, or one or two mistakes anyone would make?" If it's a
pattern — say so directly: "That's the fire-fast signal. Waiting only makes it worse for both
of you." If it's isolated mistakes: "That's normal — the fire-fast rule isn't for this."

---

**Remote cofounder situation**
State the data point directly, then ask what's different about their case: "Altman is
blunt that he's skeptical — communication and speed are what an early startup runs on, and in
his data almost none of the most successful software startups had cofounders in different
locations. What's your plan for closing that gap, and is it structural or just 'we'll try
harder to communicate'?"

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what decision they're facing, ask: "What's the specific
cofounder or hiring decision in front of you right now?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly — don't run through every
framework. If they've already given you enough to know which thread applies (cofounder
choice, evaluating a hire, equity, firing, remote), skip straight to it.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context**, the way Altman does — not an
infinite chain of clarifying questions. If a founder describes a hire who's failed the same
type of decision for three weeks straight, say "that's the fire-fast case" — don't ask a
fourth question to avoid saying it.

## What Not To Do

- **Don't validate "cofounder dating."** If someone describes finding a cofounder through a
  matching event or a few conversations with a stranger, don't treat that as a neutral
  starting point — name the risk directly, using the 9-of-75 data point if it's useful.
- **Don't let "we'll figure out equity later" pass without pushback.** This is one of the
  most common, most costly things founders defer. Ask when they're doing it this week.
- **Don't present the three-question hiring filter as a formality to nod along with.** If a
  founder says yes to two of three, that's not a pass — Altman's data is about all three.
- **Don't treat one or two mistakes as grounds for the fire-fast rule.** That's not the
  situation it's built for — check for a pattern first.
- **Don't cite the "give 10% to the first ten hires" number as gospel.** It's a real,
  useful default, not a rule that overrides someone's actual cap table math.
- **Don't ignore the burnout/support-network point when discussing hiring intensity.** If a
  founder is describing a punishing hiring push, it's fair to note that this is meant to be
  temporary and taxing, not a permanent operating mode without support.
