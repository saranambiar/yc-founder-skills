## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder is defining core values for the first time, deciding
whether to hire (or fire) on culture fit despite strong functional skills, facing a decision
where the pragmatic choice and the values-aligned choice diverge, or wondering whether culture
work is worth the time cost given it doesn't show up in short-term metrics. Lin (former Zappos
COO) opens with the framework; Chesky (Airbnb) grounds it in real decisions, including one
that cost Airbnb real optionality (declining to acquire a well-funded, aggressive clone).

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I don't know how to even start writing down our core values — where do I begin?"
- "I have a great candidate, technically excellent, but something feels off about fit"
- "A pragmatic business move conflicts with what I think we stand for — which wins?"
- "Is spending real time on culture worth it when we're pre-revenue and every hour counts?"
- "How do I get culture to actually show up in day-to-day decisions, not just a values poster?"
- "How is culture different from brand, and does it matter for a company this small?"

This is not the right skill for choosing a cofounder or evaluating an individual early hire's
skills (`hiring/team-and-cofounders-altman.md`) or for the execution/focus side of running the
company day to day (`operations/execution-altman.md`) — though "champion the mission" connects
directly to Altman's point that mission-driven ideas sustain founders through a decade.

## Assumptions

1. **The founder is building for the long term**, not a quick flip. Chesky is explicit that
   the single best piece of advice for building something to sell fast is to skip culture
   work entirely — this whole framework assumes that isn't the goal.
2. **There's already a real team, or hiring is about to start.** Values matter most once
   there's more than one person whose decisions represent the company.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Both speakers state specific,
   sometimes counterintuitive claims (diversity of background yes, diversity of values no;
   culture doesn't pay off short-term) from lived experience, including a real, costly decision.

## Insights

> "Every [beliefs, assumptions, core values] and [real actions] of each member of the team in
> pursuit of our company['s] [mission]."
— Alfred Lin, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: Lin's working definition of company culture, built by filling in blanks in a
dictionary-style sentence — deliberately distinguishing it from a values poster: it's the
combination of what people actually believe and what they actually do, aimed at a mission.

> "The best companies to work for [by Fortune's index] returned 11.8% annually from 1997-2003
> — almost twice the S&P 500 and Russell 3000. There's real power in companies that treat
> their employees well, where there's a lot of trust, where there's a strong culture."
— Alfred Lin, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: Offered as the "this isn't just mushy stuff" counterpoint — a concrete financial
argument for treating culture as a real input to results, not a nice-to-have.

> "Nobody is going to say 'I want to be lied to every day' [about the value of honesty].
> Everybody talks about teamwork. But there's a difference in the level of teamwork you see in
> an intramural sports team versus [an elite] baseball team."
— Alfred Lin, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: Lin's warning against generic values — a value like "honesty" or "teamwork" is
trivially agreeable and therefore does no work. Zappos spent a year going from 37 candidate
values down to 10, specifically to get past generic-sounding words to something with real depth.

> "Airbnb was never meant to be the big idea. It was meant to be the thing to pay the rent so
> we could think of the big idea. And along the way, by solving our own problem, it became the
> big idea."
— Brian Chesky, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: The origin story Chesky says he's told "10,000 times" — included not for the founding
myth itself but to set up his real point: the company almost wasn't founded on ambition at all.

> "I think of founders as parents and the company as a child. If the parents are
> dysfunctional, not working together, the child is gonna be pretty fucked up. You don't want
> that — you want your culture to be awesome."
— Brian Chesky, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: Chesky's frame for why founder relationships and habits (working together, shared
accountability) become the company's DNA directly — not metaphorically, but mechanically.

> "I felt it would be a tragedy for us to outlive our company and just watch it rise and fall.
> We wanted a company that would endure. Companies that were around a long time had a clear
> mission, a clear sense of values, and a shared way of doing something unique to them."
— Brian Chesky, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: The reasoning behind writing Airbnb's core values down before they'd hired a single
employee — Chesky says he later learned from Zappos founder Tony Hsieh, who wished he hadn't
waited until 100 employees to do the same thing.

> "I think we're one of the only companies that wrote our core values down before we hired
> anyone... I kind of felt like your first engineer was bringing DNA to your company. It
> wasn't about getting someone to ship the next three features — it was: do I want to work
> with a thousand more people like this?"
— Brian Chesky, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: Explains why Airbnb spent four to five months and interviewed hundreds of people for
a single early hire — the decision wasn't sized to the role, it was sized to what that hire
would replicate at scale.

> "You want diversity of beliefs, diversity of backgrounds, age — but you don't want
> diversity of values. That's the one thing that shouldn't be diverse."
— Brian Chesky, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: A sharp distinction Chesky draws explicitly — variety in who people are and how they
think is good; variety in what the company fundamentally stands for is not.

> "There are three things they never tell you about culture. First, no one ever talks about
> it — there are tons of articles about product and growth, almost none about culture. Second,
> it's hard to measure, and hard-to-measure things get discounted. Third, and biggest: it
> doesn't pay off in the short term. If you wanted to build a company and sell it as fast as
> possible, the number one piece of advice I'd give you is: fuck up the culture. Hire quickly,
> don't be deliberate. Culture is an investment that can slow near-term progress."
— Brian Chesky, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: Chesky's most direct statement on the tradeoff culture work actually carries — not
that there's no cost, but that the cost is real and short-term, while the payoff is long-term
and the choice to skip it is a real, legitimate-sounding option for a different kind of company.

> "In mid-2011, [the Samwer brothers] cloned us, raised $90 million, and hired 400 people in
> 30 days... The pragmatic decision was probably to buy them — you can't risk losing
> international. We ended up not buying them, because I didn't like the culture. I felt like
> we were missionaries and they were mercenaries, and I believe missionaries outlast and
> outendure mercenaries."
— Brian Chesky, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: The concrete, expensive example of values overriding the pragmatic move — Chesky
names this as controversial at the time (many advisors told him to buy), and notes Europe is
now more than 50% of Airbnb's revenue regardless.

> "If you can hire anybody in the world, would you hire the person sitting across from you?
> ...We have separate people called core-values interviewers who are never in the candidate's
> function — an engineer is never interviewed on values by another engineer — so they're not
> biased by 'I know how good they are technically.'"
— Brian Chesky, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: Two specific hiring mechanics: a raise-the-bar test for functional interviewers, and
a structurally separated values interview to prevent technical impressiveness from masking a
values mismatch.

> "It's literally better to have 100 people that love you than a million people that just
> kind of like you... if all you need is 100 people to love you, then what you need to do is
> things that don't scale."
— Brian Chesky, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014, citing advice from Paul Graham

Context: The reasoning behind Airbnb's early door-to-door, live-with-the-host approach
(including Chesky and Gebbia personally photographing early listings) — design the perfect
experience for one person before trying to solve how to do it at scale.

> "Culture is the principles and beliefs inside the company that you want people aligned with
> long term. Brand is the promise outside the company that everyone identifies with. They're
> two sides of the same coin — and your employees are often your brand's real evangelists."
— Brian Chesky, CS183B Lecture 10, 2014

Context: Chesky's answer to why Silicon Valley underinvests in both culture and brand relative
to product and growth — and why a company with a genuinely strong culture tends to produce a
strong brand as a byproduct, not through separate marketing effort.

## Principles

1. **Culture is beliefs plus real actions in pursuit of the mission — not a values poster.**
   If it doesn't show up in what people actually do, it isn't the culture, regardless of what's
   written down.
2. **Generic values ("honesty," "teamwork") do no work because nobody disagrees with them.**
   Real core values require enough specificity that a reasonable person could actually fail to
   embody them.
3. **Derive values from the founder's own experience** — who you've loved working with, who
   you've hated working with, and what's uniquely, credibly tied to your specific mission —
   not from a generic list of admirable-sounding words.
4. **Culture doesn't pay off short-term, and that's exactly why it gets skipped.** The
   founders who most need to hear "invest in culture" are the ones under the most pressure to
   move fast, which is precisely when it's hardest to justify.
5. **Founders' own working relationship becomes the company's DNA directly**, not just by
   example — dysfunction between founders propagates into the company the way a family's
   dysfunction shows up in a child.
6. **Hire for culture fit as rigorously as for skill — ideally with a structurally separate
   values interview** so functional impressiveness can't mask a mismatch.
7. **Diversity of background is good; diversity of values is not.** These are different axes,
   and conflating them either produces a company with no coherent culture or an excuse to avoid
   real diversity of people.
8. **When a pragmatic decision and a values-aligned decision genuinely diverge, that's the real
   test of whether the culture is real.** Airbnb's decision not to acquire the Samwer clone —
   against pragmatic advice — is the concrete proof point, not a values statement alone.
9. **The first hires carry outsized weight because they're "DNA," not headcount.** Evaluate an
   early hire against "do I want a thousand more people like this," not just the next sprint.
10. **Brand is culture's external face — strong culture tends to produce strong brand as a
    byproduct**, largely through employees acting as evangelists, not through separate
    marketing effort.
11. **"Do things that don't scale" is a direct consequence of the 100-people-who-love-you
    logic**, not a separate growth tactic — you can only personally serve a small number of
    people intensely, and that's exactly the point at the beginning.
12. **Culture requires daily habit, not a crash diet.** Like fitness, it degrades without
    continuous practice and can't be recovered with a single intense push later.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Fuck up the culture" is Chesky's ironic framing of the tradeoff, not literal advice.**
  He's naming what someone optimizing purely for a fast flip would do — not recommending it.
  Don't let a founder under time pressure use this line to justify neglecting values work by
  treating the joke as permission.
- **The missionaries-vs-mercenaries decision to decline the Samwer acquisition was a specific,
  well-resourced, high-context call — not a template that "values should always beat pragmatic
  business logic."** Chesky had the runway and conviction to make this bet; a founder in a
  genuinely existential situation may not have the same room to prioritize values over survival.
- **"Diversity of values" being discouraged can be misused to justify excluding people for
  superficial or discriminatory reasons dressed up as "values fit."** The intended target is
  alignment on what the company fundamentally stands for (mission, ethics, standards) — not an
  excuse to select for personal similarity or background homogeneity, which Chesky explicitly
  separates out as something that should be diverse.
- **The "would you take this job with N years left to live" question is presented with its own
  built-in correction** (Chesky moved from one year to ten specifically because the one-year
  version screened for the wrong thing — people who said yes to that were people who should be
  spending time with family, not people more committed to the company). Using the harsher,
  one-year version uncritically reproduces the exact mistake Chesky describes correcting.
- **Choosing not to build a technical moat (Airbnb's decision to open-source rather than guard
  code, preferring network effects and experience as the moat) was a choice specific to their
  business's actual defensibility.** It isn't general advice to skip building technical
  defensibility — check against `market-strategy/competition-monopoly-thiel.md`'s monopoly
  characteristics for whether a given company's real moat is technology or something else.
- **The parent-child metaphor for founders and company is evocative but can license unhealthy
  founder-identity fusion with the company if taken too literally.** Use it for the specific
  point being made (founder dysfunction propagates directly) — not as a justification for
  treating the company as inseparable from the founder's personal identity indefinitely.

## Thinking Framework

**Writing core values for the first time**
Ask: "Who have you loved working with, and what did they have that you want more of? Who have
you hated working with, and what's the opposite of that?" Push past the first generic answer
("honesty," "hard work") — ask what specifically that means in a way a reasonable person could
actually fail to live up to. Then: "Does this value connect uniquely to your mission, or would
any company say this?" If any company could claim it, it's not there yet.

---

**Candidate is technically strong but something feels off**
Ask: "If you separated the technical evaluation from the values evaluation completely — maybe
even had someone outside the role interview for values only — what would that conversation
surface?" Recommend the structurally separate values interview directly if it doesn't already
exist. Then ask Chesky's raise-the-bar question: "If you could hire anyone in the world for
this role, would it be them?"

---

**Pragmatic move conflicts with stated values**
Don't resolve this abstractly — ask what's actually at stake on each side. "What's the real
cost of the values-aligned choice, and is it survivable?" If yes: name the Samwer precedent
directly as a real example of this exact tradeoff being made deliberately. If the pragmatic
choice is closer to existential for the company: don't apply the precedent uncritically — this
is exactly the caveat where context changes the answer.

---

**Wondering whether culture work is worth the time cost right now**
Ask directly: "Is the goal to build something enduring, or something to sell fast?" If
enduring: name the tradeoff plainly — culture doesn't pay off short-term, and that's precisely
why it's easy to skip, not a sign it doesn't matter. If genuinely optimizing for a fast exit:
say so — Chesky's own answer for that goal is different, and pretending otherwise doesn't help
the founder.

---

**Trying to reinforce culture beyond just employees (e.g., a marketplace's supply side)**
Ask: "Do the people who represent your company externally (hosts, partners, contractors) need
to share your values the same way employees do?" Use Chesky's own late realization about
Airbnb hosts directly — this wasn't obvious to him early on, and required building explicit
programs (Superhost) to correct for it once he saw the problem.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "What's the specific culture question
right now — writing values for the first time, a hiring decision, a values-vs-pragmatism
conflict, or something else?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes a value
that's generically agreeable ("we value integrity"), say so directly: "That's not doing any
work yet — nobody would say the opposite. What's the more specific, uniquely-yours version?"

## What Not To Do

- **Don't accept a generic, universally-agreeable value as finished.** Push for the specific,
  falsifiable version tied uniquely to the mission.
- **Don't treat "fuck up the culture" as literal permission to deprioritize values work under
  time pressure.** It's naming the tradeoff, not endorsing the choice.
- **Don't let "diversity of values shouldn't exist" become cover for excluding people on
  superficial grounds.** Keep the target narrow: alignment on mission and standards, not
  personal or background similarity.
- **Don't apply the Samwer-clone decision as a template that values always beat pragmatism.**
  Check whether the company can actually survive the values-aligned choice first.
- **Don't recommend the harsh "one year left to live" screening question without the
  correction Chesky himself made to it.** Use the softer version, or better, ask what it's
  actually screening for and build a better version of that question.
- **Don't treat "give away your tech, build brand/network effects as your moat" as universal
  advice.** It was specific to Airbnb's actual defensibility — check what a given company's
  moat actually is before recommending this.
