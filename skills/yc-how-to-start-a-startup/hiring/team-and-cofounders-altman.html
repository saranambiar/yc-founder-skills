Choosing Cofounders and Making Your First Hires

Sam Altman  ·  President, Y Combinator

Founders apply less scrutiny to their cofounder than they would to a junior hire — and treat their first ten employees' equity as an afterthought. Altman argues both are exactly backwards.

Core Argument

The One Argument Altman Makes

Every decision about who joins the company in its earliest days is disproportionately high-stakes, and founders systematically under-invest scrutiny exactly where the cost of being wrong is highest. A bad cofounder or a bad first hire doesn't get diluted by scale the way it would at a big company — there is no scale yet. Altman's claim across cofounders, hiring, equity, and firing is the same shape every time: treat the earliest people decisions with more rigor than instinct suggests, because the failure mode is not friction, it's death.

Team

Choosing a Cofounder

Cofounder choice is, in Altman's data, the single highest-leverage early decision — and the one founders are most likely to treat casually.

9 / 9
of the YC companies in one batch that added a random cofounder between interview and start all fell apart within the year. Out of roughly 75 companies in that batch, 9 did this — and every single one failed as a team.

What actually works

Cofounders with real shared history — ideally years, ideally from working together already. The best source: college, or an interesting company like Facebook or Google where you'll meet people worth starting something with.

"Cofounder dating"

Meeting a stranger who also wants to start something and deciding to start together on the spot. Altman: "You would never hire someone like this, and yet people are willing to choose their business partners this way."

Top 20 most valuable YC companies: all have 2+ founders
YC funds roughly 1 in 10 solo-founder teams
2–3 cofounders is the sweet spot — 5 is too many

"We had one YC batch where nine of about 75 companies added on a random cofounder between when we interviewed the companies and when they started, and all nine of those teams fell apart in the next year."

— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2

The "relentlessly resourceful" / James Bond test: YC's public phrase for what to look for is "relentlessly resourceful." Altman's more colorful version, borrowed from Paul Graham: does this person behave like James Bond — unflappable, decisive, ready for anything — more than they're a domain expert? It sounds silly; it's meant to be memorable, not a substitute for real evaluation (see "Where This Can Mislead You").

Hiring

Making Your First Hires

"Try not to" is Altman's actual starting instruction. Every employee adds burn, complexity, and slower decisions — and the cost of getting an early hire wrong is high enough that a single bad one in the first three hires can end the company outright.

Airbnb's first hire

Five months spent interviewing before hiring a single person. Only two hires in their first year. Before hiring anyone, they wrote down the cultural values every Airbnb employee had to have — including "you had to bleed Airbnb."

Chesky's screening question

Brian Chesky used to ask candidates if they'd take the job even with a medical diagnosis giving them one year left to live. He later relaxed it to ten years — but Altman says he still asks some version of it.

The three-question filter Altman actually uses: Are they smart? Do they get things done? Do I want to spend a lot of time around them? A yes on all three — not two of three — is what he says he's almost never regretted.

Trial project > interview

"It's way better to work with someone on a quick project for a day or two before hiring them." Most first-time founders are bad interviewers but good at evaluating someone after actually working together.

Reference checks, done right

Not "how was so-and-so." Ask: is this person in the top 5% of people you've worked with? What specifically did they do? Would you hire them again — and if not, why not?

Experience vs. aptitude

Matters for someone running a large org. Matters much less for most early hires — Altman says most of the best hires he's ever made had never done that specific job before.

Sourcing: personal referrals

The best source by far, for the first 100+ employees at most great tech companies. Go past the awkwardness of asking everyone you know — and look outside the Valley, where competition for engineers is brutal.

Equity

Equity and Vesting

Altman's claim: founders get the equity instinct backwards, being stingy with the people who compound value over years and generous with investors who write one check.

What founders usually do
Stingy with employees.
Generous with investors.
Investors write a check once and often contribute less than promised afterward.
What Altman argues for
Generous with employees.
Fight to reduce investor take.
Employees compound value over years, if the company succeeds.
~10%
of the company, as a rough guideline, split across the first ten employees — vesting over four years, so they have to earn it either way.

Cofounder vesting isn't optional

Standard Silicon Valley term: four years to fully vest, with a one-year cliff (25% at year one, then continuing). Without it, a cofounder who leaves after two months keeps a permanent, unearned slice of the cap table — which makes the company very hard to fund afterward. Set the equity split itself early too: "this is not a discussion that gets easier with time."

Firing & Retention

Firing Fast, Retaining Well

Two sides of the same coin: the discipline to remove someone who isn't working before the damage compounds, and the discipline to actually keep and grow the people who are.

"Every first-time founder waits too long. The right answer is to fire fast when it's not working out. It's better for the company. It's also better for the employee."

— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2

The actual signal

Not one or two mistakes — anyone makes those. The signal is a sustained pattern over weeks where someone is consistently getting the call wrong. Altman: at that point "it'll be painfully aware to everyone." If it still feels ambiguous, it's probably not this case yet.

Also fire for

Office politics and persistent negativity — even from someone otherwise competent. Something that might just be a drag at a big company is "completely toxic" at startup scale.

Retention basics first-time CEOs miss

Let the team get credit for what goes well; take responsibility yourself for what doesn't. Don't micromanage. Give real, clear feedback and actual 1:1s. Dan Pink's three motivators — autonomy, mastery, purpose — are worth designing around.

The turnover trap

One YC founder's hard-earned lesson: "it turns out you shouldn't tell your employees they're fucking up every day unless you'd like them all to leave — because they will."

Q&A

From the Q&A

Four live questions that sharpen the prepared material — including one Altman flags he'll address later, and answers on remote cofounders and the hire-vs-customer tradeoff.

When should cofounders decide the equity split?

Very soon after starting to work together — and it should be near-equal. "This is not a discussion that gets easier with time." If you're not willing to give your cofounder an equal share, that should make you think hard about whether you want them as a cofounder at all.

What if a hire's inexperience becomes crippling as the company scales?

Really smart people who can learn tend to find a new role in the company as it grows, even if their original role outgrows them. Altman says he hasn't seen this be a frequent problem — the risk is overstated relative to the risk of not hiring for aptitude at all in the early days.

Hire a suboptimal employee, or lose a customer to a competitor?

For one of the first five hires: lose the customer. "It's better to lose some customers than kill the company." Altman flags this shifts later — it's a stage-specific answer, not a permanent rule.

What about cofounders in different locations?

"Don't do it." Altman is explicit he's skeptical of remote teams generally, and especially early on when communication speed outweighs everything else. His test: try to name a single one of the 30 most successful software startups of all time with cofounders in different locations.

Apply With Judgment

Where This Can Mislead You

This advice is calibrated for a specific, very early stage. Applied without that context, it produces some real wrong turns.

"Fire fast" isn't "fire on your first bad feeling." The signal is a sustained pattern over weeks, not one or two mistakes anyone would make. If it still feels ambiguous whether someone's failing, it's probably not yet the fire-fast case.
The remote-cofounder rule is 2014-specific, not a timeless law. This predates distributed teams becoming normal and well-tooled industry-wide. Treat it as a real, data-backed warning about communication overhead — not proof a specific remote arrangement can't work with deliberate structure.
The equity numbers are real YC norms, not universal constants. ~10% to the first ten hires and standard 4-year/1-year-cliff vesting are reasonable defaults — not rules that override your actual cap table and investor terms.
The James Bond framing is memorable, not diagnostic. It's a vibe check, not a substitute for the three-question filter or an actual trial project. Don't let "they seem like a James Bond type" stand in for real evaluation.
The intensity this lecture asks for isn't license to ignore burnout. In the same session's Q&A, Altman is direct: "founder depression is this serious thing, and you need to have a support network." Extreme dedication without support is a different thing than extreme dedication with it.
Principles

What Founders Actually Take Away

Six action-oriented principles, grounded in specific claims and data points Altman gave.

  1. 1
    A cofounder gets less scrutiny than a junior hire — that's backwards.

    Apply at least as much rigor to a cofounder decision as to any hire. Real shared history, ideally years, beats a fast match with someone you don't know.

  2. 2
    The cost of a bad early hire is nonlinear.

    A mediocre hire causes friction at a big company. In the first five hires, it can kill a startup outright — there's no scale to absorb it.

  3. 3
    Trial work beats interviewing.

    A day or two of real collaboration reveals more than a conversation — and most first-time founders are bad interviewers anyway.

  4. 4
    Employee equity is where founders are backwards.

    Employees compound value over years; investors write a check once. Stinginess with early-employee equity is a common, correctable mistake.

  5. 5
    Cofounder vesting isn't optional.

    Without it, a cofounder who leaves early can keep a permanent, unearned slice of the cap table — and make the company very hard to fund.

  6. 6
    Fire on a pattern, not a mistake — but fire fast once the pattern is real.

    The signal is weeks of consistently wrong calls, not one bad day. Once it's real, waiting only makes it worse for both sides.

Apply Now

The Honest Test

Run this against your actual team situation right now.

Run this on your team right now

  1. How long have you actually known your cofounder, and in what context — did you work together, or did you decide to start a company based on a few conversations?
  2. For your last hire: were they smart, did they get things done, and did you actually want to spend time around them — all three, not two of three?
  3. Have you and your cofounder set the equity split and vesting terms yet? If not, what's stopping you from doing it this week?
  4. Is there someone on your team right now whose failure is a pattern over weeks, not a single bad day? If so, what are you waiting for?
  5. What are you doing to make your first ten employees feel like the equity they're earning actually reflects what they're building?
