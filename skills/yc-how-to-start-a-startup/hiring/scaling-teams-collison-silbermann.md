## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder is making one of their first ten hires, evaluating
talent in a domain they don't personally understand well, unsure whether a candidate's
enthusiasm for the mission is genuine, or figuring out how culture and transparency should
change as the company grows past the size where everything happens automatically in one room.
Three voices are in this transcript — Patrick and John Collison (Stripe) and Ben Silbermann
(Pinterest) — attributed separately, since they sometimes emphasize different things.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I'm hiring my first engineer/salesperson and I don't know how to evaluate them"
- "I'm not an expert in this role I'm hiring for — how do I judge candidates?"
- "A candidate says they're passionate about our mission, but something feels off"
- "How do I actually check references instead of getting empty praise?"
- "We've grown past the point where culture just happens automatically — what now?"
- "Should my early hires be expected to grow into management, or is that unrealistic?"

This is not the right skill for choosing a cofounder specifically
(`hiring/team-and-cofounders-altman.md`) or for defining core values from scratch
(`hiring/culture-chesky-lin.md`, which this lecture is presented as "Part 2" of).

## Assumptions

1. **The founder is hiring, or about to hire, real employees** — not evaluating a cofounder,
   which carries different stakes and a different evaluation process.
2. **The company is somewhere on the path from a handful of people to a few hundred.** Some of
   this framework (transparency tooling, autonomous team units) is specifically about scaling
   past the size where everything happens automatically.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** All three speakers state
   specific, sometimes counterintuitive claims (don't hire your brilliant friends at Google;
   push people off the cliff rather than easing them in) backed by their own companies' history.

## Insights

> "I think culture, to some degree, is basically the resolution to a bandwidth problem. In
> some idealized world you could be involved in every decision — but you can't, even at five
> or ten people, and by 50 it's completely hopeless. Culture is what the invariants are that
> you want to maintain as you get specifically involved in fewer and fewer decisions over time."
— Patrick Collison, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: A structural, non-mystical definition of why culture matters at all — it's the
mechanism that lets decisions stay aligned with founder intent even as founders become
physically unable to be involved in most of them.

> "You're not just hiring those first ten people — you're actually hiring 100 people. Think of
> each one as bringing along another ten people with them. Figuring out exactly what those 90
> people should look like is obviously going to be quite consequential for your company."
— Patrick Collison, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: The reasoning behind why the first ten hires carry disproportionate weight — their
influence compounds through everyone they'll eventually bring in or shape, not just their own
individual contribution.

> "We looked for people who were genuine and straight — people others trust, with intellectual
> honesty. People who really liked getting things finished — a lot of people are excited about
> tons of things, only a subset are excited about completing them. And people who just cared a
> great deal — it was offensive to them when something was a little bit off. We used to have
> every API error phone all of us, and we'd point out spelling mistakes to each other on every
> outgoing email."
— John Collison, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: Three specific, retrospectively-identified traits shared by Stripe's first ten
hires — offered with an explicit caveat that this is "very limited experience, widely
over-extrapolated," not a universal formula.

> "Think like a value investor — you're looking for human capital significantly devalued by
> the market. You probably shouldn't try to hire your brilliant friends at Facebook or Google —
> they're already discovered. Our early hires were undervalued in some way: our designer was
> 18 and in high school in Sweden; our CTO was still in college."
— Ben Silbermann, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: A direct reframe of where to look for early talent — known, already-validated people
are both harder to convince and, definitionally, not an edge over other companies competing
for the same person.

> "It's really important, even as companies get bigger, that you don't whitewash the risks. At
> PayPal, after interviewing with Thiel and Levchin, they'd tell you Visa and MasterCard wanted
> to kill them and they might be doing something illegal — but if you succeed, you'll redefine
> payments. The right people select in, or they select out, based on that."
— Ben Silbermann, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: Directly counters the instinct to oversell a role during recruiting — being explicit
about the real difficulty filters for people who are drawn to hard problems specifically,
which is a better filter than enthusiasm alone.

> "I think people often have imposter syndrome when it comes to interviewing for roles outside
> their expertise. Before talking to anyone, we'd try to get a sense of what world-class looks
> like in that discipline — talking to people we knew were world-class and asking exactly what
> traits they look for. Precalibrating matters, because learning what good and bad looks like
> during the interview process itself is extremely expensive."
— Ben Silbermann, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: A concrete method for evaluating talent in a domain the founder doesn't know well —
borrow calibration from people who do, before the interview, rather than trying to develop
judgment live during costly interview time.

> "For a majority of our first ten people, we worked with them in some capacity before
> committing to hiring them — usually for a week in advance. It's pretty hard to fake it for a
> week; it tends to become clear pretty quickly."
— Patrick Collison, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: Stripe's direct alternative to interview-based evaluation for the highest-stakes early
hires — a real work trial reveals what interviews can't, though Collison notes it becomes
impractical (too costly for the candidate) at larger scale.

> "I don't really know what '10x' means. A more intuitive version: is this person probably the
> best out of all of their friends at what they do? It's a little sensitive to how well they
> chose friends, but I find it a more helpful way to think about it."
— Patrick Collison, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: A reframing of the vague "10x engineer" concept into something more checkable —
relative standing among a real peer group the candidate already has, rather than an abstract
multiplier.

> "Referencing is really important. We ask things like: if I talked to Jonathan in a couple
> weeks and asked what you'd be most proud of, or what you're working to improve, what would he
> say? That tests self-awareness and creates social accountability. Then to make a soft
> question quantitative: is this person the top 1% of people you've worked with? Top 5%? Top
> 10%? That forces a scarcity a generic 'is this person good' question never gets."
— John Collison, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: Two specific techniques for getting real signal from references, who default to being
nice: testing self-awareness by predicting a third party's answer, and forcing relative
ranking instead of accepting unranked praise.

> "We're much more of the push-people-off-the-cliff school. Get engineers committing code on
> day one; get business hires into real meetings on what they're meant to work on, day one.
> Progress is only measured by how much real work they're doing — that's also the fastest way
> to find the actual problems."
— Patrick Collison, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: A direct rejection of gentle, gradual onboarding in favor of immediate real
responsibility — paired with an equally direct expectation of fast, frequent feedback,
including on cultural adaptation specifically.

> "One of the really lucky, great decisions we made was that our 14th or 15th hire was a
> professional recruiter who'd worked at startups and at big companies like Apple. She knew
> where the pipeline breaks down and taught everyone not just how to screen for talent, but how
> to scalably identify who's going to be culturally good for the company."
— Ben Silbermann, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: A specific, concrete scaling unlock — bringing in dedicated recruiting expertise
early enough to systematize what had been ad hoc, rather than waiting until hiring volume
demanded it reactively.

> "When someone says 'I'm really passionate about what you're doing' and then lists seven
> companies they're also interviewing at that have nothing to do with each other except being
> at the same stage — that's a sign they're not being authentic about what they care about.
> Those people often won't stick it out when things get hard, because they signed up for an
> experience, not for achieving a goal."
— Ben Silbermann, CS183B Lecture 11, 2014

Context: A concrete red flag for distinguishing genuine mission-driven candidates from people
drawn to "the startup experience" generically — the tell is incoherent breadth across
unrelated companies rather than a specific, consistent thread of interest.

## Principles

1. **Culture is the mechanism that keeps decisions aligned with founder intent as founders
   become unable to be involved in most decisions.** It matters structurally, not just
   sentimentally, and its importance grows roughly with headcount.
2. **Each of the first ten hires functions like ten hires, not one** — their influence
   compounds through everyone they'll eventually shape or bring in. Treat these decisions with
   proportionate weight.
3. **Look for undervalued talent, not already-validated talent.** People who are known
   quantities are both harder to recruit and, by definition, not a real edge over competitors
   for the same hire.
4. **Precalibrate your judgment before interviewing outside your own expertise** by asking
   people you know are world-class in that discipline what they actually look for — don't try
   to develop taste live during expensive interview time.
5. **A real work trial reveals what an interview can't, for the highest-stakes early hires
   specifically.** It's hard to fake genuine competence and fit over a real week of work.
6. **"Best of their friends at what they do" is a more checkable proxy for exceptional talent
   than an abstract "10x" claim.**
7. **Reference checks need techniques that force real signal** — predicting a third party's
   answer to test self-awareness, and forced relative ranking (top 1%/5%/10%) instead of
   unranked praise, which references default to giving out of social pressure to be nice.
8. **Be transparent about real difficulty and risk when recruiting, not just the upside.**
   Honesty about hard problems filters for people who want hard problems, which is a better
   selection mechanism than enthusiasm generated by an oversold pitch.
9. **Onboard by giving real responsibility immediately, not gradual easing-in — paired with
   fast, frequent feedback, including explicit feedback on cultural adaptation.**
10. **A dedicated recruiting hire, brought in earlier than headcount alone would suggest, can
    systematize hiring judgment before it's forced to scale reactively.**
11. **Watch for candidates interviewing across many unrelated companies at the same stage as a
    red flag** — it signals attraction to "the startup experience" generically rather than
    genuine conviction about the specific mission, and predicts they won't stick around when
    things get hard.
12. **Transparency (radical internal information-sharing) has to evolve its tooling and its
    cultural norms as headcount grows**, or it either breaks technically (email volume) or
    breaks socially (stage fright and drive-by criticism suppressing honest sharing).
13. **The right time horizon for hiring decisions expands with company age** — early hires
    must be immediately productive; only later does it become reasonable (and eventually
    necessary) to make longer-term talent investments.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Push people off the cliff" onboarding assumes the company already has enough structure
  (a real problem to hand someone, clear ownership) for immediate responsibility to be
  meaningful rather than chaotic.** Applied to a genuinely disorganized early team, "immediate
  real work" can just mean immediate confusion — the technique works because Stripe paired it
  with fast, deliberate feedback, not because throwing someone in alone is sufficient.
- **The "value investor" framing for undervalued talent can be misapplied as "avoid anyone
  impressive or already accomplished."** The actual claim is narrower — already-validated,
  widely-recognized people are harder to convince and represent less of an edge, not that
  accomplished people are bad hires when they are genuinely convinceable.
- **The three traits identified for Stripe's first ten hires (genuine/straight, finishers, care
  a great deal) are explicitly flagged by the speakers themselves as "very limited experience,
  widely over-extrapolated."** Treat them as one real data point, not a universal formula for
  identifying great early hires at any company.
- **"Don't hire your brilliant friends at Google" is about where to focus limited early
  recruiting effort, not a rule against hiring accomplished people who are genuinely willing to
  join.** If a known-great person wants in for real reasons, that's not a red flag — the
  point is not to expect that path to be your primary recruiting strategy.
- **The transparency model (BCC everyone on every email, full internal openness) is Stripe's
  specific choice, made possible by a genuine, company-wide alignment on mission.** It required
  real, ongoing tooling and cultural investment to keep working past ~50 people, and the
  speakers themselves express uncertainty about whether it holds at much larger scale (5,000+).
  Don't treat it as a plug-and-play practice independent of that ongoing investment.
- **The "interviewing at seven unrelated companies" red flag is a real signal, but it's not
  proof of insincerity on its own.** Some genuinely early-career candidates reasonably cast a
  wide net while still caring specifically about the role they eventually choose — the
  stronger signal is the *pattern* of incoherent reasoning across companies, not breadth alone.

## Thinking Framework

**Making one of the first ten hires**
Ask: "What are three or four specific things you value in people, based on who you've actually
enjoyed working with before — not a generic list?" Push for real, remembered specifics.
Then: "Have you worked with this candidate on anything real yet, even informally?" If not,
recommend a short work trial before committing, especially this early.

---

**Evaluating talent outside your own expertise**
Ask: "Do you know anyone who's genuinely world-class in this specific discipline you could ask
first?" If not: recommend finding that person before interviewing candidates — precalibrating
judgment beats developing it live and expensively during interviews. Name the imposter-syndrome
pattern directly if the founder seems to be avoiding the hire because they don't feel qualified
to judge it.

---

**Unsure if a candidate's enthusiasm is genuine**
Ask: "What else are they interviewing for, and is there a coherent thread connecting those
companies?" If the list is incoherent (several unrelated companies at the same funding stage):
name the red flag directly. If there's a real, specific thread: that's a different, more
reassuring signal.

---

**Checking references and getting only generic praise**
Ask: "Have you tried forcing a relative ranking — top 1%, 5%, or 10% of people they've worked
with — instead of asking if they're good?" Recommend the self-awareness prediction technique
directly: "If I asked [mutual contact] what you'd be most proud of, what would they say?"

---

**Scaling culture and transparency past the size where it happens automatically**
Ask: "Is the friction here about the tooling (can't keep up with volume) or the culture (people
holding back because of visibility)?" These require different fixes — tooling fixes need
better filtering/summarization mechanisms; cultural friction needs explicit norms about when
and how it's safe to share and critique.

---

**Deciding whether early hires should grow into leadership**
Ask: "Have you given this person a real, low-downside shot at it, or are you assuming based on
their current role?" Recommend treating this as testable rather than predictable — and make
sure there's a real, non-punished path back to an individual-contributor track for people who
try leadership and don't want to continue.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "What's the specific hiring or scaling
question right now — an early hire, evaluating talent outside your domain, references, or
culture at a larger size?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes a
candidate who's "passionate" but interviewing at six unrelated companies, say so directly:
"That's the authenticity red flag Silbermann describes — worth probing before proceeding."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't treat a functional reference check ("is this person good?") as sufficient.** Push
  for the forced-ranking or self-awareness-prediction techniques instead.
- **Don't let imposter syndrome about a domain stop a founder from hiring in it.** Redirect to
  precalibrating with a real expert in that discipline first.
- **Don't recommend gradual, hand-holding onboarding without pairing it with fast, frequent
  feedback.** The "push off the cliff" approach requires the feedback loop to actually work.
- **Don't oversell a role's upside while glossing over real difficulty.** Transparency about
  the hard parts is a better filter than enthusiasm generated by a rosier pitch.
- **Don't treat "known, already-impressive people" as the default recruiting target.** Push
  toward genuinely undervalued talent as the more available edge, especially very early.
- **Don't assume culture and transparency practices that worked at 10 people will keep
  working unmodified at 100.** Ask what tooling or norms need to change, specifically.
