## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder is doubting whether they have what it takes because they
don't fit the "superhero founder" image, deciding where to locate their company, checking
whether an idea is genuinely contrarian, stuck between two pieces of conflicting standard
advice, unsure when to pivot versus persist, or trying to evaluate a cofounder. Hoffman's
central move is rejecting the idea that a great founder is uniformly excellent at everything —
replacing it with a small number of real edges, a testable contrarian thesis, and the
judgment to navigate genuine paradoxes rather than resolve them permanently in one direction.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I don't feel like I have all the skills a founder is supposed to have"
- "Should we move to [wherever everyone says startups should be]?"
- "Is my idea actually contrarian, or am I just telling myself that?"
- "I keep getting told to be persistent, and also told to pivot fast — which is it?"
- "We've had a rough few months and I don't know if that means we should quit or push harder"
- "How do I evaluate a potential cofounder beyond just liking them?"

This is not the right skill for the mechanics of choosing and vetting a cofounder or early
hire in detail (`hiring/team-and-cofounders-altman.md`) or for deriving core values
(`hiring/culture-chesky-lin.md`) — this is specifically about the founder's own mindset and
self-assessment, plus how an investor evaluates that in others.

## Assumptions

1. **The person is evaluating their own fitness or approach as a founder**, or evaluating a
   cofounder — not asking about a specific tactical business decision.
2. **There's a real idea or company already in motion**, even early. The contrarian-thesis and
   confidence-tracking framework needs something concrete to test against, not an abstract
   question about founder personality traits.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Hoffman states specific,
   sometimes uncomfortable claims (founders have no work-life balance; most self-described
   contrarian ideas aren't actually contrarian) from direct experience at PayPal and LinkedIn.

## Insights

> "The classic image is founder as Superman or Superwoman — a panopticon of skills. I'm great
> at product, strategy, management, fundraising. The reality is a founder deals with all these
> different headaches and no one is universally powered. Generally you hope to have a couple
> superpowers — not a function of genius, but a real edge unique to you and the problem."
— Reid Hoffman, CS183B Lecture 13, 2014

Context: Hoffman's opening correction to the founder-as-superhero myth, illustrated by an
anecdote about reading that "Bill Gates is smarter than Einstein" early in his own
entrepreneurial career — a framing he says is both wrong and actively unhelpful.

> "Location is a test for whether you're thinking like a great founder — because part of the
> job is seeking the networks essential to your task, not assuming you can do this anywhere.
> Groupon couldn't have been founded in Silicon Valley — it needed a massive sales force, and
> Silicon Valley is allergic to that kind of plan. A fashion company probably shouldn't move to
> Silicon Valley either, even if the idea itself is good — you want the networks that support
> what you're actually doing."
— Reid Hoffman, CS183B Lecture 13, 2014

Context: A direct argument against treating Silicon Valley as the default location regardless
of business type — the value of a location is in the specific networks (talent, capital,
customers) it concentrates for a specific kind of business, not a general property that
transfers to any startup.

> "It's easy to become contrarian. It's hard to be contrarian and right. Contrarian is relevant
> to an audience — the test is: how would a smart, expert person actually disagree with me?
> If you can't articulate that, your idea probably isn't contrarian. With LinkedIn, two-thirds
> of my network, including very smart people, thought I was nuts, because a network product has
> no value until it has enough people in it. What I knew that they didn't was a specific way to
> grow toward that size before needing to deliver on the full value proposition."
— Reid Hoffman, CS183B Lecture 13, 2014

Context: The core test for a real contrarian bet — not just disagreement from anyone, but
disagreement from someone smart and informed, paired with a specific, articulable thing the
founder knows that the critics don't.

> "You have to hold apparent paradoxes at once: do the work yourself, and delegate it — both
> at 100%. Be flexible, and be persistent. Have belief, and have fear. Focus internally on the
> product, and focus externally on networks and intelligence. Part of being a great founder is
> executing judgment on what the current problem actually looks like, not resolving these into
> one permanent answer."
— Reid Hoffman, CS183B Lecture 13, 2014

Context: Hoffman's central structural claim — most standard founder advice comes in
contradictory pairs, and the skill isn't picking the correct side once, it's judging which side
the current moment calls for.

> "Have an investment thesis — why you think this is a good idea, including what you know that
> others don't. Then constantly ask: am I increasing confidence in this thesis, or decreasing
> it? If confidence is decreasing, that doesn't mean jump out immediately — but it's your signal
> to figure out what would fix that, which becomes your actual action plan."
— Reid Hoffman, CS183B Lecture 13, 2014

Context: The mechanism Hoffman uses to decide between persistence and flexibility in practice —
illustrated with PayPal's August 2000 crisis (burning $12 million, no revenue, confidence
dropping), where the response was to diagnose what would restore confidence, not to panic or
blindly persevere.

> "At PayPal, the whole use case was splitting a dinner tab with Palm Pilots — zero to one in
> every restaurant. We realized cash-on-Palm-Pilots wouldn't work even before launch, just by
> thinking through the actual mechanics. That's when Max came up with sending payments over
> email instead — and that's what the whole thing pivoted into."
— Reid Hoffman, CS183B Lecture 13, 2014

Context: A concrete example of catching a fatal flaw in a thesis through direct reasoning about
mechanics before wasting time on a launch that couldn't have worked — the specific
company-saving pivot (email payments) came directly out of that diagnosis.

> "Product distribution is more fundamental than the product itself — no matter how good your
> product is, if it doesn't reach customers, you're finished. And financing is the layer below
> that, because if you run out of money the whole effort disappears even with a great idea. When
> you're fundraising, you should already be thinking about the next raise and the relationships
> that will matter for it."
— Reid Hoffman, CS183B Lecture 13, 2014

Context: Hoffman's layered priority stack — product quality matters, but distribution and
financing are the more fundamental constraints that determine whether a good product ever gets
the chance to matter.

> "I'm a huge believer in references — I only meet with someone who comes to me through a
> reference. With Airbnb, I could interrupt their pitch two minutes in and offer to invest,
> because I already had references on them before they walked in. That's true of most great
> investors — the network is what's really key."
— Reid Hoffman, CS183B Lecture 13, 2014, from the Q&A

Context: Hoffman's own filter for evaluating founders as an investor — a strong reference,
obtained before the meeting, does more work than the pitch itself.

> "What frequently gets me wrong about someone is mistaking mimicked adaptability for real
> adaptability — someone who's learned to look like they're reasoning with you, listening to
> your concerns, while actually ignoring you. Ignoring me might be fine. Ignoring the world in
> general is usually a disaster. The phrase I look for is infinite learning curve."
— Reid Hoffman, CS183B Lecture 13, 2014, from the Q&A

Context: A specific failure mode in evaluating founders — surface-level responsiveness in
conversation doesn't guarantee the founder actually updates their approach when the real world
provides disconfirming evidence.

> "What makes a great co-founding team is whether they collaborate to get to truth, not whether
> they just sing from the same songbook. At PayPal, Max would frequently walk into Peter's
> office and say, 'here are things that are going to kill us.' That collective problem-solving
> — not agreement for its own sake — is what actually makes a great team."
— Reid Hoffman, CS183B Lecture 13, 2014, from the Q&A

Context: Hoffman's test for cofounder quality — genuine, ongoing challenge and reasoning
between cofounders, illustrated by a specific real exchange at PayPal, rather than surface
harmony.

## Principles

1. **A great founder has a couple of real superpowers, not universal excellence.** The
   superhero-founder image is not just wrong, it's actively unhelpful for self-assessment.
2. **Choose a location for the specific networks it provides your specific business, not for
   general prestige.** The right location differs by business type — what's ideal for a
   mobile marketplace is often wrong for a sales-force-dependent or physical-goods business.
3. **A real contrarian idea survives the test of "how would a smart, informed critic
   disagree with me" — and requires a specific, articulable thing you know that they don't.**
   Mere disagreement from anyone isn't the bar.
4. **Standard founder advice comes in contradictory pairs (persistent/flexible, belief/fear,
   internal/external focus, do-it-yourself/delegate) — the skill is judging which the current
   moment calls for, not resolving the pair once and for all.**
5. **Track whether your confidence in your investment thesis is increasing or decreasing over
   time — that's the practical signal for persisting versus changing course.** A single bad
   stretch isn't the signal; a sustained, unaddressed decline is.
6. **Take intelligent, focused risk — bet on the specific thing you believe, sized so that
   being right about it makes many other things break your way — rather than generalized
   risk tolerance.**
7. **Product distribution matters more than product quality, and financing is the layer
   beneath both.** A good product that can't reach customers, or a company that runs out of
   money, fails regardless of quality.
8. **References, obtained before a conversation, are a stronger signal than the pitch itself.**
   This is true both for founders evaluating investors and investors evaluating founders.
9. **Real adaptability means actually updating in response to the world, not performing
   attentiveness while quietly ignoring feedback.** The tell is whether behavior changes when
   the evidence warrants it, not whether the founder seems to be listening.
10. **A great cofounding team collaborates to surface hard truths to each other, rather than
    maintaining surface harmony.** Genuine internal challenge is a sign of health, not
    dysfunction.
11. **What "great" requires is domain-specific.** Software rewards speed and learning velocity;
    hardware punishes mistakes disproportionately and rewards accuracy; physical-goods
    businesses require real operational efficiency discipline. There's no universal founder
    profile independent of the specific problem.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Founders have no work-life balance" is Hoffman's strong, blunt personal view, delivered
  provocatively (via a specific anecdote mocking a governor's pitch about balanced
  lifestyles).** Treat it as a real, considered claim about the intensity real founders
  typically sustain for a period — not as a universal prescription that anyone without total
  imbalance isn't a real founder, or as license to treat burnout as automatically virtuous.
- **The "investment thesis, track confidence" framework requires genuine, honest tracking to
  work — it can be misused to rationalize staying the course indefinitely** by always finding
  some reason confidence "should" be increasing. The check is whether the founder can point to
  specific, real evidence, the way Hoffman does with PayPal's August 2000 numbers — not a vague
  feeling that things are fine.
- **The contrarian test ("how would a smart critic disagree with me") is a genuinely useful
  filter, but it's not proof by itself that an idea is good — only that it's genuinely
  contrarian rather than falsely so.** A real contrarian idea can still be wrong; the test
  screens out ideas that were never actually contrarian, not ideas that are contrarian and
  incorrect.
- **The Silicon Valley location advice is time- and category-bound to 2014's specific
  networks** (mobile, marketplaces, certain software categories). The underlying principle —
  match location to the specific networks your business actually needs — is durable; the
  specific list of what's concentrated where changes over time and should be re-derived, not
  assumed.
- **"References only" as an evaluation filter works for someone with Hoffman's own network
  density and reputation.** A less-connected founder or investor can't necessarily replicate
  this exact filter — the transferable lesson is that a credible third party's word carries
  real signal, not that cold, unreferred approaches are worthless in every context.
- **The domain-specific founder-fit point (software rewards speed, hardware punishes mistakes)
  is a real and useful distinction, but it's a spectrum, not a strict binary** — most
  businesses have some blend of these pressures, and treating a business as purely one type
  can miss real constraints from the other.

## Thinking Framework

**Doubting whether you have "what it takes"**
Ask: "What are the one or two things you're genuinely better at than most people around this
specific problem?" Push past a generic self-assessment toward something concrete and specific.
If they can't name anything specific: that's worth addressing directly, but redirect away from
"do I have all the skills" — that's the wrong question per Hoffman's framing.

---

**Choosing a location**
Ask: "What specific networks does this business actually need — talent, capital, customers,
distribution partners — and where are those actually concentrated?" Push back directly if the
answer is "everyone says you should be in [place]" without a specific network reason tied to
the business itself.

---

**Checking if an idea is genuinely contrarian**
Ask: "How would a smart, informed person in this space actually argue against you — not a
strawman, a real objection?" If they can't produce a real objection: the idea may not be
contrarian at all. Then ask: "What do you know that they don't?" If there's no specific answer:
name this directly as the missing piece, using the LinkedIn network-density example if helpful.

---

**Stuck between conflicting advice (persist vs. pivot, focus vs. explore)**
Don't resolve this as if one side is always correct. Ask: "What's your investment thesis, and
is your confidence in it increasing or decreasing right now, based on specific evidence?" Push
for concrete evidence, not a vague feeling. If confidence has been genuinely declining over a
sustained period: that's the pivot signal. If it's one rough stretch: don't treat it as the same
signal.

---

**Evaluating a cofounder**
Ask: "When you disagree, does one of you actually change the other's mind sometimes — or does
it always resolve toward agreement without real friction?" Reference the PayPal example
directly: the sign of a strong team is one cofounder walking in and saying "here's what's going
to kill us," not constant harmony.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "What's the specific question right
now — your own fit as a founder, location, whether an idea is contrarian, persist-vs-pivot, or
evaluating a cofounder?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes an idea as
"contrarian" but can't name a real, smart objection to it, say so directly: "That's not
actually contrarian yet by Hoffman's test — worth finding the real objection before treating
this as validated by disagreement."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't validate the "I need to be great at everything" framing.** Redirect toward
  identifying the one or two specific real edges instead.
- **Don't accept "Silicon Valley" (or any default location) as sufficient reasoning without a
  specific network justification tied to the business.**
- **Don't accept "people think I'm crazy" as proof of a contrarian idea on its own.** Push for
  the specific thing the founder knows that smart critics don't.
- **Don't treat "be persistent" or "be flexible" as a permanent stance to adopt.** Redirect to
  the investment-thesis-confidence framework for judging which applies right now.
- **Don't let vague optimism substitute for specific evidence when assessing whether confidence
  is increasing or decreasing.** Push for concrete data points.
- **Don't treat cofounder harmony as automatically a good sign.** Ask specifically whether real
  disagreement and mutual correction happen.
