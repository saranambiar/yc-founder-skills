## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder is validating a problem before building anything,
defining what belongs in their MVP, struggling to get their first handful of users, or trying
to distinguish real signal from polite feedback. Cheung grounds all of this in her own
failure first — a year spent building a platform for life coaches and therapists neither she
nor her cofounder would ever personally use — before walking through what she did differently
at Homejoy.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I have an idea, but I'm not sure if it's actually a problem worth solving"
- "I don't know what should and shouldn't be in my first version"
- "I can't get anyone to try my product, even people I know"
- "Everyone I show my product to says they like it, but I'm not sure I believe them"
- "A user asked for a specific feature — should I just build it?"
- "Should I keep building in private until it's polished, or launch now?"

This is not the right skill for growth mechanics once you have real usage, or for deciding
when to pivot — see `growth/growth-mechanics-cheung.md`, the second half of this same
lecture. For the deeper mechanics of running a structured user interview specifically, see
`user-research/user-interviews-shear.md`.

## Assumptions

1. **There's a candidate problem or idea, but no validated product yet.** This skill is about
   the path from idea to a validated first version, not about generating ideas from nothing.
2. **The founder has the ability to personally immerse themselves in the target industry or
   user base**, at least temporarily. Cheung's method (become a cleaner, get a job at a
   cleaning company) assumes the founder can get genuinely close to the work itself.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Cheung speaks from her own
   costly mistake (a wasted year) and states specific, sometimes counterintuitive claims
   (become a "cog" in the industry you want to disrupt) from direct experience.

## Insights

> "One of the biggest mistakes I've made... we had two goals: create a company that made
> people happy, and create something impactful. So a good proxy for that was to make it huge.
> We came up with life coaches and therapists. But when we started using the product
> ourselves, we're not cynical people, but life coaches and therapists are just not people we
> would use ourselves. It wasn't even a problem we had."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014, on Pathjoy, the company before Homejoy

Context: Cheung and her cofounder spent almost a year on this before realizing it. Her
corrective: state the problem in one sentence, then ask whether it's a problem you personally
have, before asking whether other people have it.

> "As a noob coming into the industry, you should take one or two months just really
> understanding the little bits and pieces of how it works — because it's when you get into
> the details that you start seeing things you can exploit, things that are really inefficient."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: A direct pushback on the instinct to stay an outsider to "see clearly." Cheung and
her cofounder became house cleaners themselves and one of them took a job at a local cleaning
company specifically to learn why local cleaning businesses couldn't scale the way Homejoy
eventually did.

> "You should be so obsessed that you know what everybody in the space is doing — running a
> list of every competitor, reading every article from search result 1 to 1,000. If they're
> public, read their S-1s, their quarterly financials, sit on the earnings calls. Most of it
> you get nothing from, but there are golden nuggets you won't find any other way."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: The level of obsessiveness Cheung argues is required before you can credibly claim to
be the expert building a solution — not a one-time competitive scan, but sustained immersion.

> "We started off with something super complicated — 'we're an online platform for home
> services, you can choose blah blah blah' — paragraphs and paragraphs. Users got bored after
> the first few sentences. We changed our positioning to 'get your place clean for $20 an
> hour,' and then everyone got it."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: On the need for a one-sentence functional positioning before showing a product to
anyone — Homejoy's own early positioning failed this test badly before being simplified.

> "Your first users should be the obvious people — you and your cofounder, your mom and dad,
> your friends and coworkers. Beyond that... at a street fair in Mountain View, on a hot day,
> we froze water bottles and handed out free cold water. People just came to us. We basically
> guilt-tripped people into booking cleanings — but the majority of them didn't cancel."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: A concrete illustration that early user acquisition sometimes requires manufacturing
a reason for a stranger to engage at all — the guilt/water-bottle tactic worked because it got
real people to try a real service, most of whom stayed once they experienced it.

> "It's not a good idea to sit there poking at them like an inquisition — why'd you do that,
> why'd you do that? What you should do is make it a conversation, get them comfortable — I
> found taking people out for drinks was a very good way to do that."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: Direct advice on interview tone — comfort produces honesty; interrogation produces
defensiveness, regardless of how good the questions themselves are.

> "There's an honesty curve. Your mom will be proud of you and only be this honest. Your
> friends will be pretty honest because they care about you. Random people who don't know you
> don't care about giving you feedback at all — unless they paid for it. If they paid and
> didn't think it was worth it, they'll really tell you, because it's money out the door."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: Cheung's model for calibrating how much to trust any piece of feedback — both social
distance and whether money changed hands shift how honest someone actually is with you.

> "The problem with retention data is it takes forever to collect. A good leading indicator is
> collecting reviews and ratings, and some notion of NPS — how likely they are to recommend
> you to a friend, on a scale of zero to ten."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: Offered as a faster proxy signal than waiting months for real retention data to
accumulate — useful specifically because the real metric takes too long to be actionable early.

> "We would ask cleaning professionals a bunch of questions, then a test clean, then onboard
> them if they were good enough — about a 3-5% acceptance rate. If you try to automate this
> too fast, you can't iterate on the questions that actually predict who'll be good."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014, on "manual before automation"

Context: Homejoy learned which interview questions actually predicted performance only because
the vetting process stayed manual and iterable long enough to find out — automating early
would have locked in untested assumptions.

> "Temporary brokenness is much better than permanent paralysis. Perfection is irrelevant at
> this stage — don't worry about edge cases, worry about the generic case of who your core
> user is. And beware the Frankenstein approach: when someone asks for a feature, get to the
> bottom of why, instead of just building it — usually what they're really saying is there's
> another problem you created, or one that needs solving before they'll pay."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: Two related warnings against two different failure modes — overbuilding for edge
cases nobody has yet, and reflexively building whatever specific feature a user names instead
of finding the underlying problem.

## Principles

1. **State the problem in one sentence, and check whether it's your own problem before
   checking whether it's anyone else's.** Cheung's costliest mistake was skipping this and
   losing a year to an idea neither founder actually needed.
2. **Immerse yourself in the industry as a "cog," not an outside disruptor.** The
   inefficiencies worth exploiting are only visible from inside the day-to-day detail.
3. **Obsessive competitive research — S-1s, earnings calls, every search result — is a real
   requirement, not paranoia.** Occasional genuinely valuable information only surfaces
   through sustained volume.
4. **Storyboard the entire user experience, end to end, before writing code.** Include how
   they find out about you and what happens after they finish using the product, not just the
   interface itself.
5. **Get to a one-sentence, functional positioning before showing the product to anyone.**
   Complicated, paragraph-length descriptions lose people before they understand what you do.
6. **Your first users are the obvious circle — you, your cofounder, family, friends — then
   deliberate, sometimes manufactured, direct outreach.** Expect a very low conversion rate
   at this stage; that's normal, not a signal to stop.
7. **Feedback conversations should feel like a conversation, not an interrogation.** Comfort
   produces honesty; being grilled produces defensiveness.
8. **Calibrate how much to trust feedback using the honesty curve — social distance and
   whether money changed hands both shift honesty.** Random strangers who paid and weren't
   satisfied are your most reliable source of hard truth.
9. **Manual process should precede automation, specifically so you can keep iterating on what
   you don't yet understand.** Automating too early locks in guesses you haven't validated.
10. **Don't build every requested feature — find the underlying problem behind the request
    first.** The literal ask is usually not the best solution to the real issue.
11. **Launch fast rather than staying stealth.** Imitation is cheaper than innovation, and a
    good idea will likely get fast-followed regardless of how long you wait.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Become a cog in the industry" assumes the founder can personally access that
  experience** (Cheung and her cofounder could physically become cleaners). For businesses
  where the target user's experience can't be personally replicated by the founder (certain
  regulated professions, highly specialized technical domains), the underlying goal — genuine,
  detailed immersion — still applies, but the specific tactic needs to be adapted, not copied
  literally.
- **The water-bottle/street-fair guerrilla tactic worked because it converted strangers into
  people who tried a real, working service — it wasn't manipulation without substance
  underneath.** Cheung is explicit that most people who used the service that way didn't
  cancel afterward. Don't extract "trick people into trying it" without the underlying
  requirement that the product actually delivers once they do.
- **The honesty curve is a useful heuristic, not a strict formula.** Money changing hands
  generally increases honesty, but it's not the only variable — a random unpaid user with
  strong opinions can still give real signal, and a paying friend may still soften feedback
  out of social obligation despite the money involved.
- **"Manual before automation" is about the validation and iteration period specifically** —
  it's not a claim that automation is bad or unnecessary once you know what you're automating.
  Homejoy did eventually build the online application; the point is sequencing, not avoiding
  automation altogether.
- **"Don't worry about edge cases" applies to the earliest stage with a small, generic core
  user base.** As the user base grows, the volume of edge cases grows with it and does need
  building for — this isn't permission to ignore edge cases indefinitely.
- **"Launch fast, imitation is cheaper than innovation" is explicitly qualified by Cheung
  herself** — she carves out an exception for businesses that require tens of millions of
  dollars just to start. Don't apply the launch-fast urgency uncritically to capital-intensive
  businesses where the calculus is different.

## Thinking Framework

**Validating a problem before building**
Ask: "Can you state the problem in one sentence? And is it a problem you personally have?" If
they can't state it in one sentence: that's the first thing to fix, not premature refinement.
If it's not a personal problem: ask directly whether they've verified it's a problem for
specific other people, not just a plausible-sounding one — and flag the Pathjoy story if the
answer sounds thin.

---

**Defining MVP scope**
Ask: "Have you storyboarded the entire experience — how someone finds you, what happens after
they finish using it — or just the interface?" Push for the full end-to-end picture before
scoping features. Then: "What's the smallest version of that storyboard that actually solves
the core problem?"

---

**Can't get first users**
Ask: "Have you exhausted the obvious circle — yourself, your cofounder, family, friends —
before trying strangers?" If not: start there. If yes and it's still not working: ask what
specific, even manufactured, direct-contact tactic they could try (a physical location where
the right people already gather, a specific reason for a stranger to engage) rather than a
passive channel.

---

**Feedback feels too polite or uniformly positive**
Ask: "Who exactly are you getting this feedback from, and are they paying?" Apply the honesty
curve directly — feedback from friends or unpaid strangers is systematically softer. Recommend
finding paying users, or at least people with real social distance, before trusting positive
signal.

---

**A user asked for a specific feature**
Ask: "What do you think is the underlying problem behind that request?" Don't let the founder
jump straight to "should I build it" — push for the diagnosis first. Reference the Frankenstein
warning directly if they're already listing several requested features to build in sequence
without asking why for any of them.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "Where are you right now — validating
the problem, scoping the MVP, finding first users, or reading feedback you're not sure you
trust?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes building
for a year without using the product themselves, say so directly: "That's the Pathjoy
pattern — worth checking whether this is actually your problem before building further."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't let a founder skip stating the problem in one sentence.** If they can't, that's the
  actual blocker, not a detail to come back to later.
- **Don't validate staying an outside observer of an industry as sufficient preparation.**
  Push for real immersion — becoming a temporary participant, not just a researcher.
- **Don't accept a complicated, multi-sentence product description as ready to show users.**
  Push for the one-sentence functional version first.
- **Don't treat interrogation-style user interviews as equivalent to conversational ones.**
  The tone itself changes what people are willing to say.
- **Don't take uniformly positive feedback at face value without checking who it's from.**
  Apply the honesty curve before trusting it.
- **Don't recommend building a specific requested feature without first asking what problem
  is actually behind the request.**
