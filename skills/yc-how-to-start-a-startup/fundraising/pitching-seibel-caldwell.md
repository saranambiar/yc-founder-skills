## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder is preparing to pitch investors, unsure when to actually
start fundraising, setting up investor meetings for the first time, wanting to check their own
pitch against a concrete example of what goes wrong, or deciding how to follow up afterward.
Seibel's framework is deliberately short — a 30-second pitch in exactly three sentences, a
2-minute pitch with four more components — on the theory that the more you talk, the more
chances you have to say something an investor doesn't like.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "How do I explain what we do in a way that doesn't take ten minutes?"
- "When should I actually start trying to raise money?"
- "How do I even get an investor meeting?"
- "I just had a pitch meeting and I'm not sure if it went well or why"
- "An investor said they'd stay in touch — what does that actually mean?"

This is not the right skill for the mechanics of early-stage sales to customers — see
`sales/early-sales-bosmeny.md`, from the same lecture but a different half with a different
speaker. For the broader framework of what investors look for beyond the pitch itself, see
`fundraising/what-investors-want-andreessen-conway.md`.

## Assumptions

1. **The founder is preparing for, or reflecting on, an actual investor conversation** — this
   is about the specific mechanics of pitching and meeting logistics, not the deeper strategic
   question of whether or how much to raise.
2. **There's a real company and (ideally) some traction or fast execution to describe.** The
   framework assumes something concrete to pitch, even if pre-launch.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Seibel and Caldwell state
   specific, sometimes blunt rules (two sentences for your unique insight, no more; anything
   short of money in the bank is a no) and demonstrate them with a live, contrasting mock pitch.

## Insights

> "The 30-second pitch is three sentences. First: what your company does — apply the mom
> test. If you can't tell your mom what you do in one plain sentence, rework it. 'We're Airbnb,
> we let you rent out the extra room in your house' — not 'we're a marketplace for space.'
> Second: how big is the market, in a multi-billion-dollar frame. Third: your traction, or if
> pre-launch, how fast you're moving."
— Michael Seibel, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: Seibel's own diagnostic for a broken pitch — most founders fail on the very first
sentence, using vague or abstracted language instead of the concrete version anyone,
including a non-technical relative, could understand immediately.

> "The more you talk, the more opportunity you have to say something people don't like. Talk
> less and it will probably be better. Your two-minute pitch adds four things: unique insight
> in exactly two sentences — your 'aha' moment. If I say 'I already knew that' or I don't
> understand what you said, you didn't nail it."
— Michael Seibel, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: A hard constraint (two sentences) forces a founder to actually crystallize their
sharpest, most specific insight rather than describing their business in general terms — and
Seibel gives himself as the real-time test of whether it landed.

> "For business model: just say it. If it's advertising, say advertising — Facebook and Google
> are massive advertising businesses. Founders get in trouble when they hedge across five
> options — advertising, maybe virtual goods, maybe this — because now you've told me you have
> no idea how you'll monetize. Say the one that's standard for your category and move on."
— Michael Seibel, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: Directly countering the instinct to avoid naming a specific model out of fear it
sounds unoriginal or is wrong — hedging across many options reads as a lack of a real answer,
which is worse than a plain, standard one.

> "For team: only mention credentials that made someone money before — 'we were the founders
> of PayPal.' Don't talk about awards or PhDs, I don't care. What I want is founder count
> (ideally two to four), technical ratio (ideally half or more engineers), how long you've
> known each other (ideally six months plus, not three days from cofounder dating), and
> whether you're all full-time."
— Michael Seibel, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: A specific, checkable list replacing the vague instinct to list impressive-sounding
but investor-irrelevant credentials — with a direct warning that over-explaining a weak
credential makes it look worse, not better.

> "For the ask: this is the one place you should use jargon, and you'd better actually know
> it — are you raising on a note or a SAFE, what's the cap, how much are you raising, what's
> the minimum check size. If you don't know this, investors read it as 'these people haven't
> done their homework.'"
— Michael Seibel, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: The one deliberate exception to "avoid jargon" — the specific financing mechanics are
exactly where precision signals seriousness, and vagueness here specifically damages
credibility.

> "Flip the power dynamic. You want to be fundraising when investors are asking to give you
> money, not when you desperately need it. If you can't show traction yet, show a fully
> committed team moving fast — quit your jobs, launched a beta in six weeks. Never put an
> investor in the position where you're saying 'we can't do anything until you give us money.'"
— Michael Seibel, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: A direct reframe of fundraising timing — the goal isn't finding the earliest possible
moment to ask, it's finding the moment where the leverage in the conversation favors the
founder, which traction or (as a substitute) visible execution speed both create.

> "Warm introductions only — from another entrepreneur or a previous investor, ideally.
> Schedule meetings in parallel, the same week, not spread out over a month: fundraising is a
> sprint, not a marathon. One trick — when an investor responds to your intro, say 'we're
> building like crazy for two weeks, can we meet in week three?' That gets everyone's meetings
> into the same window, and it signals you're not desperate."
— Michael Seibel, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: Specific, tactical advice for the actual logistics of scheduling — both the batching
itself and the framing used to achieve it do real signaling work beyond pure convenience.

> "In the bad mock pitch: vague description that required three follow-up questions just to
> understand the product, evasive on real numbers ('we don't want to open the kimono'),
> name-dropping unrelated big companies for market size, and never driving the conversation
> anywhere. In the good one: concrete relatable examples, real specific numbers (350 locations,
> 11% weekly growth), an honest counterintuitive insight from real data, and a clear, specific
> ask with existing commitments and a real deadline."
— Dalton Caldwell and Qasar Younis, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014, from the live mock pitch

Context: The two live, contrasting pitches make the abstract advice concrete — the bad pitch
fails on almost every principle Seibel names (vague opener, evasive numbers, irrelevant
comparisons), and the good one succeeds by being specific and direct on each of the same points.

> "After the meeting: anything other than a check or wired funds is a no. Build real deal
> heat — genuine demand to be in your round — because that's what actually drives terms. Do
> diligence on your investors, not just the reverse; you're selling them part of your company.
> And know when to stop: fundraising is more legible and easier than building the company,
> which makes it tempting to spend too much time on it."
— Qasar Younis and Dalton Caldwell, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: Closing guidance on the part after the pitch itself — treating soft "stay in touch"
responses honestly, working the investor side of diligence just as hard as the reverse, and
naming directly why fundraising can become a trap some founders over-invest in because it
feels more like measurable progress than the harder, more ambiguous work of building.

## Principles

1. **The 30-second pitch is exactly three sentences: what you do (the mom test), market size,
   and traction (or execution speed if pre-launch).** Most pitches fail on the first sentence
   by being too abstract.
2. **The more you say, the more chances you create to say something an investor doesn't
   like.** Brevity is a real strategic choice, not a compromise.
3. **A real unique insight fits in two sentences.** If it takes longer, it likely isn't
   crystallized yet — practice until the "aha" reaction is real and immediate.
4. **State your business model plainly, even if it's the standard one for your category.**
   Hedging across multiple options reads as not having a real answer.
5. **Only cite team credentials that previously made someone money.** Otherwise, stick to
   checkable facts (founder count, technical ratio, tenure working together, full-time status).
6. **Know your specific financing terms cold — instrument, cap, amount, minimum check.** This
   is the one place precision and jargon are expected, not avoided.
7. **Fundraise from a position of strength, not need.** Traction is the strongest form of
   leverage; a fully committed, fast-moving team is the substitute when traction doesn't exist
   yet.
8. **Use warm introductions exclusively, and batch meetings into the same week.** Fundraising
   is a sprint with real signaling value in how it's scheduled, not just a logistics exercise.
9. **Concrete, specific numbers and honest, checkable insights beat name-dropping and
   evasiveness every time.** The contrast between the two mock pitches demonstrates this
   directly, point for point.
10. **Treat any response short of money in the bank as a no**, while still building genuine
    deal heat to drive better terms and doing real diligence on investors in return.
11. **Fundraising is not success, and it's more legible and easier than building the company —
    which is exactly why it's tempting to over-invest time in it.** Know when to stop and get
    back to building.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **The strict sentence limits (two sentences for unique insight, three for the whole 30-second
  pitch) are a discipline for forcing clarity, not a claim that a real business can be fully
  explained in that space.** The constraint is meant to sharpen the pitch's opening, not to
  substitute for a deeper conversation once real interest exists.
- **"State your business model plainly, even the standard one" assumes there is a standard,
  known model for the category.** Genuinely novel business models may legitimately need more
  than one sentence — the point is to avoid hedging out of uncertainty, not to compress real
  novelty into false simplicity.
- **"Only cite credentials that made someone money" is calibrated to investor conversations
  specifically** — it doesn't mean other credentials (deep technical expertise, relevant
  domain background) are irrelevant to whether someone is actually a strong hire or cofounder;
  it's specifically about what an investor, in a two-minute window, finds persuasive.
- **The "flip the power dynamic, don't fundraise from need" framing is genuinely useful, but
  it can be misapplied to delay fundraising indefinitely while waiting for a stronger position
  that may not materialize without capital.** Some categories genuinely require funding before
  any meaningful traction is possible — the substitute signal (committed team, fast execution)
  exists precisely for that case; use it rather than waiting for traction that structurally
  can't happen without the money.
- **The meeting-scheduling trick (delaying responses to batch meetings into one week) is a
  real, useful tactic — but it depends on genuine interest already existing.** It's a
  scheduling and signaling technique, not a way to manufacture interest that isn't there.
- **"Fundraising does not equal success" is a real, important caution — but it isn't a reason
  to treat a successful raise as unimportant.** The point is not to conflate the process with
  the outcome it's meant to enable, not that raising money doesn't matter at all.

## Thinking Framework

**Preparing a pitch**
Ask: "Can you say what you do in one plain sentence your mom would understand?" If not: that's
the actual starting point, before market size or traction. Work through all three 30-second
sentences first, then the four 2-minute additions, one at a time — don't let the founder jump
to the ask before the fundamentals are solid.

---

**Deciding when to start fundraising**
Ask: "Right now, are investors approaching you, or are you the one initiating every
conversation?" If the latter and there's no real traction yet: ask what evidence of fast,
committed execution exists instead — a working beta shipped quickly, a fully committed
full-time team. Use that as the substitute leverage rather than waiting passively for traction
that may not come without some capital.

---

**Setting up investor meetings**
Ask: "Is this a warm introduction, or are you going in cold?" Redirect firmly toward warm
intros only. Then ask about scheduling: recommend batching responses into a single week rather
than accepting whatever slots come first, and suggest the "we're building for two weeks" framing
directly if it fits the situation.

---

**Reflecting on how a pitch meeting went**
Ask them to walk through what they actually said for each of the seven components (three
30-second, four 2-minute). Compare directly against the mock-pitch contrast: was the opening
description concrete or vague? Were the numbers specific or evasive? Was the market size
grounded in the real business or name-dropping unrelated giants?

---

**Following up after a meeting**
Ask: "What did they actually say — a specific commitment, or something softer?" If it's
anything short of a real commitment: name it plainly as a no for now, without cutting off the
relationship. Ask whether they've done real diligence on this investor in return, and whether
genuine deal heat exists yet to strengthen the terms of a live round.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "What's the specific pitching question
right now — preparing a pitch, fundraising timing, setting up meetings, reviewing how one
went, or following up after?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes an
investor who said they'd "stay in touch" as a promising lead, say so directly: "That's a no
for now, by Seibel and Caldwell's own rule — anything short of a real commitment should be
read that way, even if the relationship is worth keeping warm."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't let a founder's opening description stay abstract or jargon-heavy.** Push for the
  mom-test version before anything else.
- **Don't accept hedging across multiple monetization options as a real answer.** Push for the
  one standard model, stated plainly.
- **Don't let irrelevant credentials (awards, unrelated degrees) substitute for the specific,
  checkable team facts investors actually weigh.**
- **Don't let a founder go into a pitch without knowing their specific financing terms cold.**
  This is the one place vagueness actively damages credibility.
- **Don't recommend cold outreach to investors when a warm introduction path is available.**
- **Don't let ambiguous, non-committal investor responses be read as real progress.** Name
  them as a no for now, while keeping the relationship intact.
