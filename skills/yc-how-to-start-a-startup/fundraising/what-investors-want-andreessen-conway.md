## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder is preparing to raise money, choosing between
investors, negotiating dilution or terms, deciding how much to raise and when, or worried
that a lack of traction disqualifies them. Three distinct voices are in this transcript —
Andreessen (multi-stage VC), Conway (seed-stage angel), and Conrad (a founder who was
rejected by 60 firms before eventually raising easily) — and they're attributed separately
throughout, since they sometimes emphasize different things from different vantage points.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I'm about to start pitching investors and don't know what actually makes them say yes"
- "How much should I raise, and how do I know if it's too much or too little?"
- "I have two term sheets — how do I know which investor is actually the better pick?"
- "I don't have a product yet, or any users — can I still raise?"
- "An investor wants me to sign an NDA before we talk"
- "I'm getting pressure to raise a huge round because 'that's what's happening now'"

This is not the right skill for: choosing a cofounder or making early hires
(`hiring/team-and-cofounders-altman.md`), evaluating whether the underlying idea is any good
(`ideas/idea-and-product-altman.md`), or market structure and long-term defensibility
(`market-strategy/competition-monopoly-thiel.md`) — though the "be so good they can't ignore
you" point connects directly to that last one.

## Assumptions

1. **The company is aiming at real venture scale**, or the founder is deciding whether it
   should be. Andreessen is explicit that venture capital is "a 100% game of outliers" — this
   framework is built around that game, not around a business that doesn't need outlier
   growth to succeed.
2. **The person is at seed or Series A stage**, which is what this panel focuses on. Later-
   stage dynamics (growth rounds, board composition) are touched on but not the center of it.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** All three speakers state blunt,
   specific claims from real experience (including Conrad's own failure before his success).
   Match that register once you understand their situation.

## Insights

> "Venture capital is a 100% a game of outliers... on the order of 4,000 venture-fundable
> companies a year want to raise venture capital. About 200 of those will get funded by a top
> tier VC. About 15 of those will someday get to $100 million in revenue, and those 15 will
> generate something on the order of 97% of all the returns for the entire category that
> year."
— Marc Andreessen, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014

Context: The foundational fact behind everything else Andreessen says — a top VC is only
looking for the extreme outlier, which explains why "checkbox" companies (good on every
dimension, remarkable on none) get passed over.

> "We have this concept: invest in strength versus lack of weakness... the companies that
> have really extreme strengths often have serious flaws. If you don't invest on the basis of
> serious flaws, you don't invest in most of the big winners."
— Marc Andreessen, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014

Context: Directly explaining why "well-rounded, no red flags" pitches often fail to raise from
top firms — the winners tend to have one extreme, remarkable strength and real weaknesses
elsewhere, not uniform competence.

> "When you first meet an investor, you've got to be able to say in one compelling sentence
> what your product does... probably 25% of the entrepreneurs I talk to today, after the
> first sentence, I still don't know what they do."
— Ron Conway, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014

Context: Conway's opening screen. If the investor can't picture the product after one
sentence, everything after that sentence is working against the founder, not for them.

> "The number one piece of advice I've ever read... is from Steve Martin: be so good they
> can't ignore you. You're almost always better off making your business better than you are
> making your pitch better."
— Marc Andreessen, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014

Context: Said right after Conrad's own story (below) — Andreessen's point is that fundraising
technique is mostly beside the point next to actually having a business that's clearly working.

> "You don't get it. If you guys were the Twitter guys, you could come in and just put
> whatever up here and we would invest in you. But you're not the Twitter guys, so you need
> all this stuff ready for us... I took the exact opposite lesson of what I think he wanted me
> to take away — which was, I should just figure out a way to be the Twitter guys."
— Parker Conrad, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014, recounting advice from a VC during his first company's fundraise (60 firms, all rejections)

Context: Conrad's key turning point. Instead of optimizing the pitch, he built a business
(Zenefits) with real cash flow that didn't strictly need VC money — and found that was exactly
the kind of business investors want to fund.

> "The other point I want to make is: bootstrap as long as you possibly can... never forget
> the bootstrap."
— Parker Conrad, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014

Context: Directly tied to his own experience — a company that could survive without raising
turned out to be dramatically easier to raise for than one that was pitching out of necessity.

> "Raising venture capital is the easiest thing a startup founder is ever going to do — as
> compared to recruiting engineer number 20, selling to enterprise customers, or getting viral
> growth going. Almost everything you'll ever do is harder than raising venture capital."
— Marc Andreessen, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014

Context: A deliberately provocative reframing — raising money isn't a milestone or a success;
it's what puts you in a position to attempt the actually hard things.

> "You can think about a startup on day one as having every conceivable kind of risk —
> founding team risk, product risk, technical risk, launch risk, market acceptance risk,
> revenue risk, cost-of-sale risk, viral growth risk. Running a startup, and raising money for
> it, is a process of peeling away layers of risk as you go."
— Marc Andreessen, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014, describing the "Onion Theory of Risk" (credited to Andy Rachleff)

Context: The organizing framework for how much to raise and when — each round should be sized
and justified by exactly which layer(s) of risk it's meant to retire, not by "raise as much as
possible."

> "The single biggest thing entrepreneurs are missing, both on fundraising and how they run
> their companies, is the relationship between risk and cash."
— Marc Andreessen, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014

Context: The claim that motivates the Onion Theory in the first place — most founders think
about raising and spending money without a clear model of which specific risks that money is
meant to retire.

> "I always look for, in that first meeting: do you feel like you respect this person, and do
> you feel like you have a lot to learn from them? ...If you'd want this person really
> involved in the company even if they didn't have a checkbook, that's a good sign. If not,
> that's probably a very bad sign."
— Marc Andreessen, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014

Context: On choosing which investor to take money from — explicitly compared to marriage
elsewhere in the panel, since the relationship runs 10-20 years and most of an investor's
value (or damage) isn't visible in a single pitch meeting.

> "If things are going well, the founder is in control, because the company needs more
> money. If things are going badly, the investors are in control — no matter what protections
> you built into the terms."
— Marc Andreessen, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014

Context: Offered as close to a fundamental rule of the founder-investor relationship — board
votes and formal protections matter far less than the underlying leverage created by whether
the company currently needs money or not.

> "We invest in people first, not necessarily the product idea — the product ideas tend to
> morph a lot... if it's pre-users, the valuation is going to tend to be correspondingly
> lower, unless one of the founders has a success track record."
— Ron Conway, CS183B Lecture 9, 2014, on investing pre-traction

Context: Directly answers "can I raise with no product or traction yet" — yes, but the basis
shifts almost entirely to the team (and is easier with a repeat/known founder).

## Principles

1. **Venture capital is an outliers game — "good on every dimension" pitches often lose to
   "extreme on one dimension, flawed elsewhere" ones.** Don't over-optimize for a well-rounded
   pitch at the expense of a real, remarkable strength.
2. **A one-sentence, immediately picturable description of the product is the first filter.**
   If it takes a paragraph, that's a problem before the meeting even really starts.
3. **Fundraising technique matters far less than the underlying business.** "Be so good they
   can't ignore you" — time spent perfecting the pitch is usually better spent improving the
   actual business.
4. **A business that doesn't strictly need the money is, paradoxically, the easiest to raise
   for.** Bootstrap as long as possible; desperation is visible and unattractive to investors.
5. **Raising money is not a milestone — it's what enables the actually hard work.** Recruiting,
   enterprise sales, and organic growth are all harder than raising, in Andreessen's experience.
6. **Size every raise to the specific risks it's meant to retire (the Onion Theory), not to
   "as much as possible."** Over-raising relative to your current risk stage causes problems
   down the line through cumulative dilution and mismatched expectations.
7. **Almost all Series A rounds are preceded by a seed round — the exception is repeat
   founders with a track record.** If aiming for a Series A, raising a seed first is the
   default path, not a detour.
8. **Choosing an investor is closer to choosing a spouse than closing a transaction.** The
   relationship runs 10-20 years, most of its value or damage is invisible in a pitch meeting,
   and second-time founders take this far more seriously than first-timers.
9. **Leverage in the founder-investor relationship tracks whether the company currently needs
   money — not the formal terms.** Protective covenants matter less than this underlying fact
   in practice.
10. **Pre-traction fundraising is real but narrower.** It shifts almost entirely to team
    quality (or a repeat founder's track record), and the valuation reflects that.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Invest in strength versus lack of weakness" can be misused to excuse real, fixable
  problems as "just my flaws that come with my strengths."** Andreessen's point is about what
  investors are willing to tolerate in a winner, not a founder's license to ignore fixable
  weaknesses that aren't attached to any actual strength.
- **"Raising VC is the easiest thing you'll do" is true relative to recruiting, enterprise
  sales, and organic growth — it is not a claim that raising is easy in absolute terms**,
  especially for founders without a network, a hot category, or a compelling story yet. Don't
  let a founder struggling to raise conclude something is unusually wrong with them just
  because the panel calls this "the easy part."
- **The Onion Theory assumes a founder has a clear, honest model of their own risk stack.**
  In practice, naming your own founding-team risk, technical risk, or market-acceptance risk
  accurately is hard — the framework is only as good as the honesty behind it.
- **The Twitter-guys story ("build something so good the pitch doesn't matter") can read as
  discouraging any attention to pitch craft at all.** Conrad's actual point is narrower: don't
  over-invest in pitch mechanics at the expense of the underlying business — not that
  communication (Conway's one-sentence test) doesn't matter.
- **The valuation anchoring story (Conrad's $9M vs. $12M cap) describes a specific market
  moment (2014 YC demo day) with specific psychological thresholds.** The actual numbers don't
  transfer directly to other years or markets — the underlying lesson (there's a real ceiling
  investors will pay regardless of what you ask for, so don't over-anchor on a number) does.
- **"Investors invest in people first when pre-traction" is most true for consumer/web
  startups.** The panel explicitly notes enterprise/SaaS companies are commonly "cold start"
  with no MVP at all and need capital just to build the first real product — a different
  dynamic than the consumer pattern being generalized from.

## Thinking Framework

**Preparing to pitch for the first time**
Ask: "Can you describe what you do in one sentence that would let a stranger picture the
product immediately?" If they can't produce one cleanly, that's the actual first thing to fix
— not the deck, not the financial model. Then ask what's the one dimension where they're
genuinely extreme, not just solid — that's the thing to lead with.

---

**Deciding how much to raise**
Ask: "What specific risks does this round need to retire before your next one?" Walk through
the Onion Theory list (founding team, product, technical, launch, market acceptance, revenue,
cost-of-sale, viral growth) and ask which apply right now. Then: "Does the amount you're
raising match what's needed to retire exactly those, or is it padded beyond that?" Raising
meaningfully more than the current risk stage justifies is a flag, not neutral.

---

**Choosing between investors or term sheets**
Ask: "In your first real meeting with each of them, did you feel respected, and did you feel
like you learned something you'd have wanted even without a check attached?" Push past "which
offers better terms" to this question specifically — it's the one the panel treats as most
predictive of a relationship that has to survive 10-20 years of real stress.

---

**No product or traction yet, wondering if fundraising is possible**
Ask: "Is this a consumer/web-style product, or something like enterprise software where a
real MVP isn't really a meaningful thing to build first?" If consumer/web with no traction: be
direct that the raise will lean almost entirely on team strength (or a known founder's track
record), and valuation will reflect that. If enterprise/infrastructure: name that a "cold
start" needing real capital before a first product exists is normal in that category, not a
red flag specific to them.

---

**Fundraising for reasons other than needing the capital (exit optionality, acqui-hire
insurance)**
Ask directly what's driving the raise. If it's largely about creating exit optionality: name
the tension plainly — investors add real value through networks and expertise regardless of
motive, but this shouldn't be the primary driver of whether to raise, only possibly of which
investor to pick.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what stage or question they're facing, ask: "Where are you in the
raise — about to pitch for the first time, deciding how much to ask for, comparing investors,
or something about traction?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes raising
significantly more than their current risk stage justifies "just to be safe," say so directly:
"That's over-raising relative to where you are — the Onion Theory would flag this." Don't ask
a fourth clarifying question to avoid saying it.

## What Not To Do

- **Don't treat a "well-rounded, no obvious flaws" pitch as inherently strong.** Push for the
  one real, extreme strength — that's what the panel says actually gets funded at the top tier.
- **Don't let a founder over-invest in pitch polish at the expense of the underlying
  business.** If the business itself has a real gap, that's the priority, not the deck.
- **Don't accept "we should raise as much as possible while we can" without pushing back.**
  Ask what specific risk the extra capital is meant to retire — if there's no clear answer,
  name the mismatch.
- **Don't let "which investor offers better terms" dominate a decision that's really about
  who a founder can work with under real stress for a decade or more.** Redirect toward the
  respect/learning question.
- **Don't accept an NDA request from an investor at face value, or ask a founder to consider
  signing one before a first conversation.** Conway is explicit this signals a lack of trust
  at the very start of a relationship that depends on trust.
- **Don't let "no traction yet" be treated as an automatic disqualifier from raising.** It
  changes what the raise is based on (team, track record) — it doesn't make raising impossible,
  especially outside consumer/web categories.
