## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder believes sales requires experience or charisma they
don't have, can't get prospects to take a call, is tempted to build a feature because one
prospect asked for it, is about to offer a free trial to close a hesitant customer, or
realizes their sales process doesn't match what their price point can actually support.
Bosmeny's central claim, from his own experience building sales at Clever with no prior
background in it: founder passion and industry knowledge trump sales experience, and most of
what looks like sales mystique is actually a small number of concrete, learnable habits.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I don't have sales experience — how am I supposed to sell this?"
- "Nobody will even take my call"
- "A prospect says they'll buy if we just add this one feature"
- "A customer wants a free trial before committing — should I give them one?"
- "Our sales cycle looks like an enterprise deal but we're charging $50/month"

This is not the right skill for pitching investors specifically — see
`fundraising/pitching-seibel-caldwell.md`, from the same lecture but a different half with
different speakers. For the broader question of what investors actually look for, see
`fundraising/what-investors-want-andreessen-conway.md`.

## Assumptions

1. **There's a real product to sell, even an early one.** This is about the mechanics of
   selling it, not validating whether it should exist.
2. **The founder (not a hired salesperson) is doing this personally**, at least at this
   stage — Bosmeny is explicit that a founder should dedicate real, focused time to this
   themselves rather than deferring it until "the sales people" are hired.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Bosmeny states specific,
   sometimes counterintuitive claims (a "no" is better than a "maybe"; never offer a free
   trial) from his own documented experience closing Clever's early deals.

## Insights

> "A lot of founders say, 'We're just going to build a great product, and when it's finally
> finished we're going to hire the sales people.' The reality is that as a founder, when it
> comes to 'hiring the sales people,' it's you. Talking to your users — that's selling. Your
> passion for the product and your knowledge of the industry actually trump sales experience."
— Tyler Bosmeny, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: Bosmeny's own path — he'd never done sales before joining an early-stage startup to
do exactly that, and later built Clever's early growth on the same skill, dedicating himself
full-time to it as one of the technical cofounders' complement.

> "Only about 2.5% of companies are 'innovators' who'll even consider buying from a startup
> with no users and no revenue. That might sound discouraging, but I found it the opposite —
> it tells you this is a numbers game. In our first two months at YC, I reached out to over
> 400 companies just to get a call."
— Tyler Bosmeny, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014, citing Everett Rogers's technology adoption curve

Context: Reframing a low response rate from a personal failure into an expected, quantifiable
fact about the market — the fix isn't a better pitch, it's more volume against a known-small
target segment.

> "If you take only one thing away from this: when you get someone on the phone, shut up. The
> best salespeople I've seen aim to talk only 30% of the call. They ask things like 'why did
> you even agree to take my call today,' or 'how do you solve this problem today?' They're not
> pitching — they're trying to understand the problem better than the prospect does."
— Tyler Bosmeny, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: A direct correction of the instinct every proud founder has — wanting to explain
every feature — replaced with a specific, measurable target (roughly 30/70 talk ratio) and
concrete question prompts.

> "This was the actual follow-up sequence for Clever's second deal ever — dozens of steps,
> including emails that went unanswered two and three times in a row, from someone who
> eventually bought. You need an almost unhuman willingness to follow up. But qualify that:
> your time is your only resource. Get people to a yes or a no as fast as possible — a 'no' is
> better than a 'maybe,' because maybes are what actually kill you."
— Tyler Bosmeny, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: Two claims that sound contradictory but aren't — follow up far more persistently than
feels natural, while also actively working to eliminate ambiguous non-answers rather than
letting a pipeline fill up with people who never quite say no.

> "Redlining is simple — don't be intimidated by it. But so many smart people spend weeks
> quibbling over minor indemnification clauses out of pride. Your goal is signed deals,
> reference customers, and revenue. If you don't get there, your startup is toast — remember
> that before you spend a month on a clause that doesn't matter."
— Tyler Bosmeny, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: A direct warning against a specific, common failure mode — treating contract
negotiation as a matter of principle or pride instead of staying focused on what actually
determines the company's survival.

> "When someone says 'I'd use your product but it's missing this one feature,' treat that as
> a pass in your head. Nine times out of ten, if you build it, there'll be one more feature or
> reason next time. Either get it in writing as part of a signed agreement, or wait until
> multiple customers independently ask for the same thing before building it."
— Tyler Bosmeny, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: A specific pattern-match against a trap ambitious founders fall into repeatedly —
treating a single person's feature request as a real, resolvable objection rather than a
polite way of saying no.

> "Free trials get you none of the things you actually need: revenue, validation, real
> commitment. You do all this work and end up having to sell them all over again at the end.
> Instead: 'We don't do free trials, we do annual agreements — but if you're not happy in the
> first 30 or 60 days, you can opt out.' That small change makes a night-and-day difference."
— Tyler Bosmeny, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: Reframing the same underlying comfort a customer wants (low risk in trying something
new) into a structure that still produces a real signed commitment, rather than a trial that
produces neither revenue nor a reliable signal of intent.

> "Christoph Janz's five ways to build a $100M company: a thousand customers at $100K, ten
> thousand at $10K, a hundred thousand at $1K, and so on. If you're trying to be a 'rabbit'
> selling a $1,000/year product but your sales cycle involves flying out, eight demos, and
> three months of redlining, you need to rethink something — that sales motion only works for
> 'elephant' deals."
— Tyler Bosmeny, CS183B Lecture 19, 2014

Context: A direct diagnostic for a specific, common mismatch — the sales process has to be
economically viable at the actual price point, and a high-touch process at a low price point
is a sign something about the pricing or the process needs to change, not a fixed cost to
absorb.

## Principles

1. **As a founder, you are the sales team, at least until real traction exists.** Passion for
   the product and depth of industry knowledge substitute for formal sales experience.
2. **Prospecting is a numbers game against a small, quantifiable slice of the market.** A low
   response rate at this stage is expected, not a signal something is wrong with the pitch.
3. **Talk less, listen more on calls.** The goal is understanding the prospect's problem more
   deeply than they've articulated it themselves, not explaining every feature.
4. **Follow up far more persistently than feels natural or comfortable** — but simultaneously
   push every open conversation toward a real yes or no. Ambiguous "maybes" are the actual
   threat to a pipeline, not a fast, clear no.
5. **Don't let contract redlining become a matter of pride.** Stay focused on what actually
   determines survival — signed deals and revenue — not winning every clause.
6. **Treat a single prospect's "I'd buy if you added this feature" as a likely pass**, not a
   real, resolvable objection — unless it's written into a signed agreement or multiple
   independent customers ask for the same thing.
7. **Never offer a free trial as the default close.** Convert the same underlying need (low
   perceived risk) into a real commitment with an opt-out window instead — an annual agreement
   with a 30-60 day escape clause, not a trial with no commitment at all.
8. **Deliberately unscalable, manual sales tactics are appropriate and expected in the earliest
   stage.** The discipline is noticing what becomes repeatable once you've done enough of it
   manually, and only then systematizing it.
9. **Match your sales motion to your price point.** A high-touch, long-cycle process only
   makes economic sense for high-price ("elephant") deals; low-price ("rabbit" or "mouse")
   products need a fundamentally lighter-touch process or the unit economics won't work.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Talk 30%, listen 70%" is a useful target ratio, not a literal, universal rule for every
  call.** Some calls (a prospect who's already sold and just needs specifics) legitimately
  need more explanation from you; the underlying discipline is defaulting to curiosity about
  the prospect's problem rather than reflexively pitching, not hitting an exact percentage
  every time.
- **"Unhuman follow-up" needs the explicit qualifier Bosmeny gives right after it — get people
  to yes or no fast.** Applied without that qualifier, relentless follow-up on someone who's
  genuinely not going to buy just burns time that should go toward prospects who might.
- **"Treat a feature request as a pass" is a strong heuristic against one very specific
  failure mode (chasing a single prospect's evolving goalposts), not advice to ignore feature
  requests generally.** This connects directly to the same underlying pattern in
  `product/mvp-and-users-cheung.md`'s Frankenstein warning and
  `user-research/user-interviews-shear.md`'s feature-validation trap — the point is digging
  into the real signal, not dismissing all requests as noise.
- **"Never do free trials" is Bosmeny's own strong stance from his specific business (B2B
  software sold to schools), not a universal law.** Some categories (certain consumer
  products, some developer tools) have free trials or freemium structures as a structurally
  central part of the model — the transferable principle is "make sure whatever you offer
  produces a real signal of commitment," not "trials are always wrong."
- **The elephant/rabbit pricing-tier framework is a useful diagnostic for mismatch, not a
  claim that a company must permanently commit to one tier.** Companies can and do move
  between tiers over time (adding an enterprise tier on top of a self-serve one, for example)
  — the point is that the sales motion has to match the tier being sold *right now*, not that
  the tier itself is fixed forever.

## Thinking Framework

**Believing sales requires experience the founder doesn't have**
Ask: "How deep is your knowledge of this specific industry and problem, and how much do you
actually care about it?" Redirect directly: those two things, per Bosmeny, trump formal sales
experience. Then ask what's actually been tried — often the real gap is volume of outreach,
not skill.

---

**Can't get anyone to take a call**
Ask: "How many people have you actually reached out to?" If the number is small: name the
2.5%-innovator math directly — this stage is a numbers game, and a low response rate at low
volume isn't yet a real signal. Recommend a specific volume target and the three channels
(personal network, conferences with an attendee list worked in advance, short cold email)
before concluding the message itself is wrong.

---

**Tempted to build a feature because a prospect asked for it**
Ask: "Is this one prospect, or have multiple independent customers asked for the same thing?"
If it's one: name the pass-in-disguise pattern directly. If they really want to pursue it:
recommend getting it into a signed agreement first, not building speculatively and hoping the
prospect follows through.

---

**Considering a free trial**
Ask: "What signal will you actually have at the end of the trial that you don't have right
now?" If the honest answer is "not much" — recommend the annual-agreement-with-opt-out
alternative directly, and explain why it produces the same comfort for the customer with a
real commitment attached.

---

**Sales cycle feels heavy relative to price point**
Ask: "What's your price point, and does your current sales process — number of calls, demos,
redlining rounds — make sense at that price economically?" Use the elephant/rabbit framework
directly to name the mismatch if one exists, and ask whether the fix is a lighter sales motion
or a different pricing tier.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "What's the specific sales question
right now — getting calls at all, handling a call, a feature request, a free-trial ask, or
your pricing-to-sales-motion fit?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes building
a custom feature for one prospect who's promised to buy once it's done, say so directly:
"That's very likely a pass in disguise — get it in a signed agreement before you build it."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't validate "we'll hire salespeople later" as a real plan for early-stage sales.**
  Redirect to founder-led sales directly.
- **Don't accept a low response rate as proof the pitch or product is wrong before checking
  the actual volume of outreach.** Push for the numbers-game framing first.
- **Don't let a founder dominate a sales call explaining features.** Redirect toward
  listening and specific diagnostic questions.
- **Don't let contract negotiation stall over minor clauses out of principle.** Redirect to
  what actually matters: signed deals and revenue.
- **Don't recommend building a one-off feature for a single prospect's stated blocker.** Push
  for a signed commitment first, or evidence from multiple independent customers.
- **Don't recommend a free trial as the default way to lower a customer's resistance.**
  Recommend the paid-agreement-with-opt-out alternative instead.
