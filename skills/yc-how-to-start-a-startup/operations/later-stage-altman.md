## Overview

This skill is relevant once a founder is approaching or past product-market fit and starting
to feel the structural strain of growth — usually somewhere between 20 and 50 employees, or
12-24 months in. Altman is explicit that everything in this lecture is intentionally *not*
early-stage advice: it's a checklist to write down and revisit later, covering the transition
every founder eventually has to make from "build a great product" to "build a great company."

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "We just hit 25 people and things that used to work have started falling apart"
- "I keep working 18-hour days doing the job myself instead of hiring for it"
- "How should we set up equity/compensation so it doesn't become a mess later?"
- "I'm exhausted and start to wonder if I even want to keep doing this"
- "We're negotiating a partnership/BD deal and I don't know how to get good terms"
- "Should I take this acquisition conversation seriously?"

This is not the right skill for pre-product-market-fit decisions like idea validation, MVP
scope, or early growth tactics — see `product/mvp-and-users-cheung.md` and
`growth/growth-mechanics-schultz.md`. For day-to-day operating mechanics once a manager layer
exists, see `operations/how-to-operate-rabois.md`; for specific people decisions (firing,
raises, equity for an individual), see `operations/how-to-manage-horowitz.md`.

## Assumptions

1. **The company has, or is close to, product-market fit** — this lecture is explicitly a
   post-PMF checklist, not early-stage guidance. Applying it too early (e.g., building formal
   management structure at 10 employees) risks the exact overcomplication Altman warns against.
2. **There's already at least a small team beyond the founders** — most of the content assumes
   hiring has started and is accelerating.
3. **The founder wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Altman states specific,
   sometimes counterintuitive rules (give away more equity than investors want, hire a
   full-time fundraiser, never hire a professional CEO) and expects the listener to act on them
   once the situation matches.

## Insights

> "The thing that tricks people is that when lack of structure fails, it fails all at once. So
> what works totally fine at 20 employees is disastrous at 30. All you need is for every
> employee to know who their manager is, and there should be exactly one, and every manager
> should know who their direct reports are. Clarity and simplicity are the most important
> things here."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014

Context: Naming the specific failure pattern — flat structure isn't gradually strained, it
collapses suddenly at a threshold — and giving the minimum viable fix (one manager per person,
clear reporting lines) rather than a complex reorg.

> "Before product-market fit your only job that matters is to build a great product. As the
> company grows, at about 25 or so employees, your main job shifts from building a great
> product to building a great company, and it stays there for the rest of your time. This is
> probably the biggest shift in being a founder that ever happens."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014

Context: The organizing claim of the whole lecture — everything else (HR, alignment,
productivity systems) is a consequence of this one job change, which most founders don't see
coming.

> "The four failure cases: being afraid to hire senior people — almost every founder says 'I
> wish I'd done that earlier' after their first great executive hire. Hero mode — trying to
> lead by example by working 18, then 19, then 20 hours a day instead of hiring, until you
> burn out. Bad delegation — 'go research it, come back with the data, I'll decide' doesn't
> scale; 'here's what I think, but you decide' does. And personal disorganization — not
> tracking what everyone is doing will come back to bite you."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014

Context: Four specific, checkable failure modes rather than generic "learn to manage" advice —
each is named with the exact wrong version and the exact right version side by side.

> "Writing down how you do things, and why you do things — these are really important. As you
> get bigger you can't just tell every new hire over lunch. If you don't write it down, someone
> else is going to say it instead, and it becomes random oral tradition. If you write it down,
> you as the founder get to basically write the law."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014

Context: A direct claim about leverage — codifying practices and values in writing is one of
the highest-leverage things a founder can do precisely because the alternative (unwritten
culture) doesn't stay silent, it gets filled in by whoever talks to a new hire first.

> "I think you should continue to give a lot of equity all the way through, not just to early
> employees. This dilutes you and the investors equally. Investors are very shortsighted and
> will fight you over every equity grant — but the data at YC shows the most successful
> companies, and the ones where investors do best, are the ones that keep giving out stock
> year after year. Think about giving out 3 to 5% of the company every year for the next ten
> years."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014

Context: A specific, counterintuitive number (3-5% per year, for a decade) presented as a place
where investor incentive and company incentive genuinely diverge — investors resist dilution
per-grant even though the data says continued generous grants correlate with the best outcomes
for everyone including them.

> "You never want an employee three years into a four-year vest starting to think about
> leaving. Stay in front of people's vesting schedules with refresher grants. I personally like
> six-year vesting because these companies take a while to build. Whatever you decide, get an
> option management system in place around this point — I've seen mistakes with an Excel
> spreadsheet cost employees or companies tens of millions of dollars."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014

Context: A concrete warning about a specific, easy-to-overlook operational failure (informal
equity tracking) with a real stated cost, plus a specific structural fix (proactive refresher
grants ahead of any vesting cliff, not reactive to a resignation).

> "The productivity of a company goes down with the square of the number of employees if you
> don't make an effort, because every pair of people adds communication overhead. The single
> word that matters most to keep a company productive as it grows is alignment. A classic test:
> if I polled ten random employees on the top three company goals right now, would they all say
> the same thing? A hundred percent of the time the founder says yes — and a hundred percent of
> the time, no two employees even list the same three goals in the same order."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014

Context: A specific diagnostic test founders can actually run themselves, paired with Altman's
observation that founders are reliably, universally wrong about whether their message has
actually landed across the company — reiteration has to be constant, not a one-time
announcement.

> "Once your product is working, switch from not caring about press to caring about it a
> little. Two things matter: figure out your key messaging yourselves, never outsource it to a
> PR firm — it's very hard to change once the press decides how to talk about you. And get to
> know three or four key journalists yourself. No journalist wants to talk to a PR flack; they
> want to hear from a founder. The biggest PR hack is to not hire a PR firm."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014

Context: A specific, counterintuitive recommendation (skip the PR firm entirely) grounded in
what actually gets journalist attention — a close relationship with a small number of
journalists over a mass blast to hundreds who never read it.

> "Five points on business development and deals: build a great product first, nothing else
> matters without it. Develop a real personal connection with anyone you're doing a big deal
> with — don't treat them as a one-off transaction. Create competitive dynamics; the way you
> get good terms is having another option. Persist beyond your comfort point. And ask for what
> you want — most of the time you won't get laughed out of the room, and you might just get it."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014

Context: A crash course explicitly framed as the minimum viable model for negotiating any deal
(fundraising, partnerships, BD, sales) — five specific, ordered principles rather than a general
appeal to "negotiate well."

> "Don't start any acquisition conversation unless you're willing to sell for a pretty low
> number. Don't ever check just hoping for the one miracle high offer — if that's going to
> happen, you'll know, because they'll make you a big offer before you can even meet them. This
> is one of the biggest killers of companies: founders get distracted, get demoralized if it
> doesn't happen, and if a low offer does come in, they've already mentally checked out and take
> it."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014

Context: A specific behavioral trap named directly — the danger isn't the acquisition offer
itself, it's the psychological effect of engaging with the conversation at all, which
pre-disposes founders to accept a bad outcome even when the deal wasn't real to begin with.

> "The thing that kills startups at some level is founders giving up. Sometimes you should
> quit. But if you mismanage your own psychology and quit when you shouldn't, that's what kills
> most of these startups — that is the final, closest death in most cases."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014

Context: Altman's closing framing on founder psychology — distinguishing between legitimate,
well-reasoned quitting and psychologically-driven quitting, and naming the latter as the
dominant real cause of failure rather than external competitive or market forces.

> "If you can wait to raise any outside capital until things are working, or at least pointing
> in the direction of working, you're way better off. Once you've raised money you can't stay
> in an exploratory phase indefinitely — you end up rushing and pivoting to whatever the first
> vaguely plausible idea is. That's bad."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 20, 2014, from the closing Q&A

Context: A direct answer to "when should founders raise a seed fund" — naming the specific
mechanism (raised capital creates time pressure that forecloses genuine exploration) rather
than a general "raise when you need to."

## Principles

1. **Structure fails suddenly, not gradually — put a minimal, clear reporting structure (one
   manager per person) in place before you hit roughly 20-25 employees**, not after it breaks.
2. **The founder's job changes fundamentally at this stage, from building a great product to
   building a great company.** Expect this shift and plan for it rather than resisting it.
3. **Hire senior executives earlier than feels comfortable** — the regret is almost always "I
   waited too long," not the reverse.
4. **Get out of hero mode by explicitly trading short-term throughput for hiring**, rather than
   working more hours until burnout.
5. **Delegate by trusting someone's judgment on the decision itself, not just the research for
   it.** "You decide" scales; "bring me the data so I can decide" does not.
6. **Write down how you do things and why you do things while the company is still small
   enough that you control the message.** Unwritten culture doesn't stay unwritten — it gets
   filled in by whoever talks to the new hire first.
7. **Keep giving employees significant equity for the life of the company, even against
   investor resistance** — sustained generosity here correlates with the best outcomes for
   founders, employees, and investors alike.
8. **Get a real option-management system and proactive refresher-grant plan in place before
   anyone's cliff is a live retention risk**, not after someone starts job-hunting.
9. **Alignment, not raw effort, is what keeps a growing company productive.** Test it directly
   by asking multiple employees to state the top company goals — don't assume a single
   announcement created lasting alignment.
10. **Handle back-office mechanics (accounting, legal document collection, provisional
    patents, trademarks, FP&A) proactively once things are working**, because every one of them
    gets dramatically harder to fix mid-crisis (a lease dispute, a financing round, a missed
    patent window) than to set up calmly in advance.
11. **Manage your own psychology explicitly as the company scales** — the highs and lows both
    intensify, external criticism increases with success, and the dominant real cause of
    startup failure is a founder quitting when they shouldn't, not external competition.
12. **Do your own PR: set your own messaging and build direct relationships with a handful of
    journalists, rather than outsourcing to a PR firm.**
13. **In any deal or BD relationship, build a real personal connection, create competitive
    alternatives, persist past your comfort point, and explicitly ask for what you want.**
14. **Treat acquisition conversations as a distraction unless you're genuinely willing to sell
    at a low number** — engaging at all creates a psychological trap that predisposes founders
    toward a bad outcome.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Give away 3-5% equity per year for a decade" is directional, not a literal formula for
  every company.** The point is that sustained generosity correlates with better outcomes than
  investor-driven stinginess — the exact percentage depends on company size, growth rate, and
  what's actually needed to retain and motivate the team.
- **"Never hire a professional CEO" is Altman's strong, stated opinion from what he's observed
  at YC-scale outcomes — it is explicitly not a universal law.** He names the exception himself
  (there are cases where it works) and frames it as "if you don't want to be the long-term CEO,
  you probably shouldn't start the company," which is a claim about founder commitment, not a
  ban on ever bringing in outside executive leadership for specific functions.
- **The "hire senior executives earlier than feels comfortable" principle is specifically about
  the post-PMF, scaling stage — it does not contradict the standard early-stage advice
  (elsewhere in this course) that senior hires are usually a mistake before then.** Applying
  this too early risks over-hiring for a company that hasn't found its shape yet.
- **The management-structure minimalism ("just make sure everyone has exactly one manager")
  is calibrated to the 20-50 employee range Altman is describing.** It is a floor, not a
  ceiling — companies well past this stage will legitimately need more structure than "one
  manager per person," and that's a different, later problem than the one this lecture solves.
- **"Don't hire a PR firm" is framed as the biggest PR hack, but it assumes the founder actually
  has the time and inclination to build direct journalist relationships.** A founder who
  genuinely cannot make time for this is choosing between two real costs (no press relationships
  vs. founder time), not getting a free lunch by skipping the PR firm.
- **The acquisition-conversation caution is about psychological self-protection, not a claim
  that acquisitions are always bad.** Some acquisitions are the right outcome — the warning is
  specifically against entertaining conversations you're not prepared to accept at the actual
  offer level, not against acquisitions as an exit path in general.

## Thinking Framework

**Hitting structural strain around 20-50 employees**
Ask: "Does every single person know who their one manager is, and does every manager know
their direct reports?" If not, that's the fix — not a complex reorg, not "no structure at all."
Then check for the opposite failure: is anyone reporting to more than one person for different
things? Simplify back toward one clear line per person.

---

**Feeling exhausted, or working unsustainable hours to keep up**
Name it directly if it matches: "This sounds like hero mode — are you working longer hours
instead of hiring for this?" If yes, walk through the actual trade: what falls behind for two
or three weeks while you hire and ramp up help, versus continuing until burnout. The right
answer accepts short-term backlog; the wrong answer is more hours.

---

**Setting up equity, compensation, or vesting for the first time**
Ask where they currently stand: informal negotiated comp, or bands already in place? Push
toward comp bands and a real option-management system before informality becomes a source of
resentment or a costly tracking mistake. Separately, ask whether refresher grants are planned
proactively or only reactively (i.e., after someone raises leaving) — proactive is the goal.

---

**Company feels less productive despite more headcount**
Run Altman's test directly: "If you asked five random employees for the company's top three
goals right now, would they give the same three, in the same order?" Use whatever gap surfaces
to diagnose whether the problem is roadmap clarity, communication rhythm (weekly management
meetings, monthly all-hands), or values/decision-framework clarity — not effort.

---

**Negotiating a deal, partnership, or fundraising conversation**
Walk through the five BD principles in order: is the product actually strong enough to lead
with? Is there a real personal relationship with the other side, or is this transactional? Is
there a competitive alternative creating leverage? Is the founder pushing past their own
comfort point? And, specifically, have they actually asked for what they want, plainly?

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Locate the stage.**
If unclear, ask: "Are you before or after product-market fit, and roughly how many people are
on the team?" Most of this skill doesn't apply meaningfully before ~20 employees or before PMF
— redirect earlier-stage founders to the right skill instead of forcing this framework on them.

**Step 1: Identify which specific system is under strain** — management structure, HR/equity,
alignment/productivity, back-office mechanics, founder psychology, PR, or a live deal — and go
to that thread above.

**Step 2: State a plain view once there's enough context.** If a founder describes months of
18-hour days doing a job they should have hired for, say so directly: "That's hero mode, by
name — the fix isn't more hours, it's accepting a short backlog while you hire."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't let a founder "innovate" on management structure.** Altman is explicit that this is
  not the place to get creative — clarity and simplicity are what matter, not novelty.
- **Don't let bad delegation continue unnamed.** "Bring me the data, I'll decide" doesn't scale
  — push toward "you decide, I trust your judgment" instead.
- **Don't let equity or vesting policy stay informal past the point where the team is growing
  fast.** Informal negotiated equity becomes a fairness and retention problem, not a neutral
  default.
- **Don't accept a single all-hands announcement as proof the company is aligned.** Test it
  directly rather than trusting the founder's assumption that the message landed.
- **Don't let a founder engage seriously with an acquisition conversation unless they are
  genuinely willing to accept the actual number on the table.** The distraction and
  demoralization risk is real even if the deal never closes.
- **Don't let quitting decisions go unexamined.** Distinguish a well-reasoned decision to stop
  from a psychologically-driven one — the latter is what Altman names as the dominant real
  cause of startup failure.
