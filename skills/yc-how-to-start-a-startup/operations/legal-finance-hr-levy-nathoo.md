## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder is incorporating for the first time, splitting equity
with cofounders, evaluating a term sheet or a specific investor request, unsure what counts as
a legitimate business expense, deciding whether to pay themselves, or about to fire someone.
Nathoo and Levy are explicit that founders don't need to know the details of any of this — they
need the basics, so they avoid the specific, well-known mistakes that repeatedly cause real
companies real pain, and know when to bring in a specialist.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "Where and how should we actually incorporate?"
- "How should my cofounder and I split equity?"
- "An investor wants a board seat / advisor shares / pro-rata rights — is that normal?"
- "Can I use company money for this? Where's the line?"
- "Should I be paying myself a salary right now?"
- "I have to fire someone — what do I need to get right?"
- "My cofounder and I are breaking up — what happens to their equity?"

This is not the right skill for the mechanics of raising money as a persuasion/relationship
process (`fundraising/what-investors-want-andreessen-conway.md`) or for evaluating a candidate
before they're hired (`hiring/team-and-cofounders-altman.md`) — this is specifically about the
legal and financial mechanics once decisions are made.

## Assumptions

1. **The founder needs the basics to avoid common, well-documented mistakes — not a
   substitute for an actual lawyer, accountant, or payroll service.** Nathoo and Levy are
   explicit about this distinction throughout.
2. **The company is US-based and likely aiming for the standard venture path** (Delaware C-corp,
   priced or unpriced financing rounds). Some specifics won't transfer directly to other
   jurisdictions or non-venture-backed structures.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Nathoo and Levy state specific,
   sometimes blunt conclusions ("don't spend a dime" trying to get fancy with incorporation;
   "don't go to Vegas on investor money") backed by real failure stories from YC companies.

## Insights

> "A company was originally formed as an LLC in Connecticut on a lawyer friend's advice. When
> they came to YC, we said convert to Delaware. The conversion paperwork had a simple but
> crucial mistake — the company thought it was a Delaware corporation for years but was still a
> Connecticut LLC. It took four different law firms to untangle when it surfaced during a big
> fundraise. The bill: five hundred thousand dollars."
— Carolynn Levy, CS183B Lecture 18, 2014

Context: The concrete cost of not keeping formation simple and standard — the mistake wasn't
exotic, it was a basic paperwork error that went undetected for years because nobody checked.

> "Execution has greater value than the idea. Who's ever heard of a billion-dollar payment for
> just an idea? Resist giving disproportionate equity to whoever's credited with coming up with
> the idea. In the top YC companies by valuation, there are zero instances of a significantly
> disproportionate founder equity split."
— Carolynn Levy, CS183B Lecture 18, 2014

Context: A direct, data-backed argument against weighting equity toward "whoever thought of
it" — the data point (zero disproportionate splits among the highest-valuation YC companies)
is offered as the actual evidence, not just a philosophical preference.

> "When ownership between cofounders is disproportionate, that's a red flag for us — we wonder
> what conversation isn't happening. Is one founder secretly thinking this is temporary? Have
> they been honest with each other about expectations for the future? If everyone's in it 100%
> for the long haul, what happened before the company existed shouldn't matter."
— Carolynn Levy, CS183B Lecture 18, 2014

Context: An unequal split is treated as a symptom worth investigating, not just a cap-table
detail — it often reflects an unresolved, unspoken difference in commitment level between
cofounders.

> "There's one piece of paper you cannot go back and fix if you miss it: the 83(b) election.
> We've seen deals blow up because a founder didn't file it. Sign the stock purchase agreement,
> sign the 83(b) election, and keep proof you actually mailed it — without proof, it just goes
> into a black hole at the IRS, and investors or acquirers will walk away from a deal over this."
— Kirsty Nathoo, CS183B Lecture 18, 2014

Context: A specific, narrow, irreversible paperwork step singled out because it's both easy to
overlook and impossible to correct after the fact — one of the few "this will actually kill a
deal" details in the whole lecture.

> "The standard vesting period is four years with a one-year cliff — even for solo founders.
> The reason isn't just protecting cofounders from someone walking away with a huge equity
> chunk. It's skin in the game, and it sets the tone for employees: you can't credibly tell an
> employee they need four-year vesting if you don't vest your own shares the same way."
— Carolynn Levy, CS183B Lecture 18, 2014

Context: Vesting is framed as serving three distinct purposes at once — fairness among
cofounders if someone leaves, an incentive to keep working through the hard parts, and a
cultural example that makes asking the same of employees credible.

> "As a founder, you have a burden to understand what you're agreeing to — 'I didn't know what
> I was signing' isn't a defense. Four investor requests to know cold: board seats (usually say
> no, unless the person adds real strategic value, not just money), advisor equity (an investor
> who already invested shouldn't need extra shares to also help you), pro-rata rights (they
> protect the investor from dilution — the corollary is founders absorb more of it), and
> information rights (monthly updates are reasonable; weekly budgets are overreach)."
— Carolynn Levy, CS183B Lecture 18, 2014

Context: The through-line across all four: agreeing to a valuation doesn't mean everything
else in a term sheet is unimportant — each of these terms has real, specific consequences a
founder needs to understand before signing, not just accept as boilerplate.

> "Understand your future dilution before you get excited about easy money. Raise $2M on SAFEs
> with a $6M cap, and when they convert, those early investors own about 25% — on top of new
> investors wanting 20% in the priced round. You've given away 45% already. That might still be
> the right call — some money on a low cap beats no money at all — but know where this leads."
— Kirsty Nathoo, CS183B Lecture 18, 2014

Context: A direct corrective against treating easy-to-raise SAFE money as costless just because
it's fast and simple — the dilution math compounds in ways that are easy to lose track of in
the moment.

> "Ask yourself: if an investor asked me for a line-by-line breakdown of what I spent their
> money on, would I be embarrassed by any line? If yes, it's probably not a business expense.
> We had a founder who took investor money to Vegas — by his Facebook photos, he had a great
> time. He's no longer with the company. That's stealing from investors."
— Kirsty Nathoo, CS183B Lecture 18, 2014

Context: A simple, practical test for the blurry cases (especially early on, working out of an
apartment) between what's a legitimate business expense and what isn't — paired with the
starkest possible negative example.

> "Founders have to be paid — working for free is against the law, and founders shouldn't let
> their company take on that liability. Unpaid wages become leverage in a founder breakup: the
> fired founder's lawyer points out the law was broken, and now the remaining founders feel
> pressure to grant vesting acceleration they wouldn't otherwise owe, just to make it go away.
> Think of cofounder wages like a marital prenup."
— Carolynn Levy, CS183B Lecture 18, 2014

Context: The connection between an operational detail (are founders on payroll) and a much
higher-stakes future risk (a founder breakup) — unpaid wages don't just risk a legal violation,
they create specific leverage that gets used against the remaining founders later.

> "Best practices for firing: fire quickly — don't let a bad employee linger. Communicate
> directly, no equivocating, no apologizing — 'we're letting you go,' not a rationalized
> explanation. Pay all wages and accrued vacation immediately, no negotiation. Cut off digital
> access immediately — we had a founder who held the company's GitHub password hostage when his
> cofounders tried to fire him. Repurchase any vested shares right away."
— Carolynn Levy, CS183B Lecture 18, 2014

Context: Five concrete, specific practices, each with a real failure mode attached — the
GitHub example in particular shows why "cut off access immediately" isn't a formality but a
real, sometimes urgent risk.

## Principles

1. **Keep formation simple and standard — Delaware C-corp, standard documents, no
   customization for its own sake.** Getting fancy costs real money when something goes
   wrong, and the standard path is standard because it's well-tested and familiar to investors.
2. **Keep every signed legal document organized and accessible.** The moments you'll need
   them (a big raise, an acquisition) are already high-stress; missing paperwork multiplies
   that stress at the worst possible time.
3. **Execution, not the idea, deserves the equity weight — near-equal founder splits are the
   norm among the most successful companies, not a compromise.** A significantly
   disproportionate split is itself a signal worth investigating, not just a number to negotiate.
4. **Sign the actual paperwork — talking about equity isn't the same as owning it.** The stock
   purchase agreement and 83(b) election specifically are not optional formalities; the 83(b)
   election is irreversible if missed.
5. **Vesting (standard: 4 years, 1-year cliff) applies to every founder, including solo
   founders.** It protects remaining cofounders, creates real incentive to stay through the
   hard parts, and sets the credible standard for asking the same of employees.
6. **Understand every term in a financing document, not just the valuation.** Board seats,
   advisor equity, pro-rata rights, and information rights each carry specific, real
   consequences — "I didn't know what I signed" isn't a valid excuse after the fact.
7. **Track future dilution before getting excited about how easy money is to raise.**
   Unpriced-round money (SAFEs) is fast and simple, but the eventual conversion math compounds
   with the next priced round in ways worth modeling out in advance.
8. **Separate company money from personal spending with a clear test: would you be embarrassed
   explaining this specific expense to an investor line by line?** If yes, it's probably not a
   legitimate business expense.
9. **Founders must be paid, at least minimum wage, and on proper payroll with taxes withheld.**
   Skipping this creates both a real legal liability and specific leverage an unhappy cofounder
   can use against the remaining team later.
10. **Fire quickly, directly, and completely — no lingering, no equivocating, and no delay on
    final wages, digital access revocation, or share repurchase.** Each of these has a specific
    real failure mode when done slowly or incompletely.
11. **Know your basic financial position at all times — cash position, burn rate, and runway.**
    This isn't optional bookkeeping; it's what lets a founder have an honest conversation with
    investors before a crisis, not during one.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **This is explicitly "the basics to avoid pain," not comprehensive legal or financial
  advice.** Nathoo and Levy are direct that founders shouldn't try to master the details
  themselves — the goal is knowing enough to avoid the well-known traps and to recognize when
  a real specialist (lawyer, CPA, payroll service) is needed, not to self-serve every legal or
  financial decision.
- **The specific numbers and instruments (SAFE valuation caps, the 83(b) election, standard
  vesting terms) reflect US startup practice circa 2014.** The underlying principles — track
  dilution, understand what you sign, keep paperwork organized — are durable; specific
  mechanics, thresholds, and even instrument names should be checked against current practice
  and jurisdiction, not assumed unchanged.
- **"Near-equal founder equity splits are the norm" is a strong empirical claim from YC's own
  portfolio, not a claim that unequal splits are never justified.** A genuinely unequal
  contribution profile (one founder joining significantly later, with a different scope of
  responsibility) can warrant a different split — the point is that disproportion should come
  from a real, discussed reason, not from crediting whoever had the original idea.
- **"Investors should be sophisticated/accredited" is a real, useful heuristic against
  informal money from friends and family who don't understand long-term illiquidity — it's
  not a claim that all non-institutional money is automatically bad**, only that the specific
  failure pattern (an investor wanting their money back because they need it for something
  else) is common enough with unsophisticated investors to actively screen for.
- **The firing best-practices list is about mechanics and directness, not about skipping real
  care for the person being let go.** "Don't apologize" and "communicate directly" are about
  avoiding equivocation and false hope, not license to be needlessly harsh — compare directly
  with `operations/how-to-manage-horowitz.md`'s point about preserving someone's dignity even
  while being honest and direct.

## Thinking Framework

**Incorporating for the first time**
Ask: "Is there a specific reason you're considering anything other than a standard Delaware
C-corp with standard documents?" If not: recommend the standard path directly, and recommend a
service built for this (the lecture names Clerky) over ad hoc lawyer arrangements, specifically
to avoid the kind of conversion mistake that cost one real company $500,000.

---

**Splitting equity with cofounders**
Ask: "Is the split close to equal, and if not, what's the specific, discussed reason?" If it's
disproportionate based mainly on "who had the idea": name that directly as the exact pattern
Levy warns against. If there's a real, substantive difference in ongoing commitment or role:
that's a more legitimate basis — but confirm it's been explicitly discussed, not assumed.

---

**Evaluating an investor's specific request (board seat, advisor equity, pro-rata, information
rights)**
Ask which of the four it is, and go through the specific tradeoff directly: board seats
usually warrant a no unless there's real added value beyond money; advisor equity on top of an
investment is often just a freebie ask; pro-rata rights are common but come with a real
dilution cost to founders; information rights are fine at a monthly cadence but a red flag if
the ask becomes weekly budgets or granular control.

---

**Unsure if something is a legitimate business expense**
Ask directly: "If an investor asked for a line-by-line breakdown, would this specific expense
embarrass you?" Use this as the practical test rather than a more abstract categorization
exercise.

---

**Deciding whether to pay yourself**
Don't treat this as optional. State directly: minimum wage, on real payroll, with taxes
withheld, is a legal requirement, not a founder preference — and skipping it creates leverage
an unhappy cofounder can use later, independent of the immediate legal risk.

---

**About to fire someone**
Walk through the five practices directly, in order: fire quickly rather than letting it
linger, communicate without equivocating or apologizing, pay all wages and accrued vacation
immediately, cut off digital and physical access immediately, and repurchase vested shares
right away. Flag the GitHub hostage story if there's any risk the person has sensitive system
access.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "What's the specific legal/finance/HR
question right now — incorporation, equity split, a specific investor term, business
expenses, founder pay, or firing someone?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes splitting
equity unevenly because one of them "had the idea," say so directly: "That's the exact pattern
that's a red flag in Levy's own data — worth having the more direct conversation about actual
commitment level instead."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't recommend a custom or unusual incorporation structure without a specific, real
  reason.** Default to standard Delaware C-corp practice.
- **Don't validate an equity split based on who originated the idea.** Redirect toward
  execution and ongoing commitment as the actual basis.
- **Don't let "we talked about the equity split" substitute for actually signing the paperwork**
  (stock purchase agreement, 83(b) election). Flag the irreversibility of a missed 83(b)
  specifically.
- **Don't wave off an investor's specific term-sheet request as unimportant just because the
  valuation is agreed.** Walk through what it actually costs or risks.
- **Don't treat "the founders are broke, we'll skip payroll for now" as a reasonable
  temporary fix.** Name the legal liability and the specific cofounder-breakup leverage risk it
  creates.
- **Don't recommend delaying or softening the mechanics of a firing** (final pay, access
  revocation, share repurchase) even when the conversation itself is being handled with care.
