## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder is deciding whether to intervene or delegate on a
specific call, confused about why adding headcount isn't producing proportional output,
setting up transparency or metrics practices, noticing the company's voice feels inconsistent
across surfaces, or promoting someone into management for the first time. Rabois's central
frame is that operating a company is fundamentally editing, not writing — your job is to
simplify, ask the right narrowing questions, allocate resources, and keep a consistent voice,
while doing far less of the actual work yourself than founders expect.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "Should I let this person do it their way, or step in?"
- "We hired five more engineers and somehow we're shipping slower, not faster"
- "How much should I be sharing internally — metrics, board decks, even compensation?"
- "I set a target for one team and now they're gaming it in a way that hurts something else"
- "Our website, our support, and our careers page all feel like they were written by different companies"
- "One of our best individual performers wants to become a manager — should they?"

This is not the right skill for hiring/evaluating specific candidates
(`hiring/team-and-cofounders-altman.md`, `hiring/scaling-teams-collison-silbermann.md`) or for
the focus/momentum side of execution (`operations/execution-altman.md`), which this skill's
editing framework complements directly.

## Assumptions

1. **There's already a real team and product** — this is about operating an existing company,
   not about the earliest idea-to-MVP stage.
2. **The founder has some latitude to change how they manage, delegate, or measure things** —
   several techniques (dashboards, transparency practices, one-on-one cadence) require real
   authority to implement.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Rabois states specific,
   sometimes counterintuitive claims (most hires are "ammunition," not "barrels"; don't share
   office space with other companies) from direct operating experience at three different
   companies.

## Insights

> "Forging a company is a lot harder than forging a product, because people are irrational.
> Building a company is basically taking all the irrational people you know, putting them in
> one building, and living with them twelve hours a day. Eventually you want a high-performance
> machine — as we joked at eBay, if the Martians took over, it would take six months for the
> world to notice."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: Rabois's framing for the whole talk — the goal of operating is to build something
that runs well independent of constant heroic effort, not to be perpetually necessary to hold
it together.

> "Andy Grove's definition of your job: maximize the output of the organization you're
> responsible for. Focus on output, not input — the old adage about measuring motion and
> confusing it with progress."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: The foundational metric Rabois returns to throughout — activity and effort are not
the thing to optimize; only actual output counts, however unglamorous the daily work of
achieving it looks (he lists ordering smoothies and training a receptionist as real examples).

> "The best metaphor for your job is editor, not writer. First, an editor simplifies — takes
> out the red pen and eliminates. Don't accept the excuse of complexity: you can build the most
> important companies in history with a very simple concept. Second, editors ask clarifying
> questions — narrow to the one, two, three things that matter most. Andy Grove estimated every
> step you eliminate can improve performance by 30-50%."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: The first two of four editing functions Rabois names — simplifying and narrowing —
framed as skills to practice and measure, not innate traits. He suggests tracking how much
"red ink" you're generating over time as a proxy for how well you're communicating what
matters.

> "Editors also allocate resources — moving a reporter from the Mideast desk to Silicon Valley
> because it's more interesting, or letting reporters pitch their own stories bottom-up. Your
> goal over time is to use less red ink every day; if this month's red ink is more than last
> month's, that's the problem to fix."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: The third editing function — resource allocation can be top-down (redirecting
people) or bottom-up (letting people propose their own initiatives) — paired with a concrete
way to measure whether your organization is internalizing priorities over time.

> "The fourth job is ensuring consistent voice — like The Economist, where every article feels
> written by the same person. Your website, PR, packaging, and recruiting pages should all feel
> like one voice. Even Apple under Steve Jobs never fully achieved this — ask anyone who worked
> there about the internal recruiting tools versus the actual products."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: The fourth editing function, with an explicit admission that even the company most
associated with obsessive consistency never achieved it completely — the goal is to get as
close as possible, not to expect perfection.

> "Task-relevant maturity: has this person done this exact task before? The more mature they
> are at it, the more rope you give them; the more novel it is for them, the more you instruct
> and monitor. Any CEO should not have one management style — your style should be dictated by
> the employee and the task. If half your reference checkers call you a micromanager and half
> say you give them lots of responsibility, that's a feature, not a bug."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: Rabois's answer to how to delegate without either abdicating or micromanaging — the
right style varies by person and by task, not by a fixed personal management philosophy.

> "Sort every decision on two axes: your level of conviction, and the consequence if you're
> wrong. Low consequence and low conviction — delegate completely, let people make mistakes and
> learn. High consequence and high conviction — you can't let a junior colleague make that
> mistake; explain your reasoning rather than just overruling."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: The conviction-by-consequence matrix, illustrated with a Square example (a marketing
hire's "Inner Square" idea Rabois doubted but let run because the consequence was low — the
hire learned something real even though the specific tactic didn't scale).

> "Most great people are actually ammunition. What you need are barrels — people who can take
> an idea from conception all the way to shipping and bring people with them. Hiring more
> engineers without more barrels doesn't get more done; sometimes it gets less done, because
> everyone still needs the same barrel's approval and attention."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: Rabois's explanation for why headcount growth often fails to produce proportional
output — velocity is bottlenecked by the number of people who can independently drive an idea
to completion, not by total headcount.

> "Peter Thiel insisted every person at PayPal do exactly one thing — enforced strictly, no
> exceptions. The insight: most people solve problems they already know how to solve — B+
> problems — instead of the high-impact, difficult A+ problem, because the A+ problem doesn't
> have an obvious solution and gets procrastinated. Multiple priorities cascade into a company
> that grows steadily but never produces a breakthrough."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: A direct, structural explanation for why spreading someone across several priorities
tends to produce safe, incremental progress rather than the harder breakthrough work —
illustrated with Thiel's famously strict one-thing-per-person policy.

> "Measure pairing indicators. Give a risk team the objective 'lower the fraud rate' alone, and
> they'll treat every customer as a suspect — you'll get the lowest fraud rate and the lowest
> satisfaction score in the world. Measure the false positive rate at the same time, and it
> forces real innovation instead of just being maximally restrictive."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: A concrete illustration of why a single metric, optimized in isolation, reliably
produces damage somewhere else — the fix is measuring the natural counterweight alongside it,
not trusting people to self-balance against an unstated tradeoff.

> "Look for anomalies, not expected behavior. At PayPal, none of the top-ten target markets
> included eBay — until someone noticed sellers were handwriting 'please pay with PayPal' into
> their listings. The first reaction was to shut it down as off-focus. David Sacks said, 'I
> think we found our market' — and built the HTML button, then automatic insertion, that became
> PayPal's actual success."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: The market that made PayPal wasn't the one it planned to target — it was discovered
by taking a strange, unexpected data point seriously instead of dismissing it as noise or a
distraction from the plan.

> "Bill Walsh's first move taking over the worst team in football wasn't a play strategy — he
> wrote a three-page memo on how the receptionist should answer the phone. If the organization
> does everything exactly right, receivers start running routes at 7 yards instead of 8. Get
> the details right everywhere, including ones users never see, like Steve Jobs insisting the
> Mac's circuit board be beautiful even though it could never be opened and seen by anyone."
— Keith Rabois, CS183B Lecture 14, 2014

Context: Rabois's closing argument, from Bill Walsh's *The Score Takes Care of Itself* — a
company that gets small, often-invisible details right compounds into overall performance
without needing to separately worry about the big outcome numbers.

## Principles

1. **Your job is editing, not writing — simplify, ask narrowing questions, allocate resources,
   and maintain consistent voice.** Complexity is rarely a valid excuse; almost anything can be
   distilled to a repeatable, simple framework.
2. **Focus on output, not activity.** Motion and progress are not the same thing, and measuring
   the wrong one rewards busyness over results.
3. **Delegation style should vary by person and by task (task-relevant maturity), not be one
   fixed personal management philosophy.** Being called both a micromanager and a delegator by
   different people is a sign you're calibrating correctly, not a contradiction to resolve.
4. **Sort decisions by your conviction and the consequence of being wrong.** Delegate
   completely on low-consequence, low-conviction calls; intervene directly (with explained
   reasoning) on high-consequence, high-conviction ones.
5. **Most hires are "ammunition"; a small number are "barrels" who can take an idea from
   conception to shipping.** Output scales with barrels, not headcount — adding ammunition
   without more barrels can reduce velocity, not increase it.
6. **Identify barrels by expanding responsibility until it breaks, and by watching who other
   employees seek out informally.** Both are observable signals, not guesses.
7. **Track individual growth rate against company growth rate to decide whether to promote,
   mentor, or replace someone in a role** — a role that made sense at one stage may not scale
   with the person, or the company, indefinitely.
8. **Limiting people to very few priorities (ideally one) counteracts the default tendency to
   solve familiar, safe problems instead of the highest-impact, hardest ones.** Multiple
   priorities cascade into steady but unremarkable progress.
9. **Every single-axis metric needs a paired counter-metric to prevent one-sided
   optimization.** Whatever you measure alone will be maximized at the expense of whatever you
   don't measure alongside it.
10. **Look for anomalies in data rather than dismissing them as noise or distraction.** Some of
    the most important discoveries (PayPal's actual core market) come from taking a strange,
    unplanned signal seriously instead of filtering it out.
11. **Invisible details compound into overall performance — obsess over them even where users
    or customers will never directly see them.** The mechanism isn't magic; it's that
    organization-wide precision habits transfer into the parts that do matter visibly.
12. **Transparency has levels (metrics access, board deck walkthroughs, meeting notes,
    physical openness, even compensation) — most companies stop at the minimum viable version,
    but the ceiling is much higher than most founders assume.**

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Only give people one thing to work on" is Thiel's strict, specific policy at one company
  — Rabois himself offers a softer version (three things) as more broadly workable.** Treat the
  strict version as a useful extreme to test your instincts against, not a rule every company
  must adopt exactly as PayPal did.
- **The barrels/ammunition framing can be misapplied to devalue genuinely skilled specialists
  who aren't end-to-end drivers.** Rabois's own framing is about ratio and bottleneck, not
  that "ammunition" people are lesser — a healthy company needs both, in roughly a 1:10-20
  barrel-to-ammunition range by his estimate, not zero ammunition.
- **Radical transparency examples (Stripe's email visibility, NeXT's banded compensation
  transparency) are presented as real, working options — not universally recommended
  defaults.** Rabois explicitly calls typical practice "minimal viable transparency" and
  argues the ceiling is higher, but every level of transparency carries real tradeoffs
  (information overload, social dynamics around visible pay) that need active management, not
  just adoption.
- **"Don't share office space with other companies" is tied to a specific claim about cult-like
  culture formation ("every good startup is a cult").** This is a strong, single-source opinion
  from one operator's experience — a founder with real resource constraints should weigh it
  against the actual cost tradeoff, not treat it as a non-negotiable rule.
- **"Promote the best individual performer, not the best people-manager" is explicitly
  Thiel's/PayPal's approach, which Rabois presents favorably but with the caveat that
  management is also a skill learned by doing, imperfectly, over time.** It doesn't mean
  management ability is irrelevant — it means starting credibility often comes from domain
  mastery, with management skill developing afterward.
- **The anomaly-hunting principle (PayPal's eBay discovery, LinkedIn's profile-vanity
  discovery) survives on true stories with real payoffs — not every anomaly is a hidden
  market.** The discipline is taking anomalies seriously enough to investigate, not treating
  every unexpected data point as automatically significant.

## Thinking Framework

**Deciding whether to intervene or delegate on a specific call**
Ask: "How much conviction do you actually have that this is wrong, and how bad is it if you're
wrong about that?" Walk through the matrix directly: low conviction + low consequence →
delegate fully. High conviction + high consequence → intervene, but explain your reasoning
rather than just overruling. If it's mixed (high consequence, low conviction; or low
consequence, high conviction): push for which axis actually dominates in this specific case.

---

**More hires, not more output**
Ask: "Of the people you've hired, how many can take an idea from conception all the way to
shipping and bring others with them — versus how many need someone else to drive that?" If
the ratio is heavily skewed toward the latter: name the barrels/ammunition pattern directly.
"You don't need more engineers yet — you need to find or develop more barrels first."

---

**Setting up transparency or metrics practices**
Ask: "What level of transparency do you have now, and is it a deliberate choice or just
whatever happened by default?" Push past "minimal viable transparency" toward a considered
choice about metrics access, board deck sharing, and meeting notes specifically. For metrics:
ask "what's the one thing this metric could get gamed at the expense of, and are you measuring
that too?"

---

**Company voice feels inconsistent**
Ask: "Have you actually compared your recruiting page, support experience, and core product
side by side?" Recommend a direct audit — Rabois's own diagnostic is literally comparing
surfaces for tone and word choice. Ask whether this was ever explicitly owned by one person
(often the founder early on) or has drifted by default across different writers.

---

**Promoting someone into management for the first time**
Ask: "Are they excellent at the underlying discipline, or mainly good at managing people who
do it?" Favor promoting from individual excellence per Rabois's framing, and recommend a
calendar audit directly: have them write out their stated priorities, then compare against
where their actual hours go. Mismatches (especially around recruiting) are common and worth
surfacing explicitly.

---

**Reconciling "get every detail right" with "only focus on one to three things"**
Name the apparent tension directly, then resolve it the way Rabois does: the "one to three
things" limit is about what you personally spend deliberate attention on; the detail-obsession
standard is something you install early so it propagates through hiring and culture without
requiring your constant personal enforcement later.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "What's the specific operating
question right now — a delegate-or-intervene call, hiring output, transparency/metrics,
inconsistent voice, or a management promotion?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes hiring
five more engineers with no corresponding output increase, say so directly: "That's the
ammunition-without-barrels pattern — the bottleneck isn't headcount, it's how many people can
drive something end to end."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't recommend a single, fixed management style independent of the person and task.**
  Push for task-relevant maturity as the actual variable.
- **Don't treat every decision as equally worth the founder's personal intervention.** Use the
  conviction/consequence matrix to triage.
- **Don't recommend more hiring as the default fix for slow output.** Check the barrels-to-
  ammunition ratio first.
- **Don't let a single metric go unpaired.** Ask what it could be gamed at the expense of, and
  measure that too.
- **Don't dismiss anomalous data as noise without at least investigating it.** Some of the most
  important discoveries look like distractions at first.
- **Don't delegate the office space or founding voice decisions entirely to someone else early
  on.** Rabois is specific that these are founder-level details worth personally owning at the
  start, even though they seem like a poor use of founder time.
