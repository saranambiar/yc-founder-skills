Execution, Focus, and Momentum

Sam Altman  ·  President, Y Combinator

There are, by Altman's estimate, a hundred times more people with good ideas than people willing to grind through executing them. This lecture is about the grind.

Core Argument

The One Argument Altman Makes

The idea is not the scarce resource — the willingness to execute it well is. Altman's claim is that almost everything that actually determines whether a startup survives comes down to three disciplines applied relentlessly by the founder personally: staying focused on two or three things at a time, holding both speed and quality as non-negotiable rather than trading one for the other, and protecting momentum above almost everything else. None of this can be delegated — whatever the founder does becomes the company's culture, by default.

Focus

Focus: Two or Three Things, Not Ten

A hundred plausible-seeming priorities compete for a founder's attention every day. Almost none of them matter. The discipline is identifying the two or three that do, and ignoring, delegating, or deferring the rest.

Altman's favorite diagnostic question

"What are you spending your time and money on?" — not what you'd say if asked, what you're actually doing. The gap between the two answers is usually the whole problem.

You get no credit for trying — only for what the market wants
Say no ~97 times out of 100
Repeat the company's 2-3 goals far more than feels necessary

"You have to identify the right two or three things, work on those, and then ignore or delegate or defer the rest... you get no credit for trying. You only get points when you make something that the market wants."

— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2
Intensity

Speed and Quality Are Not a Tradeoff

The instinct is to treat "move fast" and "be obsessed with quality" as opposing forces you have to balance. Altman's argument, using Apple, Facebook, and Google as examples: the best companies hold both at once — frugal and fast in some places, uncompromising on quality everywhere.

The plane story

Altman's company was about to lose its first big customer to an incumbent that had already "locked them up." His team drove to the airport, flew out same-day, and sat in the incumbent's lobby from 6am until someone would meet them. They closed the deal about a week later — a deal Altman says wouldn't have happened without showing up in person. "When people say get on planes in marginal situations, they usually mean it literally."

"Facebook has this famous [motto] that says move fast and break things. But at the same time they manage to be obsessed with quality... you need a culture where people have very high quality standards for everything the company does but still move quickly."

— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2
Momentum

Momentum Is the Thing to Protect

If Altman could tell a founder only one thing about running a company, this is it: a winning team keeps winning; a team that hasn't won in a while gets demotivated and keeps losing. The spiral, once it starts, is the actual mechanism behind most startup deaths that follow a strong start.

If I can only tell founders one thing: for most software startups, that translates to keep growing. For hardware startups, it translates to don't let your ship date slip.

How Facebook recovered momentum in 2008

When growth slowed, Mark Zuckerberg didn't call a vision meeting — he formed a growth group focused on small, concrete wins. It became the most prestigious group at the company and, by Altman's account, turned the whole company's dynamic around.

What doesn't work

Long speeches about vision and rallying the troops. Employees at a company where momentum has sagged don't want to hear it. Save the vision speeches for when you're already winning — get momentum back through small, real wins instead. "Sales fix everything in a startup."

Competitor press is a bigger threat to morale than the competitor usually is. "Don't worry about a competitor at all until they're actually beating you with a real, shipped product. Press releases are easier to write than code, and that is still easier than making a great product." Altman cites Henry Ford: "the competitor to be feared is one who never bothers about you at all, but goes on making his own business better all the time."
Apply With Judgment

Where This Can Mislead You

This is intense, opinionated advice from one specific stage and shape of company. A few places it needs a caveat.

"Startups are bad for work-life balance" sits right next to a burnout warning. Earlier in the same session, in the pre-lecture Q&A, Altman is direct: "founder depression is this serious thing, and you need to have a support network." Extreme intensity is meant to be sustained through real support — not white-knuckled alone.
"You get no credit for trying" can be misapplied to punish good bets that didn't land. The point is to focus effort where the market will actually reward it — not to retroactively shame every well-reasoned effort that happened not to pan out.
The plane story is a vivid anecdote, not a formula. It worked once, under specific circumstances. "Show up in person in marginal situations" is a reasonable heuristic for underrating physical presence — not proof that showing up always wins the deal.
"Keep growing" assumes venture-scale growth dynamics. This is calibrated for software and hardware startups with a clear growth or shipping metric. Don't force-fit "growth at all costs" onto a business that's shaped differently, just because it's the most repeated line in the lecture.
"Ignore competitors until they ship" isn't "ignore competitors who are quietly winning without press." The test is a real, shipped product taking your users — not whether they're loud about it.
Principles

What Founders Actually Take Away

Six action-oriented principles, grounded in specific claims Altman made.

  1. 1
    Execution, not the idea, is the scarce resource.

    Roughly 100:1, by Altman's estimate — far more people have good ideas than are willing to grind through executing them.

  2. 2
    The founder's behavior is the culture — it can't be delegated.

    If you want a company that moves fast and holds a quality bar, you have to visibly be that yourself. You can't hire a COO to be it for you.

  3. 3
    You can only truly focus on two or three things at once.

    Everything else gets ignored, delegated, or deferred — not because it doesn't matter, but because spreading attention across ten things means none of them get done well.

  4. 4
    Speed and quality are not actually a tradeoff.

    Treat both as non-negotiable. Be frugal and fast in some places; hold an uncompromising quality bar everywhere.

  5. 5
    Momentum is the thing to protect above almost everything else.

    A winning team keeps winning; a stalled team spirals further down. If forced to give one piece of advice, this is Altman's.

  6. 6
    Recover lost momentum with small real wins, not speeches.

    Vision talks work when you're already winning. When you're not, sales — and small, concrete progress — fix more than a rally does.

Apply Now

The Honest Test

Run this against how you're actually spending your time this week.

Run this on your company right now

  1. What are you actually spending your time and money on this week — not what you'd say if asked?
  2. Can you name your company's two or three current goals? Could everyone on your team name them the same way?
  3. Where are you treating speed and quality as a tradeoff instead of holding both? Name the specific place.
  4. Is your team's momentum currently winning or stalled? If stalled, what's the smallest real win available this week — not the speech you're tempted to give?
  5. Is a competitor's noise actually backed by a shipped product taking your users, or just press?
