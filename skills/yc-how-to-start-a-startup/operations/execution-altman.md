## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder already knows roughly what to build and is struggling
with the harder, less glamorous problem: actually getting it done, fast, without losing
quality or momentum. Altman's core claim is that execution — not the idea — is where almost
all founders actually fail, because there are far more people with good ideas than people
willing to grind through the execution of them.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I have ten important things to do and can't figure out what to focus on"
- "Our growth has stalled and the team is starting to argue about what to do"
- "A competitor just got a lot of press and it's demoralizing my team"
- "I feel like we need to move fast, but every time we do, quality suffers"
- "I keep deciding, then re-deciding, and nothing actually ships"
- "What does a startup CEO actually spend their time on day to day?"

This is not the right skill for: choosing a cofounder, evaluating a hire, or equity/vesting —
see `hiring/team-and-cofounders-altman.md`, built from the first half of this same lecture.
It's also not about deciding what to build (that's ideas/product territory) — this is about
the discipline of doing, once you already know the what.

## Assumptions

1. **The founder already has some clarity on what to build.** This skill is about focus,
   speed, and momentum in executing — not about product-market fit or idea validation.
2. **The company is small enough that the founder's own behavior sets the culture directly.**
   Altman is explicit that "whatever the founders do becomes the culture" — this framework is
   calibrated for a stage where the founder's execution habits are still the whole company's
   execution habits, not diluted across a large organization.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Altman states specific,
   sometimes uncomfortable claims (you get no credit for trying, startups are bad for work-life
   balance) backed by his own experience. Match that register once you understand their
   situation.

## Insights

> "Ideas by themselves are not worth anything. Only executing well is what adds value, or
> what creates value."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: Opening frame for the whole second half of the lecture — there are, in Altman's
estimate, at least a hundred times more people with good ideas than people willing to put in
the execution effort. The scarce resource isn't the idea.

> "Whatever the founders do becomes the culture... you cannot hire a COO and have them do
> this while you go off to conferences. The company needs to see you as this maniacal
> execution machine."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: Direct rejection of delegating execution discipline. If the founder wants a culture
of hard work, attention to detail, and frugality, it has to visibly start with the founder,
not be outsourced or announced.

> "You have to identify the right two or three [things], work on those, and then ignore or
> delegate or defer the rest... you get no credit for trying. You only get points when you
> make something that the market wants."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: On focus. Altman's claim is that most startups fail not from working too little but
from working hard on the wrong two or three things, while a hundred plausible-seeming
priorities compete for attention every day.

> "If [the viral coefficient] is .99 the company will eventually flatline and then die. If
> it's 1.01, you'll be in this happy place of exponential growth forever."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: A concrete illustration of how a small amount of extra work on the right thing is
the entire difference between failure and exponential success — not a large, dramatic
difference, a marginal one.

> "Facebook has this famous [motto] that says move fast and break things. But at the same
> time they manage to be obsessed with quality... you need a culture where people have very
> high quality standards for everything the company does but still move quickly."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: The two things founders usually treat as a tradeoff — speed and quality — Altman
argues have to coexist, and names Apple, Facebook, and Google as companies that did both:
frugal and fast in some places, uncompromising on quality everywhere.

> [Story, paraphrased from the lecture] Altman's own company was about to lose a critical
> first customer to an incumbent. His team drove to the airport, flew out, sat in the
> incumbent's lobby from 6am until someone agreed to meet them, and closed the deal about a
> week later — a deal Altman says would not have happened without showing up in person.
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: Offered as literal advice, not just a metaphor — "get on planes in marginal
situations" — a real account of a deal Altman believes he'd have lost otherwise.

> "If I can only tell founders one thing about how to run a company, it would be this: for
> most software startups, this translates to keep growing. For hardware startups it
> translates to don't let your ship date slip."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014

Context: On momentum. Altman's single highest-priority piece of advice — a winning team keeps
winning, a team that hasn't won in a while gets demotivated and keeps losing, and this spiral
is, in his experience, the actual mechanism behind most startup deaths after a strong start.

> "Don't worry about a competitor at all until they're actually beating you with a real,
> shipped product. Press releases are easier to write than code, and that is still easier
> than making a great product."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 2, 2014, citing a Henry Ford line on the same point

Context: On competitor noise as a momentum killer. Altman's point isn't that competitors
don't matter — it's that press about a competitor is almost never evidence they've actually
beaten you, and it disproportionately damages team morale relative to the actual threat.

## Principles

1. **Execution, not the idea, is the scarce resource.** Altman's estimate is roughly 100:1 —
   far more people have good ideas than are willing to grind through executing them.
2. **The founder's own behavior is the culture — it cannot be delegated.** If you want a
   company that moves fast, pays attention to detail, and is frugal, you have to visibly be
   that yourself; you can't hire it into existence.
3. **You can only truly focus on two or three things at once.** Everything else has to be
   ignored, delegated, or deferred — not because those things don't matter, but because
   spreading attention across ten things means none of them get done well.
4. **You get no credit for trying.** Effort on the wrong priorities produces nothing — the
   market only rewards effort that lands on what it actually wants.
5. **Small, consistent extra effort compounds into the entire outcome.** The viral coefficient
   example (.99 dies, 1.01 grows forever) is the general pattern: the margin between failure
   and success is often small and continuous, not large and dramatic.
6. **Speed and quality are not actually a tradeoff — treat them as both non-negotiable.**
   Companies that do both (Apple, Facebook, Google, in Altman's examples) are frugal and fast
   in some places, but hold an uncompromising quality bar everywhere.
7. **Decisiveness is a discipline, not a personality trait.** Indecisive founders talk about
   grand plans without committing; the best founders are recognized by how quickly they've
   gotten new, visible things done between conversations.
8. **Momentum is the thing to protect above almost everything else.** A winning team keeps
   winning; a team that stalls gets demotivated and spirals further down. If forced to give
   one piece of advice, this is Altman's.
9. **Recovering lost momentum happens through small real wins, not speeches.** Vision talks
   work when a company is already winning — when it isn't, they read as hollow. Sales,
   specifically, "fixes everything" in Altman's experience.
10. **Competitor press is a bigger threat to morale than the competitor usually is.** Treat a
    competitor's actual shipped product as the signal that matters, not their press coverage.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Startups are bad for work-life balance" is presented as a flat statement of reality, but
  it sits right next to a burnout warning Altman gives earlier in the same session** (in the
  pre-lecture Q&A): "founder depression is this serious thing, and you need to have a support
  network." Don't let "extreme intensity" advice get applied without that caveat — the
  intensity is meant to be sustained through real support, not white-knuckled alone.
- **"You get no credit for trying" is a useful corrective against busywork, but it can be
  misapplied to punish legitimate, well-reasoned bets that didn't pan out.** The point is to
  focus effort on what the market will actually reward, not to retroactively shame any effort
  that didn't produce results — some good bets still lose.
- **The plane story is a vivid anecdote, not a universal formula.** It worked once, in a
  specific deal, under specific circumstances. "Show up in person in marginal situations" is
  a reasonable heuristic for underrating the value of physical presence — it isn't proof that
  showing up always wins the deal.
- **"Keep growing" as the one rule can be misapplied to businesses without the growth
  dynamics Altman is describing.** This advice is calibrated for venture-scale software and
  hardware startups with a clear growth or shipping metric. A founder building something
  slower-moving or fundamentally different in shape shouldn't force-fit "growth at all costs"
  just because it's the most repeated line in this lecture.
- **"Don't worry about competitors until they ship" is a real corrective against overreacting
  to press, but it isn't advice to ignore competitors who are quietly shipping and gaining
  distribution without press coverage.** The test is a shipped, working product taking your
  users — not whether they're loud.

## Thinking Framework

**Too many priorities, no clear focus**
Ask: "What are the two or three things you're actually spending time and money on right now —
not what you'd say if asked, what you're actually doing?" Altman calls this one of his
favorite diagnostic questions, because the gap between the two answers is usually the whole
problem.

If they can't name two or three: that's the finding — say so, and ask what would have to be
true for them to cut the list down this week.

---

**Growth has stalled / team is arguing about direction**
Ask: "Has this been going on for a specific stretch of weeks, and has the team started
disagreeing about what to do?" If yes — name the pattern directly: "That's the exact sequence
Altman describes — stalled momentum leads to disagreement, disagreement leads to more stalling."
Then: "When Facebook's growth stalled in 2008, they didn't call a vision meeting — they formed
a small group focused on tiny, concrete wins. What's the smallest real win available to you
this week?"

---

**Competitor got press / team is demoralized**
Ask: "Have they actually shipped something that's taking your users, or is this press without
a working product?" If it's press without product: say so plainly using the Henry Ford framing
— "the competitor to fear is the one who never bothers about you and just keeps making their
own product better." If they have genuinely shipped and are winning users: this isn't the
"ignore it" case — treat it as a real signal instead.

---

**Speed vs. quality tension**
Ask: "Where specifically do you feel the tradeoff — is it that moving fast is producing bad
output, or that holding a quality bar is slowing you down?" Push on whether the founder has
actually decided which things can be frugal/fast (per Altman's Apple/Facebook/Google framing)
and which things get held to an uncompromising bar — most founders haven't drawn that line
explicitly, which is why it feels like one universal tradeoff instead of a choice per area.

---

**Indecisiveness**
Ask: "What's the decision you're circling? What would you lose by just picking one path this
week instead of continuing to weigh both?" If the founder is describing a genuinely
long-running back-and-forth with no new information arriving, say so directly — that's the
indecisiveness pattern Altman calls a startup killer, not a case needing more analysis.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're stuck on, ask: "What's the specific execution problem
right now — too many priorities, stalled growth, competitor noise, or something else?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes ten
priorities and says they're all critical, say so directly: "That's the core problem, not a
detail — pick two or three." Don't ask a fourth clarifying question to avoid saying it.

## What Not To Do

- **Don't accept "everything is a priority" as an answer.** Push for the two or three things
  actually receiving time and money, per Altman's own diagnostic question.
- **Don't let "we're moving fast" get treated as automatically good.** Ask what quality bar
  is being held alongside the speed — Altman's framing requires both, not speed alone.
- **Don't treat competitor press coverage as equivalent to competitor threat.** Check for an
  actual shipped product before treating it as a real signal.
- **Don't respond to stalled momentum by suggesting a vision speech or rally.** Altman is
  specific that this doesn't work when a team isn't winning — the fix is small, real wins.
- **Don't repeat "startups are bad for work-life balance" without the burnout/support-network
  caveat from the same session.** Extreme intensity without support is a different thing than
  extreme intensity with it.
- **Don't treat the plane story as a formula to replicate literally in every "marginal
  situation."** Use it to argue for taking physical presence seriously as an option, not as
  a guarantee.
