## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder is deciding whether to demote or fire someone, handling
a request for a raise, designing an equity or vesting policy, needing to fire someone while
preserving their dignity, or facing pressure to decide something on the spot. Horowitz
deliberately teaches exactly one management concept — that any consequential decision has to
be seen through every affected perspective, not just the person you're talking to — and spends
the whole lecture showing how that one discipline changes the right answer across very
different situations.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I have a hardworking executive who's just not skilled enough for the role — demote or fire?"
- "A great employee just asked me for a raise — what do I say?"
- "Should we let people keep their vested stock options longer than the standard 90 days?"
- "I have to fire someone and I don't know how to do it without destroying them"
- "Someone just asked me for something important and I feel pressure to answer right now"

This is not the right skill for the mechanics of hiring or evaluating a candidate before they
join (`hiring/team-and-cofounders-altman.md`) or for the day-to-day operating discipline of
running the company (`operations/how-to-operate-rabois.md`), which this skill's single
principle complements directly.

## Assumptions

1. **There's a specific, real decision in front of the founder involving at least one other
   person's outcome** — this isn't a framework for abstract company strategy.
2. **The founder has the authority to actually decide the policy or outcome in question** —
   whether to fire, what the raise process looks like, what the equity policy is.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Horowitz states a specific,
   sometimes uncomfortable conclusion in each of his three cases (ad hoc raises are "encouraging
   [the wrong] behavior"; badmouthing someone you fire reflects on you, not them) rather than
   leaving the answer open.

## Insights

> "You have to be able, when making critical decisions, to see the decision through the eyes
> of the company as a whole. You have to add up every employee's view and then incorporate
> that into your own view. Otherwise your management decisions are going to have weird side
> effects and potentially dangerous consequences."
— Ben Horowitz, CS183B Lecture 15, 2014

Context: The single concept Horowitz says he sees CEOs mishandle more consistently than
anything else, from the earliest stage to the largest scale — easy to state, difficult to
actually practice under pressure.

> "How does your engineer feel about someone who is NOT the Head of Sales anymore having 1.5%
> of the company? Are you going to take the equity away? How productive will he be if you do?
> Will people give him the same respect — 'I knew you when you were Head of Sales, now you're
> telling me what to do?'"
— Ben Horowitz, CS183B Lecture 15, 2014, on demoting vs. firing a hardworking but underqualified executive

Context: A demotion feels kinder than firing from the CEO's seat and the affected executive's
seat — but Horowitz walks through the second-order effects on equity fairness, the demoted
person's authority, and what the whole company learns about what failure actually costs at the
top of the equity table.

> "If you give out raises when people just ask you for them, you will have a lot of people
> asking you for raises. That is called encouraging behavior. The cultural conclusion is
> everyone now feels a fiduciary responsibility to their family to ask for a raise constantly,
> because if they don't, they might be missing out on one they'd otherwise have gotten."
— Ben Horowitz, CS183B Lecture 15, 2014

Context: Giving a good employee a raise when they ask feels purely positive from the CEO's
side — Horowitz's point is that from every other employee's perspective, it teaches that
asking (not performance) is what determines pay, which reliably produces exactly the
behavior it rewards.

> "The right answer: be formal to save your own culture. Look at all inputs — talk to everyone
> they work with, evaluate the actual work, do it periodically, not off-cycle and not on
> request. At the end I'll tell you your raise or that you're not getting one, but I'm not
> doing it because you asked."
— Ben Horowitz, CS183B Lecture 15, 2014

Context: The concrete alternative to ad hoc raises — a real, periodic, input-gathering process
that people can trust precisely because it doesn't respond to who lobbies hardest or asks most
often, including from Horowitz's own experience with aggressive executives trying to
individually negotiate outside the process.

> "Up until 2004 there was a law — APB Opinion Number 25 — that made a 10-year option exercise
> window impossible if you ever wanted to go public, because it tied an unpredictable
> compensation expense to your stock price. That's why the 90-day rule exists. It's absolutely
> right to question whether it should still exist now that the reason for it is gone — but I
> wouldn't go as far as saying every company should adopt the alternative."
— Ben Horowitz, CS183B Lecture 15, 2014, evaluating Sam Altman's proposal that startups adopt 10-year option exercise windows

Context: Horowitz first establishes why a rule made sense historically before evaluating
whether to change it — a specific, disciplined move that separates "why does this exist" from
"should it still exist," rather than assuming either that old rules are automatically wrong or
automatically still justified.

> "There are two coherent cultural stances, not one right answer: 'we're fair no matter what —
> you get your 10 years regardless of how rich or poor you are when you leave,' or 'your stock
> is only meaningful if you stay until we succeed — don't join us if you're planning to leave in
> 18 months, because our policy guarantees you'll get nothing if you do.'"
— Ben Horowitz, CS183B Lecture 15, 2014

Context: Rather than declaring one exercise-window policy universally correct, Horowitz frames
it as a real choice between two different, internally consistent cultural philosophies — each
with real tradeoffs (dilution economics, retention "for the wrong reasons," fairness to people
who leave vs. commitment from people who stay).

> "Toussaint took into account three perspectives when deciding what to do with defeated
> soldiers and slave owners: his own soldiers' view (kill them, take everything), the enemy's
> view (their whole cost structure depended on slave labor), and the view of the resulting
> culture he was actually trying to build. The solution: end slavery, let owners keep their
> land, make them pay workers, and lower their taxes to make the economics work — because the
> bigger goal was a stronger, world-class culture, not simple revenge."
— Ben Horowitz, CS183B Lecture 15, 2014, on Toussaint Louverture's Haitian Revolution

Context: Horowitz's most extended illustration of the lecture's single principle — a decision
that satisfied no single constituency's first instinct, but that produced the only successful
slave revolution in history and an economy that briefly out-exported the United States, because
it was built from multiple real perspectives at once rather than the most emotionally obvious one.

> "You can take somebody's job, but you don't have to take their dignity. It's not necessary to
> get up in front of the company and say what you really feel. Thank them for their work. The
> right frame is: I made a mistake matching this person to what the company needed — not 'you
> suck.' What you say in that meeting is their reputation, because everyone in your company will
> get called for a reference on them eventually."
— Ben Horowitz, CS183B Lecture 15, 2014, from the Q&A

Context: A direct answer on how to fire someone — attribute the failure to a hiring/matching
mistake rather than personal inadequacy, and recognize that how you talk about someone after
firing them reflects on your own judgment and culture, not just on them.

> "If something is really important and you haven't thought it through from every angle, pause.
> 'I'm taking this seriously, but I have to think it through — I'll come back to you.' The
> mistake is deciding under the covers, quietly, hoping nobody notices — what we call a kimchi
> problem: the deeper you bury it, the hotter it gets. It surfaces later, worse."
— Ben Horowitz, CS183B Lecture 15, 2014, from the Q&A

Context: A concrete technique for resisting the pressure to answer immediately on emotionally
loaded requests — pausing explicitly, rather than either deciding impulsively or trying to
handle something quietly and hoping it stays contained.

## Principles

1. **See every consequential decision through the eyes of everyone it will actually reach —
   not just your own view or the person you're talking to.** This is the entire lecture's
   thesis; every case is an application of it.
2. **A demotion isn't automatically kinder than a firing — check what it teaches the rest of
   the company about what failure at a senior, highly-compensated level actually costs.**
   Equity fairness and authority erosion are real second-order effects, not abstractions.
3. **Ad hoc raises, given because someone asked, train the whole company that asking — not
   performing — determines pay.** This is a predictable, not surprising, cultural consequence.
4. **Protect a raise or comp process by making it formal and periodic, evaluated from
   multiple inputs, and explicitly not responsive to individual lobbying or timing.** This
   protects the culture and reduces anxiety for people who don't advocate loudly for themselves.
5. **Before deciding whether an old policy should change, understand why it existed in the
   first place.** A rule with an obsolete original reason isn't automatically wrong to keep or
   automatically right to change — evaluate the actual current tradeoffs.
6. **Some policy questions have more than one internally coherent right answer.** Present the
   real alternative cultural stances and their tradeoffs rather than assuming a single
   universal best practice exists.
7. **When incorporating former adversaries or defeated competitors, decide based on the
   culture you're actually trying to build, not just the immediate desire for retribution or
   convenience.** Toussaint's choice to make defeated generals part of his own army, and to
   preserve slave owners' economic viability while ending slavery, both came from optimizing
   for the resulting culture and economy, not the easiest immediate reaction.
8. **When firing someone, attribute the failure to a hiring/matching mistake, not personal
   inferiority — and preserve their dignity and reputation when discussing it with others.**
   How you talk about someone you fired is a signal about your own judgment to everyone still
   watching.
9. **Pause on emotionally loaded, high-stakes requests rather than deciding on the spot.**
   The pressure to have an immediate answer is real, but an unconsidered decision made quickly
   is more costly than a short, explicit delay.
10. **Never handle something quietly hoping it won't surface.** Problems handled "under the
    covers" don't disappear — they resurface later, worse, and with less trust attached.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"See every perspective" is not the same as "satisfy every perspective."** Toussaint's
  actual policy toward slave owners satisfied none of the three viewpoints' first instinct
  fully — the discipline is incorporating every view into the decision, not deferring to
  whichever party is loudest or most sympathetic.
- **The raise-process principle can be over-applied into total rigidity that ignores genuinely
  exceptional, time-sensitive situations** (a competing offer with a real deadline, a critical
  retention risk). Horowitz's own point is about protecting against ad hoc favoritism, not
  about refusing to ever exercise judgment within a periodic process.
- **The stock-option exercise-window discussion is specific to a particular legal and market
  context (post-2004 US accounting rules, high-valuation late-stage startups).** The
  underlying discipline — understand why a policy exists before changing it, and recognize
  when more than one coherent stance exists — is durable; the specific numbers and legal
  backdrop should be checked against current rules, not assumed unchanged.
- **The Toussaint case is an extreme, historically singular example** (Horowitz calls it the
  only successful slave revolution in history) — useful for demonstrating the principle at its
  most dramatic, not a template that guarantees similar outcomes when applied to an ordinary
  business conflict with a competitor or difficult employee.
- **"Attribute a firing to a hiring mistake" is genuinely good practice for preserving dignity
  and reputation — but it shouldn't be used to avoid ever having a direct, honest conversation
  about specific performance issues with the person being let go.** Horowitz distinguishes
  between what you say to the person (honest) and how you talk about them afterward to others
  (dignity-preserving) — collapsing these into one vague, softened message to the person
  themselves isn't what he's recommending.

## Thinking Framework

**Demote vs. fire a hardworking, underqualified executive**
Ask: "What's this person's equity level relative to others at their function, and what would
it mean to the rest of the company if they stayed in a lower role at that equity?" Push past
the immediate kindness of a demotion toward the concrete downstream questions: does the
demotion feel fair to others at the company, and will this person retain enough authority to
actually be useful in the new role, or will they be undermined by everyone who remembers their
old title?

---

**Handling a raise request**
Ask: "If you say yes right now, what does every other employee who didn't ask learn about how
raises get decided here?" If there's no formal, periodic process: name that as the actual gap
to fix, not just this one request. Recommend deferring this specific ask into a real process
rather than deciding it in isolation, and explain that framing to the person directly.

---

**Designing an equity or vesting policy**
Ask: "Is your priority treating people the same regardless of circumstance, or rewarding people
specifically for staying until the company succeeds?" Present both as legitimate, coherent
choices with real tradeoffs (dilution/return-to-pool economics vs. fairness to departing
employees) rather than picking one as objectively correct. Then ask what policy already exists
by default and whether it was a deliberate choice or an inherited one.

---

**Firing someone**
Ask: "What's the honest reason this didn't work — and have you framed it as a hiring/matching
mistake rather than a personal failure?" Then, separately: "What will you say about them to the
rest of the company and to future reference-callers?" Treat these as two different
conversations with two different audiences, both requiring honesty, but calibrated differently.

---

**Feeling pressure to decide something important right now**
Ask: "Have you actually thought this through from every angle it touches, or are you about to
decide under time pressure?" If it's the latter: recommend the explicit pause technique
directly — "I'm taking this seriously, I need to think it through, I'll come back to you" —
rather than either deciding hastily or quietly trying to handle it without surfacing it.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "What's the specific management
decision right now — a demotion/firing call, a raise request, an equity policy, a firing
conversation, or feeling pressure to decide something on the spot?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes wanting to
quietly give one employee a raise "just this once" without a broader process, say so directly:
"That's the exact pattern Horowitz warns produces a company where everyone feels obligated to
ask constantly — worth building the formal process instead of making this a one-off."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't evaluate a demotion or firing decision only from the CEO's and the affected person's
  perspective.** Add the rest of the company's view explicitly before deciding.
- **Don't grant an off-cycle raise in response to a single request without a broader process
  in place.** Name the cultural consequence directly if a founder is about to do this.
- **Don't assume an old policy is either sacred or obsolete without first understanding why it
  existed.** Check the original reason before deciding whether it still applies.
- **Don't present an equity/vesting question as having one universally correct answer.**
  Lay out the real alternative stances and their tradeoffs.
- **Don't let a founder badmouth someone they've fired to the rest of the company**, even if
  the frustration is genuine. Redirect toward dignity-preserving language and a
  mistake-in-matching frame.
- **Don't let a founder decide something significant on the spot just because they feel
  pressure to have an immediate answer.** Recommend the explicit pause technique instead of
  either impulsive decision-making or quiet, unsurfaced handling.
