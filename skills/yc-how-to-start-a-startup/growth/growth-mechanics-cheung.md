## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder is choosing which growth channel to focus on, unsure
whether their growth is actually sustainable, evaluating a referral program, or wondering
whether stalled growth is a sign to pivot. This is the second half of Cheung's lecture — the
first half (validating a problem and building an MVP) is a different founder situation, see
`product/mvp-and-users-cheung.md`.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I want to try five growth channels at once to see what sticks"
- "We're growing, but I don't know if it's the good kind of growth or a leaky bucket"
- "Our referral program isn't producing much — what are we missing?"
- "I'm spending on ads but I'm not sure if the economics actually work"
- "Growth has stalled for a few weeks — is it time to pivot?"

This is not the right skill for validating the initial problem or building the first version
of the product — see `product/mvp-and-users-cheung.md`, the first half of this same lecture.

## Assumptions

1. **There's already a real MVP with some real users**, even a small number. This is about
   growing an already-validated starting point, not finding the first users at all.
2. **The founder (or a very small team) is the one doing growth work directly**, not a
   dedicated growth team — Cheung is explicit this framework is for the earliest stage.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Cheung gives specific numeric
   thresholds (3-4 weeks of no growth, 3-12 month payback windows) and a concrete personal
   story (Homejoy as the 13th idea) rather than only open-ended prompts.

## Insights

> "Early on, when it's just you and your cofounder, you're not going to create a team just for
> growth — it's going to be one person. You're going to be tempted to try five different
> strategies at once. Really, what you should do is take one channel and execute on it for an
> entire week, and just focus on that."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: A direct corrective against spreading thin early — testing several channels at a
third of your time each for weeks produces a weaker read on any single channel than fully
committing to one for the same total time.

> "Channels always change — anything from Facebook ads to Google ads. You should always be
> iterating and optimizing. And when a channel fails you early on, get rid of it — but over
> time, go back and look at it again. We couldn't afford Google ads early on because national
> competitors could outbid us. Today we make more money per job — we should probably revisit it."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: A channel's viability isn't fixed — it depends on your own unit economics at the
time, which change as the business improves. A channel correctly rejected once isn't
permanently rejected.

> "Sticky growth is getting existing users to keep buying. The only thing that matters is
> delivering a good, even addictive, experience — measured through CLV and retention cohort
> analysis. A cohort at time zero is 100% of your users; you want that curve to flatten out
> over time so churn approaches zero. A really bad retention curve goes to zero — I can't
> explain a good business with a curve like that."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: The core diagnostic for whether a product is fundamentally working — a retention
curve that flattens (versus one that decays to zero) is, in Cheung's view, close to a
necessary condition for a viable business, not just a nice-to-have metric.

> "Viral growth needs a really good experience — good enough that people shout about it on
> Twitter — plus good referral mechanics: the customer touch point (when they learn they can
> refer), the program mechanics ($10 for $10, or whatever), and the conversion flow when their
> friend actually clicks through."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: Three separate, independently optimizable parts of a referral program — a weak
result can come from any one of the three, and Cheung recommends testing timing, incentive
structure, and conversion flow as distinct variables rather than one undifferentiated "referral
program."

> "The correct way to think about paid growth: is your CLV more than your CAC? CAC is CPC
> divided by conversion rate. You have to break this down by customer segment, not aggregate —
> someone in Nashville and someone in a much smaller market don't have the same lifetime value,
> and buying ads without knowing the difference mixes signal you need to keep separate."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: The core paid-growth math, with a specific warning against evaluating CAC/CLV in
aggregate — an ad that looks profitable in blended numbers can be unprofitable for a specific
segment, and vice versa.

> "If in the first month a customer is worth $100 of the eventual $300, but you paid $200 to
> acquire them, you're in the hole for $100 until the end of the period — that's when you get
> into unsustainable territory. A safe payback window is about 3 months; up to 12 months if
> you're risk-tolerant; beyond 12 months is very unsafe. If you're funding this with credit
> cards, you will find yourself declaring bankruptcy very soon."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: A direct, numeric framework for how much acquisition risk is tolerable — the danger
isn't spending on acquisition itself, it's the gap between when you pay and when the revenue
actually arrives.

> "Homejoy, in its current concept, is literally the thirteenth idea we fully built and tried
> to get customers for. The three criteria for when to move on: you realize you can't grow, or
> none of your users are high-retaining, or the economics of the business just don't make
> sense."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: Cheung names the growth criterion as the trickiest of the three, specifically because
there are enough stories of founders who stuck with a stalled idea for years before it took
off that "just wait longer" feels like reasonable advice even when it usually isn't.

> "You should have a growth plan from the start — an optimistic but realistic curve, doubling
> week over week from one user. In the beginning, if people actually want your product, you
> should be able to maintain that just by manually finding people. If you go three or four
> weeks in a row of no growth or backwards growth while genuinely executing hard, it's time to
> consider whether you're fundamentally doing something wrong — not starting over completely,
> but reconsidering the approach."
— Adora Cheung, CS183B Lecture 4, 2014

Context: A specific, falsifiable trigger for the pivot conversation — not "whenever it feels
slow," but a defined multi-week window of genuinely flat or negative growth despite real effort.

## Principles

1. **One person, one channel, one full week before judging.** Spreading effort across several
   channels early produces weaker signal on all of them than fully committing to one.
2. **A channel's viability changes as your own economics change — revisit rejected channels
   later, don't write them off permanently.**
3. **Sticky growth lives or dies on the retention/cohort curve.** A curve that flattens toward
   low churn is close to a necessary signal of a working business; one that decays to zero
   almost never describes a viable one.
4. **Viral growth has three independently optimizable parts** — the touch point timing, the
   incentive mechanics, and the invitee's conversion flow. Diagnose which one is weak rather
   than treating "referrals aren't working" as one undifferentiated problem.
5. **Paid growth is only justified when CLV exceeds CAC — evaluated by customer segment, not
   in aggregate.** Blended averages hide segments where the math doesn't work.
6. **Payback time, not just CLV vs. CAC, determines sustainability.** A profitable-on-paper
   channel can still bankrupt a company if the cash gap between spend and return is too wide.
7. **A safe payback window is roughly 3 months; beyond 12 months is unsafe territory** —
   especially if funded by debt rather than existing capital.
8. **There are three concrete criteria for deciding to pivot: can't grow, no high-retaining
   users, or the unit economics don't work.** Growth is the trickiest of the three to read
   correctly, precisely because occasional survivorship stories make "just wait longer" sound
   like reasonable advice.
9. **Set a specific growth-curve expectation from the start, and use a defined multi-week
   window (3-4 weeks) of genuinely flat/negative growth — under real effort — as the concrete
   trigger to reconsider the approach.** Don't wait indefinitely, and don't panic after one bad
   week either.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Test one channel for a full week" is a rule of thumb for the earliest stage with very
  limited hands, not a claim that channel diversification is bad at any stage.** As a company
  grows, running multiple channels in parallel with dedicated ownership is normal and often
  necessary — the point here is specifically about not spreading one or two people too thin.
- **The "3-4 weeks of no growth means consider a pivot" heuristic assumes genuine, full-effort
  execution during that window.** It's not meant to apply to a founder who's been distracted,
  under-resourced, or not actually testing the growth plan seriously — the signal is only
  meaningful if the effort was real.
- **Homejoy being "the thirteenth idea" can be read as license to keep pivoting indefinitely
  without ever committing.** Cheung's own three criteria are meant to prevent exactly that —
  the point of having explicit criteria is to stop relying on gut feeling about when to move on,
  not to make pivoting easier or more frequent by default.
- **The payback-time numbers (3 months safe, 12 months risk-tolerant) are Cheung's own
  judgment from a specific type of business (a local services marketplace), not a universal
  constant.** Businesses with different capital structures or customer economics may
  reasonably operate with different payback tolerances — treat the specific numbers as a
  useful anchor, not a hard rule.
- **"I can't explain a good business with a retention curve that goes to zero" is a strong
  claim worth taking seriously, but not an absolute law.** Some genuinely one-time-purchase
  business models have structurally different retention shapes — check whether repeat usage is
  actually the right success metric for the specific business before applying this rigidly.

## Thinking Framework

**Choosing a growth channel with limited time**
Ask: "What have you actually tried, for how long, one at a time?" If several things have been
tried in parallel or briefly: name the problem directly. "That's not enough signal on any one
of them — commit to one channel for a full week before judging it." Then help pick the single
most promising channel to commit to next.

---

**Not sure if growth is sustainable**
Ask: "Have you plotted your retention/cohort curve — does it flatten out, or decay toward
zero?" If they haven't measured this: that's the first gap to close. If it decays toward zero:
name it plainly — that's the pattern Cheung says she can't explain a good business having.

---

**Referral program underperforming**
Ask: "Which of the three parts is weak — are people not learning they can refer, is the
incentive not compelling, or is the conversion flow for the invitee bad?" Diagnose each
separately rather than treating "referrals aren't working" as one problem.

---

**Evaluating paid acquisition spend**
Ask: "What's your CLV vs. CAC, broken out by customer segment — not blended?" If they've only
looked at blended numbers: recommend segmenting before drawing conclusions. Then ask about
payback time specifically: "How long until you recover what you spent to acquire them, and how
are you funding that gap?"

---

**Wondering if it's time to pivot**
Ask about all three criteria directly, one at a time: "Can you not grow, are your users not
high-retaining, or do the unit economics just not work?" Then ask specifically: "Have you had
3-4 consecutive weeks of flat or negative growth while genuinely executing hard?" If yes to a
real multi-week window: name it as the concrete trigger Cheung describes. If it's been one bad
week, or effort hasn't been consistent: don't treat this as the pivot signal yet.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "What's the specific growth question
right now — which channel to focus on, whether growth is sustainable, a referral program, or
whether to pivot?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes running
four acquisition channels simultaneously as a two-person team, say so directly: "That's spread
too thin to get real signal on any of them — pick one and commit to a full week."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't validate running multiple channels in parallel with a very small team.** Recommend
  committing to one at a time first.
- **Don't accept a retention curve that decays toward zero as "we'll fix it later."** Name it
  as a likely sign the underlying product isn't there yet.
- **Don't treat a weak referral program as a single problem.** Diagnose the touch point,
  mechanics, and conversion flow separately.
- **Don't evaluate paid acquisition using blended CLV/CAC across all customers.** Push for
  segment-level numbers before drawing conclusions.
- **Don't let a founder use "someone eventually made this the 13th idea work" as license to
  pivot indefinitely without applying the three explicit criteria.**
- **Don't treat one bad week of growth as a pivot signal, or four genuinely bad weeks as
  something to keep waiting out.** Use the specific multi-week window as the actual trigger.
