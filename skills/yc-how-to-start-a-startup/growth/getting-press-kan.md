## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder wants press coverage without a clear reason why, is
deciding whether a PR firm is worth the cost, doesn't know the actual mechanics of reaching a
reporter, or is treating being covered in the press as a proxy for success. Kan's central
move is refusing to treat press as an end in itself — every example he gives (Social Cam,
Exec, Twitch) targeted a different, specific audience for a specific business reason.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I want to get covered in TechCrunch but I'm not sure what that would actually get us"
- "Should we hire a PR firm?"
- "How do I actually get a reporter to write about us?"
- "We got a lot of press but it didn't translate into anything"
- "When in our company's life should we start caring about press at all?"
- "A competitor just got a lot of press coverage — should we be worried?"

This is not the right skill for the earlier-stage hustle of finding first users and turning
them into champions — a different topic from the same lecture, see
`growth/unscalable-things-williams-tang.md`. Competitor press specifically as a morale/
momentum issue is covered in `operations/execution-altman.md`.

## Assumptions

1. **The founder has, or should have, a specific business goal in mind for press** — not
   press as a general aspiration. Kan's framework doesn't work if the goal is just "be in the
   news."
2. **The company has at least a launchable product, milestone, or story** — this is about
   getting real coverage for something real, not manufacturing news from nothing.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Kan states specific, sometimes
   counterintuitive claims (don't hire a PR firm early; press is a vanity metric) from his own
   repeated experience across four different companies with four different press strategies.

## Insights

> "A lot of people, when they first get started, think about getting press as something that
> happens magically — like journalists are out there trying to discover the best stories,
> like it's a meritocracy. That's absolutely not the case."
— Justin Kan, CS183B Lecture 8, 2014

Context: Kan's opening correction — press coverage is a mechanical, relationship-driven
process a founder can learn and execute deliberately, not a reward that arrives for having a
good enough company.

> "Before you think about press, think about who you want to reach and what your actual goal
> is. If you don't have any goals, you're not gonna achieve them... getting in the news is
> nice because you can send it to your mom, but if you don't have an actual business goal with
> it, it's really just not a good use of time."
— Justin Kan, CS183B Lecture 8, 2014

Context: The organizing principle of the whole talk. Kan gives three different real examples —
Social Cam wanted to be positioned as a "video Instagram" for Silicon Valley investors; Exec
wanted local San Francisco customers, so national press was actively useless; Twitch wanted
credibility specifically within the gaming industry, so they targeted gaming trade press, not
general tech press.

> "You really have to think about, objectively, if I wasn't the founder of this company,
> would I want to read a story about what I'm pitching? An incremental feature release might
> not be interesting just because you added something like Facebook contact import."
— Justin Kan, CS183B Lecture 8, 2014

Context: A direct check against founders' natural tendency to think everything about their own
company is newsworthy — the test is whether a disinterested outsider would actually care.

> "Your press angle doesn't have to be original — it has to be 'original enough.' You don't
> want to be the second cooler company to raise $5 million on Kickstarter — the first one gets
> all the news. But the first video game console to raise $10 million on Kickstarter was huge
> news, even though others had raised a lot on Kickstarter before."
— Justin Kan, CS183B Lecture 8, 2014

Context: Explains why a genuinely similar story can either be a non-story or a big story
depending entirely on whether it's the first instance of a specific, novel combination — worth
checking before investing pitch effort.

> "The best thing to do, rather than cold-emailing a reporter, is go to entrepreneurs who were
> just written about and get them to introduce you to that reporter. It's the easiest favor in
> the world for them — they don't need anything from that reporter, they're just introducing
> someone interesting. And the reporter is getting an intro to someone already vetted."
— Justin Kan, CS183B Lecture 8, 2014

Context: The specific mechanic Kan recommends over cold outreach — a warm introduction through
someone the reporter already trusts carries real weight on both sides of the introduction.

> "Once you've set a date for your news to go out, get the intro in advance — probably a week
> or more. Get a face-to-face meeting if possible, or at least a phone call. The worst thing to
> do is just an email exchange, because it's very easy for them to forget about it or ignore
> it."
— Justin Kan, CS183B Lecture 8, 2014

Context: Specific tactical guidance on timing and medium — reporters need real lead time and
real-time engagement (not asynchronous email) to actually commit to writing a story.

> "What I usually do is write out the story I would want to see published, in bullet points,
> and memorize it. When I talk to the reporter, I'll walk them through a conversation
> structured like that outline. They take notes, and what I wrote eventually gets translated
> into the actual story."
— Justin Kan, CS183B Lecture 8, 2014

Context: Kan's specific pitching technique — prepare the story you want told in advance so the
conversation naturally produces the notes a reporter needs, rather than hoping they extract
the right angle themselves.

> "In the beginning, you want to do PR yourself before you hire someone else to do it. Firms
> can help with contacts and logistics, but they can't tell you what's interesting about your
> company — I've never had anyone from a firm tell me the actual stories I was producing.
> They're also expensive — we were spending $5,000-$20,000 a month, which is a lot for a
> startup. Generally not a good use of money, especially early."
— Justin Kan, CS183B Lecture 8, 2014

Context: A direct, specific cost figure and a specific claim about what firms can and can't
do — they handle logistics, not the substance of what makes a story worth telling.

> "Getting press feels like a vanity metric — it feels like success because successful
> companies like Google and Facebook are covered all the time. But it doesn't actually mean
> you're successful, making money, or making users happy. It's sometimes a really good
> strategy for your first 100 or 1,000 customers, but it's not a scalable user acquisition
> strategy."
— Justin Kan, CS183B Lecture 8, 2014

Context: Kan's direct corrective against conflating press coverage with actual business
health — useful for an early bootstrap of users, not a substitute for a real growth engine.

> "It's kind of a golden rule, or pay-it-forward thing — you should help your fellow
> entrepreneurs get coverage, because they'll help you get coverage. Whenever I'm meeting with
> reporters, I always throw out names of other things I think would be interesting stories for
> them — and that usually comes back."
— Justin Kan, CS183B Lecture 8, 2014

Context: The long-term relationship-building layer underneath the mechanics — reporters value
founders who consistently bring them good stories, not just their own.

## Principles

1. **Define the specific audience and business goal before pursuing press at all.** Press
   for its own sake produces nothing; press targeted at a specific audience (investors,
   local customers, an industry) can directly serve a specific goal.
2. **Check objectively whether a disinterested outsider would care about your story**, not
   whether it feels significant to you as the founder.
3. **A story doesn't need to be original — it needs to be "original enough."** Being first in
   a specific, narrow framing matters more than genuine novelty.
4. **Warm introductions through recently-covered entrepreneurs beat cold outreach.** The
   introduction is a low-cost favor for the introducer and a pre-vetted lead for the reporter.
5. **Give reporters real lead time and real-time engagement — a week or more, and a call or
   meeting, not an email exchange.** Asynchronous, last-minute outreach is easy to ignore.
6. **Prepare the story you want told in advance, in bullet points, and walk the reporter
   through it as a structured conversation.** Don't rely on them to extract the right angle.
7. **Do PR yourself before hiring a firm.** Firms handle contacts and logistics; they can't
   tell you what's actually interesting about your company, which is the part that matters.
8. **Press is a vanity metric that correlates with success without being success.** Useful for
   an early customer bootstrap, not a scalable acquisition channel, and not evidence of health
   on its own.
9. **Build lasting relationships with a small number of reporters, and pay it forward by
   surfacing other people's stories to them.** This is a relationships business more than a
   transactional pitching business.
10. **Plan a "regular heartbeat" of stories rather than trying to maximize any single one.**
    Space out news deliberately so a company stays in view without becoming stale.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Press is a vanity metric" doesn't mean press is worthless — Kan is explicit it can be a
  genuinely good strategy for the first 100-1,000 customers.** The caution is about conflating
  it with actual business health or treating it as a scalable channel, not about avoiding it.
- **"You don't need to be original, just original enough" can be used to justify a weak,
  copycat story.** The Ouya example worked because it was genuinely first in a specific,
  meaningful category (video game console crowdfunding), not because any minor variation on an
  existing story qualifies.
- **The specific dollar figures ($5,000-$20,000/month for PR firms) are from 2014 and Kan's
  own specific experience.** Treat the underlying claim (firms are expensive relative to what
  they actually contribute) as the transferable lesson, not the exact numbers.
- **"Do it yourself before hiring a firm" is advice about the early stage specifically.** It's
  not a claim that PR firms are never worth it — Kan's critique is that firms can't substitute
  for the founder's own judgment about what's interesting, which matters most before that
  judgment is well developed.
- **The Twitch Plays Pokémon example is presented honestly as something Kan's team didn't
  originate — they only "gave it legs."** Don't extract "manufacture a viral moment like this"
  as a repeatable tactic; the lesson is narrower: have supporting context ready (other coverage
  for reporters to reference) and make the company available and helpful when something
  organically takes off.

## Thinking Framework

**Wanting press without a clear reason**
Ask: "If this got covered, what specifically would you want to happen next — who reads it, and
what do you want them to do?" If there's no clear answer: name it directly. "That's the vanity-
metric trap Kan describes — press without a specific goal usually isn't worth the time it
costs." Push for a specific audience (investors, a local customer base, an industry) before
proceeding.

---

**Deciding whether to hire a PR firm**
Ask: "Do you currently know what's actually interesting about your company well enough to
pitch it yourself?" If yes, and cost is the main question: recommend doing it in-house first —
per Kan, firms handle logistics, not substance, and the substance is the hard part. If the
founder genuinely can't identify the story: that's a different, harder problem a firm can't
solve either.

---

**Not sure how to actually reach a reporter**
Ask: "Do you know any founders who've recently been covered by the reporter or outlet you
want?" If yes: recommend the warm-introduction path directly over cold outreach. If no:
recommend starting with a smaller, more reachable target (industry blogs, local press) to
build the first relationship, rather than aiming at a top-tier outlet cold.

---

**Got press but it didn't translate into results**
Ask: "What was the actual goal going in — and did the coverage reach the audience the goal
required?" If the goal was vague or the audience was mismatched (e.g., national press for a
local product): name the mismatch directly rather than concluding press "doesn't work."

---

**Competitor got a lot of press coverage**
Redirect rather than validating alarm: "Has their coverage translated into anything you can
observe — users, revenue signals — or is it just coverage?" Connect to
`operations/execution-altman.md`'s point that competitor press is a bigger threat to morale
than the competitor usually is.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "What's the specific press question
right now — whether to pursue it at all, how to reach a reporter, or whether to hire a firm?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes wanting
press "to look legitimate" with no specific audience or goal, say so directly: "That's the
vanity-metric case Kan warns about — worth defining a real goal before spending time on this."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't help pursue press without first identifying a specific audience and goal.** Press
  without a goal is close to wasted effort by Kan's own account.
- **Don't recommend a PR firm as a default early-stage move.** Recommend the founder learn the
  mechanics themselves first, per Kan's direct advice and cost data.
- **Don't treat press coverage as evidence of business health on its own.** Check for the
  actual downstream effect (the specific goal it was meant to serve) before treating it as a win.
- **Don't recommend cold-emailing a reporter as the first move if a warm introduction path
  exists.** Warm intros are dramatically more effective per Kan's own account.
- **Don't validate "let's get press about this incremental feature" without the outsider test.**
  Ask whether a disinterested person would actually want to read the story.
- **Don't let competitor press coverage drive strategic anxiety without checking for an actual
  underlying signal.** Coverage and real traction are not the same thing.
