## Overview

This skill is relevant when a founder is choosing between ideas, doubting an idea because it
sounds small or niche, questioning market timing, or diagnosing why their product has users
who like it but nobody who's genuinely attached to it. Altman presents idea evaluation and
early product-building as one continuous throughline — the first two of his four areas
(idea, product, team, execution) — which is why they're one file here.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I have two ideas and can't decide which one to spend years on"
- "Everyone I've told this idea to thinks it's bad or pointless"
- "I have users, and they seem fine with the product, but nobody's telling their friends"
- "An investor said my market is too small"
- "I keep trying to explain what we do and it takes a full paragraph"
- "Should I worry about the well-funded competitor that just launched?"

This is not the right skill for: whether to start a startup at all (see
`ideas/why-start-a-startup-moskovitz.md`, the second half of this same lecture), cofounders
or hiring (`hiring/team-and-cofounders-altman.md`), focus/execution/momentum
(`operations/execution-altman.md`), or deep product craft and user research (Cheung's and
Hale's lectures, not yet built in this repo). Market structure and long-term defensibility —
a related but distinct question from "is this a good idea to start with" — is covered by
`market-strategy/competition-monopoly-thiel.md`.

## Assumptions

1. **The goal is a large, long-term company via hyper-growth**, not a lifestyle business or a
   side project. Altman says this explicitly at the top of the lecture — this advice is
   calibrated for that goal and "much of it doesn't apply" otherwise.
2. **The person has at least a candidate idea, or a product with some real usage.** Frameworks
   applied to nothing concrete produce nothing concrete back.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Altman states specific,
   sometimes counterintuitive claims (good ideas often sound bad, ignore your competitors)
   backed by YC's own portfolio pattern-matching. Match that register once you understand
   their situation.

## Insights

> "Great execution is at least ten times more important and a hundred times harder than a
> good idea. But the pendulum has swung way out of whack here. A bad idea is still bad."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: A direct pushback on "the idea doesn't matter, just pivot" culture. Altman says he
used to believe this himself and now thinks it's wrong — successful pivots almost always land
on something the founders themselves wanted, not a random substitute idea.

> "I care much more about the growth rate of the market than its current size... I'd prefer
> to invest in a company that's going after a small but rapidly growing market than a big but
> slow growing one."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: Altman's claim that most investors are obsessed with market size today and don't
think about how the market evolves — which he calls one of the biggest systemic mistakes
investors make.

> "Sequoia's famous question: why now? Why is this the perfect time for this particular idea,
> and why couldn't it have been done two years ago, and why will two years in the future be
> too late?"
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: Offered as a sharper version of the market-timing question — for the most successful
startups Altman's seen, there's a real, specific answer to "why now." Its absence is grounds
for suspicion.

> "The best ideas often look terrible at the beginning. The 13th search engine, without all
> the features of a web portal... the tenth social network, limited only to college students
> with no money? Also terrible. These all sounded really bad, but they turned out to be good.
> If they had sounded really good, there would have been too many people working on them."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: On Google and Facebook specifically. The logic connects directly to market
structure — an idea that sounds obviously good invites competition before you've won
anything.

> "If it takes more than a sentence to explain what you're doing, it's almost always a sign
> that it's too complicated."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: Altman's simplicity test for both ideas and products — the best ideas are either very
different from existing companies in one important way, or totally new; anything requiring a
long explanation to differentiate ("we're X but for Y people") usually fails.

> "It's better to build something that a small number of users love than a large number of
> users like... it's much easier to expand from something that a small number of people love
> to something that a lot of people love, than from something that a lot of people like to a
> lot of people love."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: The load-bearing framework of the "product" half of the lecture. Altman frames this
as a forced choice — you usually can't have both broad reach and deep love in version one, and
the "love from a few" side compounds far better than the "like from many" side.

> "Very few startups die from competition. Most die because they themselves fail to make
> something users love. Don't worry about your competitors raising a lot of money and what
> they may do in the future. They probably aren't very good anyway."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: A direct claim about where founders misallocate their worry — competitor anxiety is
common and, in Altman's experience, rarely the actual cause of failure.

> "Ben Silverman [Silbermann], when everyone thought Pinterest was a joke, recruited the
> initial Pinterest users by tagging up strangers in coffee shops... He also used to run
> around the Apple Store in Palo Alto and set all the browsers to the Pinterest home page
> real quick before they caught him and kicked him out."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: The concrete illustration of "doing things that don't scale" (Altman references Paul
Graham's essay directly) — recruit your first loved users by hand, not through ads.

> "If your product gets 10% better every week, that compounds really, really quickly... the
> best companies usually have the tightest feedback loops."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: On why the founder-to-user feedback loop matters more early than at any later stage —
software startups can close that loop in hours, and the best ones do.

> "It really is true that the company will build whatever the CEO decides to measure."
— Sam Altman, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: On metrics discipline — ignore vanity numbers like total registrations; track active
usage, retention, revenue, and be honest when they're not moving the right way.

## Principles

1. **A bad idea is still bad — execution can't rescue it.** Pivot culture has swung too far;
   successful pivots almost always land on something the founders already wanted, not a
   randomly substituted idea.
2. **Evaluate market growth rate, not just current size.** A small, rapidly growing market
   beats a big, slow one — and most investors get this backwards by fixating on size today.
3. **Ask "why now."** A credible answer to why this couldn't have worked two years ago and
   won't be too late in two years is a real signal; its absence is grounds for suspicion.
4. **Good ideas often sound bad at first, and that's a feature.** If an idea sounds obviously
   good, it likely already has too much competition.
5. **Mission-driven ideas sustain founders through a decade; derivative ones don't.** A good
   startup usually takes ten years — an idea you don't personally believe in won't survive that.
6. **If it takes more than a sentence to explain, it's probably too complicated.** True for
   the idea and for the product's initial pitch.
7. **Love from a small group beats like from a large group, and compounds better.** This is a
   forced choice in most first versions — choose depth over breadth.
8. **Organic growth by word of mouth is the tell.** Its absence is a signal the product isn't
   good enough yet — not a distribution problem to be patched with PR or partnerships.
9. **Recruit your first users manually, one at a time.** Don't buy ads for early qualitative
   feedback — go find people who'll actually use the thing and tell you the truth.
10. **You get what you measure.** Vanity metrics (total registrations) hide the truth; active
    usage, retention, and revenue don't.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Execution is 10x more important than the idea" and "a bad idea is still bad" are both
  true at once — don't cherry-pick one to justify either idea-worship or idea-dismissal.**
  Altman states both directly; the point is that execution can't rescue a genuinely bad idea,
  not that idea quality alone determines outcomes.
- **The one-sentence explainability test can wrongly penalize genuine novelty.** Some real
  breakthroughs (Altman's own examples: Google, SpaceX) are hard to explain briefly precisely
  because there's no existing category for them yet. Check whether a complicated explanation
  reflects unclear thinking or genuine novelty before treating it as a red flag.
- **"Ignore competitors, most aren't good anyway" is early-stage advice about where to spend
  worry, not a claim that competitive structure never matters.** It doesn't contradict
  Thiel's monopoly framework elsewhere in this repo — it's about not letting competitor
  anxiety distract from product quality day to day, not a claim that defensibility is
  irrelevant long-term.
- **The love-vs-like framing assumes a version-one product narrow enough to have a "small
  group that loves it" moment.** Some categories (certain infrastructure or enterprise tools)
  may not have an obvious version of this moment in the same shape — the underlying principle
  (avoid wide-and-shallow) still applies even if the visible tactic looks different.
- **"Students have better instinct on fast-growing markets" is a specific claim about
  immersion in a technology or behavior, not a blanket grant of market-timing insight to
  anyone young.** The instinct comes from proximity to the trend, not age by itself.

## Thinking Framework

**Choosing between multiple ideas**
Ask: "Which one do you think about most when you're not trying to think about work?" This is
Altman's literal tiebreaker. Then run the "why now" test on each: "Why is this the right time
for this one — what couldn't have been done two years ago, and what would be too late in two
years?" Whichever has the more credible answer to both is the stronger bet.

---

**Doubting an idea because it sounds small or niche**
Ask what specifically makes it feel too small — market size today, or something else? If it's
market size: reframe directly. "Altman cares more about growth rate than current size — a
small market growing fast beats a big one growing slowly. What's your evidence this market is
growing, not just currently small?" If they don't have an answer yet, that's the actual next
step to recommend, not reassurance.

---

**Users like the product, but don't love it**
Ask: "What would your most engaged users actually miss if the product disappeared tomorrow?"
If the answer is vague or generic, say so — that's the signal they're in "liked by many" mode,
not "loved by a few." The fix Altman gives is narrowing, not broadening: "What's the smallest
version of this that a small group would be genuinely upset to lose?"

---

**No organic growth**
Ask directly: "Are people telling their friends about this without being asked?" If not, name
it plainly: "Altman's read on this is specific — no organic growth this early usually means
the product isn't good enough yet, not that you need a bigger distribution push." Then ask
what the most engaged users are doing differently from everyone else.

---

**What to measure**
Ask what they currently report on or track weekly. If it includes total registrations, signups,
or downloads as a headline number: flag it directly. "That's the kind of number Altman says to
ignore — it hides whether people are actually active. What does your retention or active-usage
number actually look like?"

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the founder hasn't said what decision they're facing, ask: "What's the specific idea or
product question right now — choosing between options, doubting an idea, or something about
how users are engaging?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If a founder describes a product
with decent usage numbers but no word-of-mouth growth, say so directly: "That's the signal the
product isn't quite there yet — not a distribution problem." Don't ask a fourth clarifying
question to avoid saying it.

## What Not To Do

- **Don't validate a wide-shallow product as "good enough for now."** If usage is broad but
  shallow, name the love-vs-like gap directly rather than treating "some traction" as a pass.
- **Don't let PR or partnership talk substitute for absent organic growth.** Altman is explicit
  that this is almost always a sign of real trouble, not a legitimate alternative path.
- **Don't accept an explanation that takes a paragraph as sufficiently differentiated.** Push
  for the one-sentence version, and treat difficulty producing one as a signal worth naming.
- **Don't dismiss a small or strange-sounding market as proof the idea is bad.** Check growth
  rate and the "why now" answer before accepting "this seems too small" at face value.
- **Don't recommend paid acquisition (ads) for a founder's first qualitative feedback users.**
  Altman's advice is to recruit them by hand — a fast, cheap channel is the wrong tool for that
  specific job even if it's the right tool later.
- **Don't treat "ignore your competitors" as "competitive structure never matters."** It's
  advice about daily focus, not a claim that defensibility questions (Thiel's territory) are
  irrelevant.
