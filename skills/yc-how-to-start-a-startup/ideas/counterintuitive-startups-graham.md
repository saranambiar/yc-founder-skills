## Overview

This skill is relevant when someone is deciding whether now is the right time to start a
startup (especially while still in school), chasing fundraising mechanics or "growth hacks"
instead of building something people want, trying to force-generate a startup idea by
brainstorming, doubting their own readiness, or going through the outward motions of a startup
(office, funding, hires) without the substance. Graham's six points are all versions of the
same warning: startup instincts trained by school and normal work actively mislead you here.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I'm in school — should I start a startup now, or wait?"
- "I keep looking for the growth hack or fundraising trick that will make this work"
- "I'm trying to brainstorm startup ideas and everything I come up with feels forced"
- "I don't know if I'm actually cut out for this — how do I tell if I'm ready?"
- "We have the office, the funding, the team — but something feels off about our progress"
- "I like this cofounder candidate a lot personally but I'm not sure that's a good reason"

This is not the right skill for evaluating a specific idea once you have one
(`ideas/idea-and-product-altman.md`), the deeper motivational question of why to start a
company at all (`ideas/why-start-a-startup-moskovitz.md`), or slow/unclear growth once a
company already exists (`growth/unscalable-things-williams-tang.md` — which Graham himself
points to directly in the Q&A).

## Assumptions

1. **The person is at a genuine decision point about starting, or already early and
   questioning their approach** — not deep into a company with real traction, where different
   frameworks apply more directly.
2. **The advice is explicitly generational and personal** — Graham frames this as literally
   what he'd tell his own kids. It's a strong point of view, not a claim that no exceptions exist.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Graham states blunt, sometimes
   provocative claims (don't start a startup in college; "growth hacks" is close to a
   meaningless term) and backs them with specific reasoning, not hedged suggestions.

## Insights

> "Startups are so weird that if you follow your instincts they will lead you astray... You
> can, however, trust your instincts about people. Just pick people the way you would pick
> people if you were picking friends. Work with people you would generally like and respect
> and that you have known long enough to be sure about."
— Paul Graham, CS183B Lecture 3, 2014

Context: Graham's first and organizing point — startup mechanics are unlike anything in prior
experience and mislead instinct, but judgment about a person's character transfers directly
from ordinary life. He notes founders more often err by not trusting a bad feeling about
someone impressive than by trusting a good one.

> "What you need to know to succeed in a startup is not expertise in startups, what you need
> is expertise in your own users. Mark Zuckerberg did not succeed at Facebook because he was
> an expert in startups — he succeeded despite being a complete noob at startups, because he
> understood his users very well."
— Paul Graham, CS183B Lecture 3, 2014

Context: A direct challenge to treating "startup knowledge" (fundraising mechanics, pitch
craft) as the thing to study — Graham argues the actually necessary expertise is about the
specific people the product serves, which can't be taught in a general class.

> "We made up a name for it: 'Playing House.' They come up with some plausible-sounding idea,
> raise funding to get a nice valuation, then rent a nice office and hire a bunch of their
> friends — until they gradually realize how completely fucked they are, because while
> imitating all the outward forms of starting a startup, they neglected the one thing that is
> actually essential: to make something people want."
— Paul Graham, CS183B Lecture 3, 2014

Context: Graham's name for founders who execute the visible rituals of a startup (funding,
office, headcount) without the substance — a trained reflex from years of succeeding at
school and work by performing the form of achievement rather than the substance.

> "In startups, gaming the system stops working. There is no boss to trick — there are only
> users, and all users care about is whether your software does what they want. They're like
> sharks — too stupid to fool. The dangerous thing is that faking does work, to some extent,
> with investors — for one, maybe two rounds. But it's not in your interest to do it. You're
> pulling a confidence trick on yourself, wasting your own time, because the startup is doomed
> regardless."
— Paul Graham, CS183B Lecture 3, 2014

Context: The nuance matters here — Graham isn't claiming investors can never be fooled, only
that succeeding at fooling them doesn't help you, because the underlying company still doesn't
work. The self-deception is the actual danger, not getting caught.

> "Whenever you hear somebody talk about 'growth hacks,' just mentally translate it to
> 'bullshit.' The way to make your startup grow is to make something users really love, and
> then tell them about it. That's Growth Hacks right there."
— Paul Graham, CS183B Lecture 3, 2014

Context: A direct dismissal of tactics-first thinking about growth — consistent with Altman's
and Williams's points elsewhere in this repo that organic growth follows from a genuinely
loved product, not a separable set of tricks.

> "It never gets any easier. The nature of the problems change — you're worrying about
> construction delays in your new offices instead of the broken air conditioner in your studio
> apartment — but the total volume of worry never decreases. If anything, it increases."
— Paul Graham, CS183B Lecture 3, 2014, on founders of big successes he's funded

Context: Directly counters the assumption that success eventually brings relief — Graham's
point, illustrated with Larry Page, is that visible success conceals rather than reduces the
underlying difficulty, because complaining is read as weakness once you've succeeded.

> "Starting a successful startup is similar to having kids — it's like a button you press
> that changes your life irrevocably... Do not start a startup in college. If you start a
> startup, you're not a student anymore — you may be nominally a student, but not for long."
— Paul Graham, CS183B Lecture 3, 2014

Context: Graham's most direct, debated claim — framed explicitly as personal advice ("I'm
talking to my own kids"), not a universal law. His reasoning: startups are all-consuming in a
way incompatible with also being a student, and there are things (aimless exploration, cheap
travel, following unlikely curiosity) available in your twenties that a successful startup
forecloses.

> "There is little to no correlation between [founders' confidence] and how things turn out...
> If you are absolutely terrified of starting a startup, you probably shouldn't do it. Unless
> you're one of those people who gets off on doing things you're afraid of. Otherwise, if
> you're merely unsure whether you can do it, the only way to find out is to try — just not now."
— Paul Graham, CS183B Lecture 3, 2014, after nine years evaluating founders at YC

Context: Graham's claim, from direct experience trying and failing to predict founder success
from confidence level, that self-assessment of readiness doesn't work — with one specific
exception carved out: genuine terror (not mere uncertainty) is a real signal worth listening to.

> "If you make a conscious effort to think of startup ideas, you'll think of ideas that are
> not only bad but bad and plausible-sounding. The way to come up with good ideas is to turn
> your brain into the type that has them unconsciously — so unconsciously you don't even
> realize at first they're startup ideas. Yahoo, Google, Facebook, Apple all started this way,
> as side projects nobody intended to be companies."
— Paul Graham, CS183B Lecture 3, 2014

Context: Graham's method: learn broadly about things that matter, work on problems that
genuinely interest you, with people you like and respect — the third part of which is also how
you find cofounders at the same time as the idea.

> "When a startup is growing but not explosively — that gray-zone question — go read 'Do
> Things That Don't Scale.' I wrote a whole essay answering exactly that question."
— Paul Graham, CS183B Lecture 3, 2014, in the Q&A

Context: Graham's own direct pointer from this lecture to the material behind
`growth/unscalable-things-williams-tang.md` in this repo — worth surfacing as a cross-reference
rather than re-deriving.

> "The advantages of hiring people you know and like are far greater than the small
> disadvantage of having some monoculture. Look at it empirically — at all the most successful
> startups, someone just hires all their pals out of college."
— Paul Graham, CS183B Lecture 3, 2014, in the Q&A

Context: A direct answer to the monoculture concern about hiring for personal fit — Graham
argues the tradeoff favors familiarity and trust, especially very early, though this is in
tension with broader diversity concerns that matter more as a company scales.

## Principles

1. **Trust your instincts about people; don't trust your instincts about startup mechanics.**
   The first is transferable from ordinary life; the second is actively misleading because
   startups are structurally unlike prior experience.
2. **Domain/user expertise matters more than startup expertise.** Knowing your users deeply
   beats knowing the mechanics of fundraising, pitching, or "how startups work" in the abstract.
3. **Watch for "Playing House"** — performing the visible forms of a startup (funding, office,
   headcount) while neglecting the one essential thing: making something people want.
4. **Gaming the system stops working with users, even though it can still work temporarily
   with investors.** Fooling an investor doesn't fix an underlying company that doesn't work —
   it only wastes your own time.
5. **"Growth hacks" as a separate discipline from making something people love is close to
   meaningless.** The real growth strategy is nearly always: build something loved, then tell
   people about it.
6. **Startup difficulty doesn't decrease with success — it changes shape and often
   increases.** Visible success conceals rather than reduces the underlying difficulty.
7. **You cannot reliably self-assess your own startup readiness from prior achievement.**
   Confidence and eventual outcome are essentially uncorrelated — with one exception: genuine
   terror (not mere uncertainty) is a real signal worth listening to.
8. **Good startup ideas come from working on genuinely interesting problems with people you
   respect — not from deliberately brainstorming startup ideas.** Forced brainstorming
   produces plausible-sounding bad ideas that fool you and everyone else.
9. **The best startups often begin as side projects nobody intended to become a company.**
   The idea being an outlier is often exactly why conscious effort wouldn't have found it.
10. **Early hires should function like founders, not employees who need managing.** If someone
    needs real management this early, that's a signal about fit, not just a staffing gap.
11. **Starting a startup in college carries a specific, real opportunity cost** — not because
    it can't work, but because it forecloses other things (open-ended exploration, cheap
    travel, undirected curiosity) that are hard to do later and that Graham argues make for a
    better founder eventually anyway.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **"Don't start a startup in college" is framed by Graham himself as personal advice ("I'm
  talking to my own kids"), not a universal rule — and it sits in real tension with YC's own
  track record of funding very young, in-school founders.** Treat it as a genuine, considered
  opinion worth taking seriously, not as settled fact that overrides someone's specific
  situation — especially since Graham's own exception (a side project that's already taking
  off) is exactly the situation some student founders are actually in.
- **"Gaming the system stops working" can be misheard as "investors can never be fooled."**
  Graham says the opposite explicitly — faking does work with investors for a round or two.
  The point is that succeeding at it doesn't help you, since the underlying company is still
  doomed; it's a warning about self-deception, not a claim about detection.
- **"Trust your instincts about people" is specifically about character and trustworthiness
  signals — not a substitute for real technical vetting or reference checks.** Pair this with
  the more rigorous hiring mechanics in `hiring/team-and-cofounders-altman.md` rather than
  treating a good personal feeling as sufficient evaluation on its own.
- **"Don't consciously brainstorm startup ideas" doesn't mean deliberate market analysis is
  worthless once you have a candidate idea.** Graham's point is about idea *generation*
  specifically — it doesn't contradict the deliberate evaluation frameworks in
  `ideas/idea-and-product-altman.md` or `market-strategy/competition-monopoly-thiel.md` once
  an idea already exists to evaluate.
- **"No correlation between confidence and outcome" has one carved-out exception (genuine
  terror) that's easy to drop when repeating the principle.** Don't let a founder use "Graham
  says you can't tell" to dismiss real, specific fear — that's the one signal he says is
  worth listening to.
- **The monoculture answer ("hire your pals, the advantages outweigh the small disadvantage")
  is given specifically about early hiring, in a Q&A, somewhat off-the-cuff.** It's in real
  tension with broader concerns about diversity and groupthink that matter more as a company
  scales — treat it as a claim about the earliest stage specifically, not a general hiring
  philosophy for a growing company.

## Thinking Framework

**Deciding whether now is the right time (especially in school)**
Ask: "Is this an idea you're already deeply pulled into as a side project, or a decision
you're making abstractly about whether to start something?" If it's already taking over real
time unprompted: that's Graham's own signal a side project is becoming real — worth taking
seriously as an exception. If it's abstract: state his view plainly — the specific cost of
doing it now versus after finishing school, and ask what's actually driving the urgency to
start now rather than build domain expertise first.

---

**Chasing fundraising/growth tactics instead of product**
Ask: "What are you actually spending time on this week — talking to users and building, or
looking for a technique?" If it's tactics-first: name it directly. "That's the trap Graham
describes — gaming the system doesn't work with users, and 'growth hacks' almost always
reduces to making something loved and telling people about it. What would change if you spent
this week's effort on the product instead?"

---

**Forcing startup idea generation**
Ask: "Are you trying to think up startup ideas directly, or working on something that
genuinely interests you and seeing what comes out of it?" If it's forced brainstorming: name
the risk directly — plausible-sounding bad ideas that fool you. Redirect toward: "What's a
problem you'd want to work on even if it never became a company?"

---

**Self-doubt about readiness**
Ask: "Is this genuine terror, or more like uncertainty and not knowing if you can do it?" If
terror: take it seriously — that's the one signal Graham says is real. If it's uncertainty:
say so plainly — "Graham's point from nine years of evaluating founders is that this doesn't
predict outcome either way. The only way to find out is to try — the question is just timing,
not whether you're the type."

---

**Progress feels slow or unclear ("gray zone" growth)**
Don't re-derive this from scratch — point directly to `growth/unscalable-things-williams-tang.md`,
per Graham's own answer in the Q&A referring to "Do Things That Don't Scale." Ask what
specifically they've tried personally and manually before treating this as a strategic puzzle.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If the person hasn't said what they're deciding, ask: "What's the specific question right now
— timing, chasing a tactic instead of substance, generating an idea, or self-doubt about
readiness?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If someone describes spending
most of their time on funding mechanics and pitch decks with little product work, say so
directly: "That's the Playing House pattern — worth checking whether the product substance is
actually there before investing more in the outward form."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't treat "don't start in college" as an absolute rule that overrides someone's specific
  situation**, especially if they already have a side project taking off unprompted.
- **Don't let "gaming the system stops working" get simplified into "investors can't be
  fooled."** The actual claim is narrower and more self-directed: fooling them doesn't help you.
- **Don't accept a good personal feeling about a candidate as sufficient vetting on its own.**
  Pair instinct about character with real evaluation of skill and fit.
- **Don't treat forced brainstorming as a valid way to generate a startup idea.** Redirect
  toward genuinely interesting problems and people, not idea-generation exercises.
- **Don't let someone dismiss real, specific terror about starting as just normal founder
  uncertainty.** That's the one self-assessment signal Graham treats as meaningful.
- **Don't re-explain slow-growth diagnosis from scratch when Graham has already pointed
  directly at the relevant material** (`growth/unscalable-things-williams-tang.md`).
