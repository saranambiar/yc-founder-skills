## Overview

This skill is relevant for someone deciding whether to start a company at all — before there's
a specific idea to evaluate, or alongside one. Moskovitz is explicit that this analysis only
makes sense against a real alternative: joining an existing company that's already working,
not staying in an unrelated career or doing nothing.

**Example situations where this skill is relevant:**
- "I want to start a company so I can be my own boss and set my own schedule"
- "I think I could build this better than the company I currently work for"
- "I'm mainly doing this because I think it's the fastest way to get rich"
- "Everyone around me is starting something and I feel like I should too"
- "I can't stop thinking about this problem even though I have a good job already"
- "I'm scared of the lifestyle cost — is it really as bad as people say?"

This is not the right skill for evaluating a specific idea once the decision to start
something is made — see `ideas/idea-and-product-altman.md`, the other half of this same
lecture. Not for cofounders/hiring or execution — see `hiring/team-and-cofounders-altman.md`
and `operations/execution-altman.md`.

## Assumptions

1. **The person is genuinely deciding whether to start something**, not already committed and
   asking how. If they've clearly already decided, this skill has less to offer than the idea/
   product or execution skills.
2. **The real alternative under consideration is joining an already-working company**, not an
   unrelated career path. Moskovitz's financial and impact comparisons only make sense against
   that specific alternative — he says so directly.
3. **The person wants a direct opinion, not just questions.** Moskovitz speaks from his own
   experience (including his own health/stress costs at early Facebook) and states blunt
   corrections to popular myths. Match that register once you understand their situation.

## Insights

> "The reality of these decisions is pretty nuanced. The people you thought were idiots
> probably weren't idiots. They probably just had a really difficult decision in front of
> them, and people pulling them in multiple directions."
— Dustin Moskovitz, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: On the "I could run this better" myth that draws people toward founding. Moskovitz's
own experience as a CEO: most of his time goes to arbitrating conflicting priorities other
people create, not executing some superior personal vision.

> "Founder depression is like a very real thing... if you start a company, it's gonna be
> extremely hard."
— Dustin Moskovitz, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014, referencing an Economist piece on founder stress

Context: Named directly, not softened — paired later with his own account of physical health
costs (recurring back injuries, chronic anxiety) during early Facebook.

> "Ben Horowitz likes to say the number one rule of a CEO is managing your own psychology.
> It's absolutely true, make sure you do it."
— Dustin Moskovitz, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: Framed as a core, unavoidable job requirement — not a nice-to-have or a sign of
weakness if it's hard.

> "If you take your foot off the gas, so will [your team]... you're always working anyway. If
> you're really passionate about an idea, it's just gonna pull you to keep working on it."
— Dustin Moskovitz, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: On the "flexibility/being the boss" myth. The team calibrates its effort to the
founder's — flexibility in practice tends to mean less freedom, not more, once past 2-3 people.

> "If you joined Facebook a couple years into its existence, you made $200 million [by
> employee #100]. Even employee #1,000, joining around 2009, still made $20 million... a
> theoretical $100M startup, your share after dilution is likely around 10% — about $10
> million. You should have a lot more confidence than Facebook in 2009 or Dropbox in 2014."
— Dustin Moskovitz, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: The concrete math behind "you'll make more money founding your own company" — using
two of the most extreme outlier outcomes in tech (Facebook, Dropbox) as the comparison, to
show the bar for founding actually beating joining is very high.

> "Bret Taylor came into Google as employee number 1,500 and invented Google Maps... my
> co-founder Justin Rosenstein prototyped chat in the browser at Google, then built the Like
> button at Facebook as employee #250. Neither needed to start a company to have that impact
> — and the Like button almost certainly would have failed if he'd tried it as a startup,
> because he needed Facebook's distribution to make it work."
— Dustin Moskovitz, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: Direct evidence against "founding is the only way to have real impact" — some ideas
need an existing platform's distribution to work at all.

> "You can't not do it — in two ways. One, you're so passionate about it that you're just
> going to do it anyway... Two, the world needs you to do it. If it's not something the world
> needs, go do something the world needs. Your time's really valuable."
— Dustin Moskovitz, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: Moskovitz's actual answer to "why start a startup," after walking through the myths.
Both halves are required — personal compulsion alone isn't enough if you're not well-suited to
the specific problem; being well-suited alone isn't enough without the compulsion to survive
the grind.

> "We were pretty convinced nobody else was going to build [Asana]. The problem had been
> around a long time and we just kept seeing incremental solutions to it... the idea was like
> beating itself out of our chest, forcing itself into the world."
— Dustin Moskovitz, CS183B Lecture 1, 2014

Context: The Asana origin story as the concrete illustration of "can't not do it" — built
nights and weekends while still at Facebook, before the decision to actually found the company.

## Principles

1. **Separate your actual reason from the romanticized myths.** Glamour, being the boss,
   flexibility, and money are all real draws — and each one falls apart under direct scrutiny.
2. **"I could run this better" usually reflects incomplete information, not superior judgment.**
   The incumbents' decisions typically look worse from outside than they are from inside a
   genuinely difficult tradeoff.
3. **Flexibility as a founder is largely illusory past 2-3 people.** Your team calibrates its
   effort to yours — taking your foot off the gas signals permission to everyone else to do
   the same.
4. **The money math rarely favors founding unless you have real conviction in a very large
   outcome.** Compare against actual numbers for joining a company already working — not vague
   assumptions about upside.
5. **Outsized impact doesn't require founding a company.** Some of the most-used products in
   the world (Google Maps, Facebook's chat feature, the Like button) were built by early
   employees, not founders — and some genuinely needed an existing platform's distribution.
6. **Managing your own psychology is a core, unavoidable job of being a founder.** Not
   optional, not a sign of weakness when it's hard — budget for it directly.
7. **Real commitment is closer to 5-10 years than the 2-3 years many first-time founders
   assume.** Underestimating this is one of the most common founder mistakes Moskovitz names.
8. **The actual test is both halves at once: you can't not do it, and the world needs you
   specifically to do it.** Personal compulsion without fit is a bad bet; fit without
   compulsion won't survive the grind.

## Where This Can Mislead You

- **The financial comparison (Facebook/Dropbox employee outcomes vs. theoretical founder
  equity) uses two of the most extreme outlier company outcomes in tech history as the
  baseline.** It's illustrative of the math and the bar required, not a realistic expectation
  for either path. Don't let a founder anchor on "$200M as employee #100" as a typical outcome
  of joining a company, any more than they should anchor on outlier founder outcomes.
- **"You should have more confidence than Facebook in 2009 or Dropbox in 2014" is a
  deliberately high, rhetorical bar, not a literal, calculable threshold.** It's useful for
  stress-testing real conviction — it isn't a checklist item to satisfy precisely before
  proceeding.
- **The stress and health material describes Moskovitz's own specific experience** (recurring
  back injuries, chronic anxiety in his early twenties at Facebook) — real and worth taking
  seriously, but anecdotal, not a diagnostic checklist for who is or isn't "founder material."
- **"The world needs you to do it" can be misapplied as license for grandiosity.**
  Moskovitz's own version of this test was comparatively modest — he and his cofounder saw a
  real, specific problem at Facebook and were convinced no one else was solving it well, not a
  claim of world-saving importance. Check for that same specificity before accepting the claim.
- **This framework assumes the real alternative is joining an already-working company.**
  If someone's actual alternative is something else entirely (an unrelated career, more
  school), the financial and impact comparisons don't transfer directly — say so rather than
  forcing the comparison.

## Thinking Framework

**Drawn to starting a company mainly for autonomy or flexibility**
Ask: "What specifically about your current situation makes you want that control?" Often the
real frustration is a decision-making constraint, not autonomy in general. Then name the myth
directly: "Founder flexibility is mostly illusory past a couple of people — your team
calibrates its effort to yours. Does that change how appealing this is?"

---

**Drawn to starting a company mainly for money**
Ask: "What's the realistic ceiling on this specific idea, and how does that compare to joining
something with real distribution already?" Run the comparison concretely rather than
abstractly. Then ask: "Would you still want to do this if the expected payout were identical
either way?" Their answer tells you whether the real driver is money or something else.

---

**Uncertain whether their motivation is durable**
Ask both halves of Moskovitz's test directly, one at a time: "Can you not do this — are you
going to keep working on it whether or not you formally start a company?" Then: "Is there a
real, specific reason you're the right person for this, not just anyone who could build it?"
A strong yes to only one is a warning sign, not a pass.

---

**Worried about the lifestyle/stress cost**
Don't minimize it. Ask: "What specifically are you worried about — the stress itself, or not
being able to just walk away if it's not working?" Name the actual mechanism Moskovitz
describes (you're accountable for your team's opportunity cost, and leaving is a real black
mark, unlike leaving a job) rather than offering generic reassurance.

## Action Prompts

**Step 0: Get the specific situation**
If they haven't said what's driving the pull toward starting something, ask: "What's actually
drawing you to this — is it the idea itself, wanting more control, the potential upside, or
something else?"

**Step 1: Match to the right thread above** and go there directly.

**Step 2: State a plain view once you have enough context.** If someone describes wanting to
start a company mainly to escape a bad manager, say so directly: "That's a decision-making
constraint, not a case for founding — worth separating those before this goes further."

## What Not To Do

- **Don't treat "I could do it better" as self-evidently true.** Push for what specifically the
  incumbent got wrong, and whether the full context was actually visible from outside.
- **Don't validate the flexibility/autonomy draw without naming what it actually looks like
  post-2-3 people.** It's a real myth Moskovitz explicitly corrects.
- **Don't let a founder run the money comparison against vague assumptions.** Push for real
  numbers on both sides — the realistic startup outcome and the realistic alternative.
- **Don't accept "the world needs this" without the same specificity Moskovitz's own example
  had.** A vague world-saving claim isn't the same as a concrete, well-evidenced gap nobody
  else is filling.
- **Don't minimize the stress/commitment cost to make someone feel better about proceeding.**
  Name it accurately — it's core to whether the decision is actually right for them.
- **Don't apply this framework to a decision where the real alternative isn't joining a
  working company.** Say so and adapt rather than forcing the comparison to fit.
